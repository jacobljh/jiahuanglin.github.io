Learning latent dynamics

A compressed state becomes a world model only when it can advance under an action. We will build that mechanism one dependency at a time, then derive why an RSSM needs both an imagination prior and an observation-informed posterior.

1 · Start from the contract, not the architecture

At time t, an agent has observations o≤t, past actions a<t, and a candidate next action at. A useful dynamics model must answer a conditional question:

p(st+1 | o≤t, a≤t)

The physical state s is hidden, so we replace the entire history with an internal state. The desired compression says that once the internal state is known, old observations add no decision-relevant predictive information:

p(ot+1, rt, ct | o≤t, a≤t) ≈ p(ot+1, rt, ct | xt, at)

Here xt is learned state, rt is reward or cost, and ct is continuation. This is the first-principles contract: compress history, apply action, predict consequences. An RNN, transformer, token model, or diffusion model is only an implementation of that contract.

The deterministic baseline is xt+1 = fθ(xt, at). It is cheap and differentiable. But the same visible situation can lead to different futures because of hidden intent, unresolved geometry, sensor noise, or genuinely random events. One vector output must then average alternatives or hide them in an unstable recurrent code. We therefore make the transition probabilistic.

2 · Factor state into memory and a local random variable

A recurrent state-space model, or RSSM, uses two complementary state variables:

  • Deterministic memory ht. A recurrent update carries ordered historical context: which objects were seen, how they moved, and which actions were applied. Once its inputs are fixed, this path is deterministic.
  • Stochastic state zt. A sampled variable represents information that is uncertain or newly revealed at this step. Different samples describe different plausible local worlds.

The update is deliberately ordered. First advance memory using the previous stochastic state and previous action:

ht = fθ(ht−1, zt−1, at−1)

Term by term: ht−1 is accumulated history; zt−1 says which plausible world was active; at−1 is the intervention that pushed that world forward; and fθ may be a GRU, state-space layer, or causal transformer block. The result ht exists before seeing ot, so it is a prediction from the past rather than a summary that leaks the current frame.

Next create two distributions over the same stochastic variable:

prior: pθ(zt | ht)     posterior: qϕ(zt | ht, eϕ(ot))

The prior predicts what state should come next using only past memory. It is available when the model imagines a future and no new camera frame exists. The posterior also receives an encoded real observation. It corrects the predicted belief using evidence and is available while filtering a recorded or live trajectory. “Posterior” here does not mean an exact Bayesian posterior; it is a learned approximation trained to infer the latent that best explains the current evidence.

The two routes must share the same latent semantics. A sample from the posterior should be decodable and useful for task heads; a sample from the prior should live in that same state space. Otherwise training can reconstruct observed frames beautifully while imagination immediately enters alien states.

3 · Derive the learning objective term by term

Because latent states are not labeled, we train them through predicted evidence. We will use one transition convention throughout the series: belief xt chooses action at; that action produces reward or cost rt, continuation ct, and next observation ot+1; the model represents those consequences in destination state xt+1=(ht+1,zt+1). Observation, reward, and continuation heads therefore factor as:

p(ot+1, rt, ct | ht+1, zt+1) = p(ot+1|ht+1,zt+1) · p(rt|ht+1,zt+1) · p(ct|ht+1,zt+1)

For one transition, a common loss is the negative evidence lower bound plus task weights:

Lt+1 = −Ezt+1∼q[log pθ(ot+1|ht+1,zt+1) + λr log pθ(rt|ht+1,zt+1) + λc log pθ(ct|ht+1,zt+1)] + β KL(qϕ || pθ)

Read it left to right. The expectation samples the destination latent from the observation-informed posterior. The log-likelihood terms ask that sample to explain the next visible evidence and the consequences of the action that just occurred. Their negative values are reconstruction or prediction costs. The weights λ establish which facts deserve capacity. The final KL divergence compares the complete posterior distribution with the imagination prior. Some codebases shift reward indices by one; that is harmless only when the data loader, transition, heads, and return calculation all use the same convention.

The KL is not merely “a regularizer that makes latents smooth.” Its operational purpose is exact: teach the prior, which must act without future observations, to predict the latent distribution that the posterior would infer after seeing those observations. In density form,

KL(q || p) = Ez∼q[log q(z|h,o) − log p(z|h)] ≥ 0

If the posterior puts probability on states that the prior thinks impossible, the ratio q/p is large and the penalty rises. Minimizing it closes the inference–imagination gap. Yet the two networks receive competing incentives. An excessively strong KL can force q≈p before the latent learns anything from the frame; the decoder then relies on deterministic memory and the stochastic state collapses. An excessively weak KL lets the posterior encode arbitrary frame details that the prior cannot forecast; reconstruction improves while imagined rollouts fail.

Implementations manage this tension with a KL weight β, “free nats” that do not penalize a small information budget, or split gradients: one term updates the prior toward a stopped-gradient posterior and another regularizes the posterior toward a stopped-gradient prior. These are optimization controls, not changes to the underlying contract.

4 · Worked numerical example: what the KL is measuring

Imagine a one-dimensional latent encoding a vehicle’s lateral location. Before the next camera frame, the transition prior predicts p(zt+1|ht+1) = N(2.0, 0.5²). After seeing lane markings in ot+1, the posterior infers q(zt+1|ht+1,ot+1) = N(2.4, 0.4²). For univariate Gaussians,

KL(q||p) = log(σp/σq) + [σq² + (μq−μp)²] / (2σp²) − 1/2

Substitute the values: log(0.5/0.4)=0.223; the posterior variance is 0.16; the squared mean surprise is also (2.4−2.0)²=0.16; and 2σp²=0.50. Therefore KL=0.223+(0.32/0.50)−0.5=0.363 nats.

The number combines two disagreements. The mean term says the evidence shifted the vehicle by 0.4 latent units relative to prediction. The variance terms say the observation made location more certain. During learning, the prior network is pushed toward a mean near 2.4 and a narrower uncertainty when past context makes that correction predictable. The posterior is simultaneously discouraged from encoding details that cannot be anticipated from history. If the frame contains a genuinely new event—a suddenly visible pedestrian—the posterior should still move; forcing zero KL would erase the surprise rather than model it.

After obtaining zt+1, a decoder predicts ot+1, while reward and continuation heads predict the cost and termination caused by at. A latent coordinate need not literally equal meters. It only needs to support these predictions and remain reachable by both prior and posterior.

5 · What is—and is not—identified by the objective

It is tempting to read individual latent coordinates as physical variables, but the loss does not require that interpretation. If an invertible transformation g remaps every latent and the transition and decoders learn the corresponding inverse, observable predictions can remain unchanged. A rotated latent basis may model the same world as an axis-aligned one. The identifiable object is therefore closer to a predictive equivalence class: states are equivalent when they imply the same future consequences under every relevant action.

This matters when deciding whether “disentanglement” is a requirement. If a planner only needs accurate reward and constraint queries, an entangled but stable state may be sufficient. If engineers need to edit object pose, transfer dynamics across embodiments, or inspect failures, structure becomes a product requirement. Object slots, coordinate frames, equivariant layers, bottlenecks, or supervised probes inject the missing inductive bias. The variational objective alone will not magically name “position,” “velocity,” and “intent.”

The Markov assumption also lives in learned state, not necessarily in a single image. We want

p(zt+1 | z≤t, a≤t) ≈ p(zt+1 | ht+1)

The recurrent memory is the mechanism that makes this approximation plausible. If velocity requires three frames but training resets memory after two, no transition head can recover the missing information. Residual dependence on long history is diagnosed by giving a probe extra past context: if it substantially improves next-event prediction beyond (h,z), the state is not sufficient for that query.

Continuous Gaussian and discrete categorical stochastic states express different local geometries. A diagonal Gaussian represents nearby alternatives as smooth coordinate changes and supports the reparameterization z=μ+σ⊙ε. A categorical latent with probabilities π1:K represents alternatives as separated symbols; its KL is

KL(q||p) = Σk=1K qk[log qk − log pk]

Discrete states can preserve contact modes or object identities without averaging them, but quantization loses within-mode detail and gradient estimators can be biased. Hybrid RSSMs often use several small categorical variables: their combinations provide many states while keeping each softmax manageable. The correct choice follows the topology of uncertainty, not a claim that one latent family is universally more expressive.

RSSMs compress time into recurrent memory and predict a small continuous or categorical stochastic state. Their per-step cost is low, making millions of imagined control steps practical. Recurrence can become an information bottleneck, and a simple Gaussian latent may underrepresent separated futures.

Autoregressive token models quantize images, objects, or latent patches, then factor a future as a sequence of categorical predictions. Cross-entropy is stable and expressive, and transformers reuse powerful sequence machinery. However, token order is a modeling choice, errors can compound within each frame as well as across time, and fine quantization increases context length.

Diffusion or flow transitions start from noise and iteratively form a next latent or trajectory. They can represent complex multimodal densities without a fixed mixture count. The cost is multiple network evaluations per prediction, which can be unacceptable inside a planner that evaluates thousands of action sequences.

Predictive embedding models predict a target encoder’s representation instead of reconstructing pixels. They avoid spending capacity on texture and may emphasize stable semantics. Their central danger is collapse: if both context and target encoders emit a constant, prediction is easy but useless. Stop-gradient targets, variance constraints, contrastive terms, or task heads must keep the representation informative.

These families are not ranked on one universal axis. Choose the predicted unit from the downstream query. A control agent may prefer a compact RSSM with reward and continuation heads. A video editor may pay diffusion compute for perceptual detail. A search agent may want discrete tokens for branching and caching. The architecture is correct only relative to latency, horizon, uncertainty, and output fidelity requirements.

7 · Heads define what the state is allowed to forget

Suppose the physical scene contains a red emergency switch, a moving crate, and an approaching wall. The latent has limited capacity, so it cannot preserve every photon. A naive design says, “First learn a general representation; later attach whatever prediction heads we need.” That separation is false during training. Every head sends gradients backward into the latent, so every head votes on what the latent must remember. The representation is not learned first and queried second; it is shaped by the queries while it is learned.

Follow the consequences one head at a time.

  1. Begin with an observation head. Ask the state to predict pixels, depth, audio, or proprioception. Because these targets are dense, almost every time step supplies a learning signal. To reduce that loss, the latent preserves sensor appearance and geometry: wall edges, surface color, sound texture, and joint configuration. The failed naive idea is to equate this dense supervision with relevance. Unpredictable wallpaper texture can consume capacity while the tiny emergency switch contributes few pixels. The derived requirement is therefore ground the state in observations, but do not let observation fidelity be the only definition of usefulness. In implementation, keep the observation head, reduce its weight or predict stable features when nuisance detail dominates, and diagnose the result by changing texture while holding task geometry fixed.
  2. Add a reward or cost head. Now ask for a task scalar or vector. Its gradient preserves variables whose changes alter consequences, such as distance to the wall or whether the switch is active. This is efficient because the latent is pushed directly toward decision-relevant distinctions. But rewards are often sparse: two physically different situations may both produce zero reward until one becomes dangerous. The requirement is to combine task pressure with a denser account of the world. In implementation, pair reward prediction with observation, object, or dynamics targets. Diagnose reward-only compression by changing geometry in situations where immediate reward stays constant and checking whether the latent notices before the delayed consequence arrives.
  3. Add a continuation head. A return calculation must know whether a trajectory survives or enters an absorbing terminal state. Predicting survival or termination probability forces the latent to retain horizon-changing events such as failure, success, or episode truncation. Omitting this head makes an imagined trajectory continue collecting value after the real episode would have ended. The complication is that continuation labels may depend on the behavior policy or data-collection rules rather than physics alone. The implementation must distinguish true terminal events from administrative time limits, and the diagnostic is simple: inspect predicted returns around every terminal category and verify that mass does not leak past absorbing states.
  4. Add geometry or object heads when spatial persistence matters. Predicting occupancy, pose, tracks, or contact forces the state to preserve objects and their relationships across time. This makes the latent easier to inspect and supports planners that need collision or manipulation queries. The naive alternative—hoping pixel reconstruction spontaneously invents stable object identity—often fails under occlusion or appearance change. The derived requirement is explicit pressure for persistent structure. The implementation consequence is extra labels or reliable self-supervision, and the diagnostic is object permanence: hide an object briefly, reveal it again, and test whether identity, pose, and contact relations remain consistent.
  5. Add value or policy heads only with a clear transfer contract. Predicting return or an action distribution forces the state to retain controllability and value-equivalent distinctions. It can make planning extremely efficient for the current task. But if the goal later changes, two states that once had the same optimal action may need to be distinguished. A value-shaped latent may already have discarded that difference. The requirement is to decide whether the model is a narrow task controller or a reusable model of the environment. In implementation, retain broader predictive heads when goals may change. Diagnose overspecialization by swapping rewards or downstream tasks while freezing the world model and measuring which distinctions can no longer be recovered.
The causal chain
A target is chosen → its loss creates a gradient → the gradient rewards particular information in the latent → limited capacity suppresses competing information → the resulting state becomes good for some future queries and blind to others. Head selection is therefore part of state design, not a reporting layer added afterward.

A robust system usually combines dense grounding with task heads, then monitors whether one loss dominates the shared encoder. Pixel reconstruction may retain wallpaper color and ignore a tiny reward-critical switch; reward-only training may ignore geometry until it matters and then generalize poorly. The diagnostic is not merely “did the total loss fall?” It is “which counterfactual changes move the latent, and are those exactly the changes that alter future consequences?”

8 · A linear training procedure

  1. Collect sequences, not shuffled frames. Store observations, applied actions, rewards or costs, terminal flags, and timestamps. Dynamics cannot be identified if temporal alignment is destroyed.
  2. Encode each observation. Produce et=Eϕ(ot). Normalize modalities and preserve action timing.
  3. Initialize memory. Use zeros, a learned initial state, or an encoder over a context window. Reset it at true episode boundaries.
  4. Advance the deterministic path. Compute ht=f(ht−1,zt−1,at−1). This ordering prevents the current observation from leaking into the prior.
  5. Parameterize both distributions. The prior head reads ht; the posterior head reads ht,et. Output means and scales, or categorical logits. Constrain scales away from zero for numerical stability.
  6. Sample differentiably. For a Gaussian, use z=μ+σ⊙ε, ε∼N(0,I). For discrete latents, use a straight-through estimator or relaxed sample if gradients require it.
  7. Predict evidence and consequences. Decode observations and task targets from (ht,zt). Use likelihoods appropriate to each variable rather than treating every output as unit-variance MSE.
  8. Optimize reconstruction, task, and KL terms. Log each unweighted and weighted component. A good total loss can conceal a collapsed latent or a neglected reward head.
  9. Validate the prior separately. Infer a start state with the posterior, remove future observations, and roll only the prior under held-out actions. Compare predicted events, rewards, geometry, and uncertainty with the real continuation.

Short sequence burn-in can establish memory before scoring losses. Truncated backpropagation controls memory use, but truncation shorter than the required dependency prevents the model from learning it. Batch construction, reset masks, and action–observation alignment are as consequential as the neural block.

9 · Failure modes become diagnosable when routes are separated

The two routes from Section 2 give us a debugging instrument. Instead of staring at one total validation loss, move through three increasingly demanding tests: posterior filtering, where every real observation can correct belief; one-step prior prediction, where dynamics must cross one gap without that correction; and free-running prior rollout, where the model repeatedly consumes its own states. The first stage that breaks identifies the missing mechanism.

  1. Sharp reconstruction, useless imagination. The physical symptom is that recorded frames reconstruct cleanly, yet an imagined future becomes meaningless as soon as observations are removed. The naive conclusion—“the decoder works, so the world model works”—confuses posterior inference with prior prediction. The likely mechanism is that the posterior carries frame information the prior cannot predict. Confirm it by measuring the gap between posterior-conditioned and prior-only metrics from the same start state. Then gradually strengthen dynamics and KL alignment and add multi-step prior losses. The diagnostic should improve first at one step and then across the rollout; if only reconstruction changes, the actual failure remains.
  2. The latent ignores the observation. Here filtering and imagination look suspiciously identical even when the frame contains new evidence. An overly strong KL or powerful deterministic path has collapsed the posterior toward the prior. Look for KL near zero and mask or replace the current frame: if the inferred state barely changes, evidence is not entering. Restore an information channel with KL warm-up, free nats, a weaker decoder, or better gradient balance. Verify recovery with controlled observations that reveal previously hidden state; the posterior should move exactly when the evidence warrants it.
  3. Prior variance explodes. The model becomes uncertain everywhere as horizon grows, even in routine regimes. Calling this “honest uncertainty” is too charitable: an underfit transition or unstable scale parameterization can use variance to hide systematic error. Plot predicted scale and entropy by horizon and regime, then compare them with actual residuals. Bound log-scales, supply missing state or action inputs, and add data where error is high. A successful repair makes uncertainty track difficulty rather than rise indiscriminately.
  4. The latent memorizes texture. Background changes move state more than reward-critical geometry because dense reconstruction overwhelms the task signal. Perform a counterfactual nuisance test: change wallpaper, lighting, or texture while preserving objects and dynamics, then inspect latent and task outputs. If they move substantially, predict stable features or objects, reweight task heads, and augment nuisance factors. Re-run the same intervention; invariance should improve without erasing real geometric changes.
  5. State jumps or averages around contact. Before contact, a smooth transition may work; at impact, the world branches into sticking, sliding, bouncing, or separating modes. One smooth latent transition averages these discrete regimes and creates a state that follows none of them. Stratify errors immediately before and after contact or other mode switches. Add stochastic or discrete mode variables, or a structured contact head, and verify that each sampled branch remains physically coherent rather than merely increasing pixel diversity.
  6. Offline loss is excellent, but control is poor. The dataset may cover only the behavior policy’s actions, or the latent may omit variables that matter only under intervention. Passive next-frame accuracy cannot prove action sensitivity. Evaluate counterfactual and planner-selected actions, not just recorded actions. If error rises off policy, collect exploratory data and add action-sensitive auxiliary predictions. The final diagnostic is closed-loop: changing an action in a fixed start belief should change predicted consequences in the same direction as the real system.

The order matters. First ask whether evidence can be encoded, then whether one transition can predict it, then whether that transition survives self-composition, and only then ask whether a planner can use it under new actions. Also visualize per-dimension KL rather than only its sum: a handful of active dimensions can hide widespread collapse. This sequence turns an ambiguous “bad world model” into a localized inference, dynamics, stability, or coverage failure.

10 · System-design implications

Make action semantics explicit. The transition must know whether at is a requested command, an actuator setpoint, or a measured executed action. In a robot with delay, the command issued at t may affect state only at t+2; in a vehicle, a low-level controller may transform steering requests before they reach the wheels. If the dataset stores only requested actions, actuator state and delay belong in the latent or the transition will interpret execution variation as randomness. A useful alignment test shifts the action stream forward and backward and checks which offset best predicts the next posterior.

Define the imagination interface. A planner should be able to copy a latent state, batch candidate actions, sample transition branches, and query reward, continuation, constraints, and uncertainty without decoding full-resolution pixels. Rendering should be optional because it often dominates latency.

Keep inference and simulation paths consistent. The live system filters observations through the posterior, while the planner forks from that state and uses the prior. Version the encoder, transition, and heads together; a latent has no stable meaning across independently changed components.

Budget uncertainty deliberately. Multiple stochastic samples improve coverage but multiply planning cost. Choose sample count from decision sensitivity, reuse common random numbers when comparing actions, and preserve rare safety outcomes rather than pruning only by average probability.

Instrument reset and timing semantics. A one-frame action offset can look like irreducible uncertainty. Episode boundaries, dropped frames, actuator delay, and sensor latency belong in the data model. If they are hidden, the RSSM must waste stochastic capacity explaining pipeline errors.

Match state capacity to queries. More latent dimensions can lower reconstruction loss without improving control. Select capacity using held-out prior rollouts and downstream decisions, not compression ratio alone. If goals may change, retain general predictive heads rather than shaping the entire state around one reward.

Separate model state from application state. A latent checkpoint is an internal implementation detail, not a durable database schema. Persist raw observations, actions, timestamps, and task outcomes so beliefs can be recomputed after retraining. When a live service must hand state between machines, version the entire model bundle and reject mixed versions rather than assuming two same-sized vectors share meaning.

11 · Bridge: why a transition distribution is only the beginning

We now have an operational loop: recurrent memory advances under action; a prior proposes the next latent without evidence; a posterior corrects it with evidence; prediction heads ground the latent; and KL aligns inference with imagination. But writing p(zt+1|ht+1) does not guarantee that this distribution represents the right kind of uncertainty. A Gaussian may still place mass between two physically separate futures, and diverse samples may carry meaningless or miscalibrated probabilities.

Lesson 05 therefore asks the next necessary question: when several futures are valid, how should modes, ignorance, and probabilities be represented and tested? We move from having a stochastic transition to making its uncertainty decision-worthy.

Takeaway
Latent dynamics is an alternating inference–imagination system. Deterministic memory carries history; stochastic state represents uncertain local worlds; the posterior uses new evidence; the prior is the only route available during imagination; and the KL trains those routes to share a usable latent language. Architecture, heads, and loss weights should be chosen from the consequences the system must predict—not from reconstruction quality alone.

Interview prompts

  • Why does an RSSM contain both ht and zt? h deterministically compresses ordered history; z represents locally uncertain or newly revealed state, allowing multiple plausible futures.
  • What is the difference between the prior and posterior? The prior predicts latent state from past memory and is available during imagination. The posterior also sees the current observation and corrects that prediction during filtering and training.
  • What does KL(q||p) do operationally? It penalizes posterior states that the imagination prior assigns low probability, teaching the prior to anticipate observation-informed inference.
  • What happens when the KL weight is too high or too low? Too high can collapse the posterior so it ignores evidence; too low lets the posterior create states the prior cannot reach, producing good reconstruction but bad rollouts.
  • Why must the current observation not enter the prior? A planner will not possess future observations. Letting them leak into the prior creates a training path unavailable at deployment.
  • How would you detect a weak transition hidden by a strong decoder? Compare posterior-conditioned prediction with prior-only one-step and open-loop rollouts; the gap exposes dependence on correction.
  • When would tokens be preferable to a continuous RSSM? When discrete branching, transformer scaling, or stable categorical likelihoods matter more than compact, very fast imagined control steps.
  • How do auxiliary heads alter the state? Their gradients decide which information survives compression; reward heads preserve consequences, geometry heads preserve spatial structure, and pixel heads preserve appearance.
