Training and scaling systems

The unit of data is a time-aligned trajectory, not a shuffled frame. Resolution, frame rate, context, horizon, views, candidates, refinements, and uncertainty samples multiply into the real training and serving bill.

The systems turn
Previous lessons assumed clean synchronized trajectories and affordable imagination. We now follow Countertop Assistant from raw sensor packets through alignment, tokenization, sampling, distributed training, rollout search, and a stateful service with a hard control deadline.

1 · Specify the trajectory contract before the architecture

A static image example can often be described by (x,y). A world-model example is an interval of physical history. At minimum it needs timestamped observations for every sensor, commanded and executed actions, episode boundaries, reward or progress, safety costs, validity masks, calibration, and environment/embodiment metadata. That record is the evidence from which action-conditioned dynamics are identified. If the record is wrong, a sophisticated model learns a sophisticated version of the wrong causal process.

Continue the running product from Lesson 14. Countertop Assistant must put the red ceramic mug on a tray within 20 seconds. A raw episode contains two RGB cameras, depth, 200 Hz joints, 1 kHz tactile values, 50 Hz commanded actions, controller-reported executed actions, the language instruction, object/task annotations when available, force-limit events, and outcome. The dataset contract should state, for every field:

  • Semantics: physical quantity, unit, coordinate frame, sign convention, and whether an action is target position, velocity, torque, or a controller parameter.
  • Time support: instant, exposure interval, aggregated window, command issue time, execution start, and known delay distribution.
  • Validity: dropped packet, clipped force, occlusion, stale calibration, teleoperation takeover, and reset/cut markers.
  • Provenance: robot revision, firmware, sensor serial/calibration, collection policy, operator, environment, and model version that requested the data.
  • Outcome: success predicate, progress, collision, spill, excessive force, human intervention, timeout, and recovery—not only a scalar reward.

Store raw immutable packets and create derived aligned views through versioned transformations. A later discovery about a 22 ms actuator delay should cause a new dataset version, not an irreversible relabeling. Split train and evaluation by environment, object instance, time period, and collection policy where leakage matters. Random clip splitting can place adjacent moments of one episode in both sets and produce an illusion of generalization.

2 · Build the data factory around causality

The ingestion path should be linear and auditable:

raw packets → clock correction → calibration/frame transforms → action execution alignment → episode segmentation → validity masks → derived state/labels → chunk index → immutable manifest → train/eval sampling views

Clock correction maps device clocks to a monotonic physical timeline and retains uncertainty. Calibration converts points and poses into named coordinate frames. Action alignment distinguishes what was requested from what the controller actually applied. Segmentation prevents prediction across resets, video cuts, teleports, or controller takeover. Validity masks let the learner know a sensor is missing rather than interpreting zeros as measurements. The final manifest should make every training sequence reproducible from content hashes and transformation versions.

Automated tests are unusually valuable here. Reproject depth into RGB and track pixel error. Integrate joint velocity and compare with encoder displacement. Check that force changes follow, not precede, contact actions. Verify conservation-like quantities and workspace bounds. Plot timestamp gaps, action saturation, camera exposure, missingness, and outcome distribution by robot revision. Watch a stratified sample of synchronized episodes; humans notice reversed events and coordinate mistakes that summary statistics miss.

Countertop Assistant has an additional issue: demonstrations contain policy bias. Human operators approach visible, easy mugs and abort dangerous grasps. Passive data therefore does not identify what happens under unsupported actions. The collection plan needs safe interventions—small approach perturbations, varied grip forces, deliberate viewpoint changes—and randomized simulator data. Record the behavior-policy probability where possible so evaluation and off-policy correction can distinguish world frequency from collector preference.

3 · Define the training example without leaking the future

Choose a context interval [τ−C, τ], an executed action sequence beginning at τ, and prediction targets through τ+H. An encoder infers belief zτ from past evidence. Dynamics predicts future latent state conditioned on actions. Decoders or heads predict observations, task variables, rewards, costs, and uncertainty. A common objective is:

L = λobs Lobs + λdyn Llatent + λrew Lreward + λcost Lsafety + λstate Ltask-state + λKL Lregularization + λcal Lcalibration

The coefficients express product priorities and gradient scale; they should not be guessed once and forgotten. Normalize by valid target count, log per-head gradient norms, and measure whether a visually large reconstruction term suppresses rare collision learning. If observation prediction can be solved through texture without state dynamics, use action-effect, inverse-dynamics, temporal consistency, object permanence, and task-state objectives. If predicting all pixels wastes capacity, decode pixels on a subset while training structured heads every step.

Loss frequency matters as much as coefficient. A collision head evaluated on one rare label per batch receives noisy gradients even with a large weight. Construct event-rich microbatches, accumulate rare-head gradients, or use a separate replay stream, while retaining ordinary examples to control false alarms. Monitor Pareto curves: lowering reconstruction loss may worsen contact calibration, and maximizing reward prediction may discard geometry needed for a new goal. A shared representation is justified only when transfer outweighs this interference; otherwise keep specialist heads or capacity routing behind a common belief.

A training sample must mimic deployment causality. Bidirectional attention inside the past is fine if all past packets are available, but future frames, future normalization statistics, completed-episode labels, and smoothed poses using future evidence cannot enter the online state. Train with realistic sensor dropout and delay. When using teacher forcing, gradually test free rollouts; a model trained only on true states never experiences its own prediction errors.

4 · Count tokens from the physical sampling plan

Let view v have resolution Hᵥ×Wᵥ, spatial patch pᵥ, frame rate fᵥ, and context length C. With one token per patch, visual tokens are approximately:

Nvisual = Σᵥ ceil(Hᵥ/pᵥ) ceil(Wᵥ/pᵥ) ceil(fᵥ C) Ntotal = Nvisual + Ndepth + Nproprio + Ntactile + Naction + Nlanguage + Nspecial

This is only the input count. Autoregressive future tokens add horizon. Dense self-attention materializes interactions proportional to N² per layer, while MLP and projection compute scale roughly linearly in tokens but quadratically in width. Space–time factorization, latent bottlenecks, recurrent state, sparse memory, and multirate tokenization change these terms; they are architectural responses to a measurement plan.

Worked trajectory and storage bill

Assume an 8-second context. Two 256×256 RGB views at 12 Hz, patch size 16, produce 2 × 96 × 16 × 16 = 49,152 raw visual tokens. A depth stream compressed to 64 tokens per frame adds 96 × 64 = 6,144. Instead of tokenizing all 200 Hz joint samples independently, a multirate encoder summarizes ten samples into 16 tokens at 20 Hz, adding 8 × 20 × 16 = 2,560. Tactile windows become 32 tokens at 20 Hz, adding 5,120. Actions add 8 tokens per 20 Hz step, or 1,280. Language and special state use 256. Total context is 64,512 tokens.

Dense attention over the entire sequence would expose 64,512² ≈ 4.16 billion token pairs per layer. At 24 layers and even two bytes per stored attention value, the naive attention maps alone would be roughly 200 GB for one sample and one head-equivalent before gradients and optimizer state. The product requirement therefore forces an architecture: encode each frame spatially, pool to perhaps 64 scene tokens, retain object/contact memory, and run temporal attention over compressed state. If 96 timesteps each contain 128 fused tokens, temporal input falls to 12,288 tokens; windowing or recurrent belief reduces it further.

Raw storage tells a different story. Two RGB streams at an average compressed 1.5 MB/s each contribute 24 MB per 8 seconds; depth at 0.8 MB/s contributes 6.4 MB; high-rate numerical sensors, actions, audio features, labels, and metadata add perhaps 4 MB. One example references about 34.4 MB of raw episode data. Ten million non-overlapping examples would be 344 TB before replication. In practice contexts overlap, so store each episode once and index windows rather than duplicating bytes.

5 · Count training compute, not only dataset size

For a dense Transformer, a rough first-order training estimate is 6PT floating-point operations, where P is active parameter count and T is training tokens. It hides attention, sparsity, multimodal encoders, and recomputation, but it catches order-of-magnitude mistakes. Suppose compression yields 12,000 model tokens per sample, the usable corpus has 8 million sampled windows per epoch, and training uses 2.5 effective epochs. Then T = 12,000 × 8,000,000 × 2.5 = 240 billion tokens. A 1.2B-parameter active model costs approximately:

Ftrain ≈ 6 × 1.2×10⁹ × 240×10⁹ = 1.728×10²¹ FLOPs

At an optimistic sustained 35% of 800 accelerators rated at 300 TFLOP/s each, effective throughput is 0.35 × 800 × 300×10¹² = 8.4×10¹⁶ FLOP/s. Idealized runtime is about 20,571 seconds, or 5.7 hours. That number is suspiciously low because the formula omits expensive vision encoders, attention terms, evaluation, data stalls, checkpointing, failed runs, and hyperparameter experiments. Reporting assumptions is the point. A realistic plan multiplies the core run by measured model FLOPs and an experimentation factor, often 5–20×.

Also count optimizer memory. Mixed-precision parameters, gradients, and Adam states can require roughly 12–20 bytes per parameter depending on sharding and precision, so 1.2B parameters may need 14–24 GB before activations. Video activations often dominate. Activation checkpointing trades extra compute for memory; fully sharded data parallelism divides model state; tensor parallelism splits large matrix operations; sequence/context parallelism splits long token axes; pipeline parallelism divides layers. The best mixture follows the measured bottleneck, not fashion.

Data delivery must sustain the accelerators too. If 800 workers each consume only 40 MB/s of decoded examples, aggregate input is 32 GB/s. Randomly seeking compressed video, decoding identical overlapping windows, or running calibration transforms on the training host can starve expensive compute. Shard episodes for sequential reads, cache deterministic encodings only when their tokenizer/version is fixed, prefetch asynchronously, and measure accelerator idle time. A cache saves encoder work but creates a versioned derived dataset; changing augmentation or encoder then requires invalidation rather than silent reuse.

6 · Sampling decides which physics receives gradient

Uniform time sampling overweights stationary views, empty motion, and routine success. Contact onset may occupy 100 ms of a 20-second episode—0.5% of time—yet determine success. A useful sampler mixes strata: common deployment traffic for calibration, event-centered windows for transitions, failure and recovery windows, long contexts for memory, high-action windows for system identification, and uncertain/disagreement cases from deployed models.

Difficulty should also be introduced in causal order. The tempting alternative is to maximize every axis—longest horizon, highest resolution, widest action set, all sensors, and perfectly balanced environments—from the first update. That produces a hard loss without revealing which prerequisite failed. A linear curriculum changes one source of difficulty only after the previous one carries usable signal:

  1. Make local change work before demanding memory. Begin the horizon curriculum with short, observation-corrected transitions. Once executed actions reliably produce the right immediate state change, scale toward long free rollout and memory. If long targets dominate too early, compounding error overwhelms learning before local dynamics work, so a bad long rollout does not tell you whether the defect began at step one or step twenty.
  2. Learn structure before spending capacity on texture. Begin resolution with coarse state and motion, then add the fine contact and identity cues that truly change decisions. Starting at maximum resolution lets texture consume capacity while geometry and action effects remain weak. The diagnostic is simple: reduce appearance detail; if task-state prediction collapses, the model may be using texture as a shortcut rather than maintaining the world.
  3. Identify supported actions before extrapolating. Begin with actions inside behavior support, where the data provides evidence about consequences. Then add safe interventions and edge cases to separate correlation from action effect. Pushing immediately into unsupported actions is not just statistically unstable; on hardware it can make data collection unsafe.
  4. Establish a synchronized sensory core before teaching degradation. Begin with stable modalities whose clocks and calibration are trusted. Then introduce noisy streams, correlated dropout, and missingness. If training always assumes the full clean sensor suite, the model may rely on a modality absent at deployment; if noise is added before the core is identifiable, the source of error becomes impossible to localize.
  5. Learn ordinary frequency before deliberately stressing the tail. Begin with common environments, then extend toward the long tail and controlled distribution shifts. Deliberately balanced training is valuable for capability, but treating that balance as the real-world frequency destroys probability calibration. Preserve the difference between “can the model handle this rare regime?” and “how often should it expect that regime?”

The implementation consequence is not five separate curricula but a measurable promotion rule. Advance an axis when its prerequisite passes a held-out diagnostic: local transition error before horizon, structured state before resolution, intervention curves before unsupported planning, sensor-subset calibration before dropout deployment, and common-regime calibration before tail reweighting. When a metric later regresses, roll back along this dependency chain instead of merely adding more mixed data.

If low-friction lifts are oversampled 20×, train the representation on them but evaluate and calibrate probabilities on the true deployment mix or apply importance weights. Maintain two dashboards: conditional capability by slice and expected performance under production frequencies. Hard-example mining must avoid a feedback loop where mislabeled or inherently stochastic cases consume all capacity.

7 · Train state, dynamics, and uncertainty in stages—but test jointly

A practical curriculum may pretrain modality encoders, learn short action-conditioned transitions, extend free-rollout horizon, add task and safety heads, then fine-tune with planner-generated hard cases. Staging reduces optimization difficulty, but frozen interfaces can lock in the wrong sufficient statistic. Joint fine-tuning should verify that a state useful for reconstruction is also useful for action effects and risk.

Scheduled sampling replaces some true previous states with model predictions, exposing compounding error. Overshooting predicts several future latent states directly from one anchor, encouraging longer consistency. Contrastive and object-permanence losses preserve identity. Distributional heads represent multiple futures; ensembles or posterior samples capture epistemic uncertainty. Calibrate after the full pipeline, since planner selection changes the action distribution and often reveals overconfidence.

Track error versus horizon rather than one average. Separate one-step posterior reconstruction from prior free rollout. Report losses per modality, action magnitude, contact mode, scene, robot, and policy. A declining total loss can hide rising collision false negatives. Evaluate old checkpoints on a frozen golden set and new data on a freshness set to distinguish regression from distribution drift.

8 · Planning creates a second, multiplicative compute bill

Training throughput and imagination throughput are different. Planning cost depends on transition evaluations, decoding, candidate generation, and synchronization. For sampling-based MPC, a useful accountant is:

transition calls per decision = K candidates × H latent steps × I refinements × E ensemble members × S stochastic samples decoded outputs = selected candidates/steps only, if latent costs are available end-to-end latency = encode/correct + propose + rollout + score + select + safety margin

Worked rollout bill

At 10 Hz, Countertop Assistant has a 100 ms decision period. Reserve 20 ms for sensor alignment/state correction, 15 ms for motion-controller and network jitter, and 10 ms safety margin, leaving 55 ms for planning. Cross-entropy-method MPC evaluates K=256 candidate action chunks for H=20 latent steps, refines I=4 times, uses E=3 ensemble members, and S=2 stochastic futures. The full bill is:

256 × 20 × 4 × 3 × 2 = 122,880 latent transition evaluations per decision at 10 decisions/s = 1,228,800 evaluations/s per robot

A claim that “the model takes 4 ms” is irrelevant unless it states batch shape and this multiplier. If an accelerator executes a batch of 256 one-step transitions in 0.45 ms, sequential horizon and four refinements already cost 0.45 × 20 × 4 × 3 × 2 = 216 ms, missing the deadline before overhead. Options include reducing candidates/ensemble samples, predicting multi-step chunks, using a distilled value model, evaluating ensemble members in parallel, warm-starting from the previous plan, sharing deterministic prefixes, or calling the full planner only at uncertainty/event boundaries.

A revised configuration might use 128 candidates, 10 two-step chunks, two refinements, and two ensemble members: 5,120 transition calls. At 0.45 ms per batched step, serial depth is 10 × 2 × 2 = 40 batches, or 18 ms, leaving time for scoring. Decode structured risk at every step but render RGB only for debugging or the final few candidates. Cache the current belief and static scene; never re-encode eight seconds of video for every branch.

Hidden multiplier
Quote context encoding + horizon × candidates × refinement × ensembles × stochastic samples × decisions per second. Add denoising steps for diffusion and tree nodes/expansions for search. P50 single-forward latency is not a capacity plan.

9 · Serving is a stateful streaming control system

Each robot owns a belief instance keyed by model, calibration, and embodiment version. The service ingests out-of-order packets, advances state causally, branches candidate futures, returns action scores before deadline, and records uncertainty/fallback events. It must handle duplicate packets, packet loss, reconnect, reset, stale state eviction, and model migration. A stateless request containing “latest frame” discards exactly the memory a world model is meant to provide.

Specify latency as a distribution and deadline-miss policy. Measure P50, P95, P99, and worst bursts under realistic concurrency. If the plan is late, the robot should continue a verified safe controller or stop—not execute a stale action. Apply backpressure by dropping redundant visualization frames before force or action events. Admission control should preserve per-agent cadence rather than letting one long rollout starve others.

Deployment optimization includes caching encodings, batching compatible rollout stages, quantizing inference, distilling large models, compiling fixed shapes, separating slow semantic updates from fast belief updates, and placing compute near the robot. But every approximation must be re-evaluated for calibration and rare risks. Quantization that barely changes average error can still erase small collision logits.

10 · Close the data flywheel without training on your own fiction

Log the physical observation after every planned intervention, not just imagined trajectories. Join plan, uncertainty, executed action, controller overrides, outcome, and model version. Prioritize cases with external model error, ensemble disagreement, fallback, near miss, novel state, or human correction. A planner can generate useful candidate actions, but its predicted futures are not ground truth; replay them in a trusted simulator or controlled real setting before labeling consequences.

Version data, code, architecture, loss, tokenizer, calibration, and evaluation together. A model registry should record the deployment envelope: supported robots, sensor set, context, trusted horizon, latency hardware, calibration thresholds, and known unsafe slices. Promotion should require offline gates, shadow-mode comparison, bounded canary rollout, and rollback. Systems discipline is part of model quality because irreproducible data and stale beliefs produce behavioral failures.

11 · A first-principles scaling review

  1. Trajectory: Are observation and executed action aligned on physical time with cuts and invalidity explicit?
  2. Information: Which hidden variables need raw resolution, and which can use objects, latents, or multirate summaries?
  3. Tokens: Write tokens per modality × rate × duration × views, then future and batch counts.
  4. Training: Compute corpus tokens, model FLOPs, optimizer/activation memory, experimental multiplier, and data bandwidth.
  5. Sampling: Preserve production frequencies for calibration while ensuring rare causal transitions receive gradient.
  6. Rollouts: Multiply horizon, candidates, refinements, ensembles, stochastic samples, and decision frequency.
  7. Serving: Budget state correction, planning, control, jitter, safety margin, concurrency, and fallback.
  8. Flywheel: Collect external outcomes at the model’s error frontier and keep a frozen, leakage-resistant evaluation suite.

Where this points next

A data and compute system can efficiently train the wrong abstraction, produce calibrated pixels but incorrect action effects, or let a planner exploit tiny errors. Lesson 16 evaluates Countertop Assistant from observation alignment through hidden state, causal interventions, uncertainty, closed-loop utility, OOD behavior, system deadlines, and product safety—then turns the complete series into a reusable design-interview framework.

Takeaway
Preserve the trajectory before scaling the model. Compute every modality token, every training pass, and every imagined branch. Sample rare dynamics without forgetting deployment frequencies. Architect training parallelism around actual memory/compute, and architect serving around persistent belief and a deadline-miss policy. Scaling is the discipline of making the entire causal loop affordable and reproducible.

Interview prompts

  • Why store commanded and executed action separately? Controllers delay, saturate, clip, or reject commands; only executed action identifies the physical transition, while the difference diagnoses actuation and policy failures.
  • What is the most dangerous train/eval leakage in trajectory data? Adjacent windows from the same episode, future-smoothed state, or future outcome information can enter both sets and make memorization or look-ahead appear to be prediction.
  • Why does raw token accounting change architecture? Two modest video streams already create tens of thousands of tokens and billions of dense attention pairs, forcing spatial compression, multirate state, sparse attention, or recurrence.
  • Why is uniform clip sampling weak? It overweights static common moments while contact, failure, intervention, and recovery occupy little wall-clock time but dominate decision quality.
  • How do you oversample rare failures without ruining calibration? Use a mixed capability sampler for training, retain true-frequency validation, and calibrate or importance-weight outputs on the deployment distribution.
  • What is the complete MPC compute product? State correction plus candidates × horizon × refinement iterations × ensembles × stochastic samples, multiplied by decision rate; add denoising or tree-expansion factors where applicable.
  • Why can an efficiently trained model still be impossible to deploy? Autoregressive horizon, candidate branching, tail latency, and concurrency may miss the control deadline even when parallel training throughput is excellent.
  • What belongs in a deployed model’s contract? Supported sensors/robots, tokenizer and calibration versions, context and trusted horizon, hardware latency envelope, uncertainty thresholds, fallback, known weak slices, and rollback path.
