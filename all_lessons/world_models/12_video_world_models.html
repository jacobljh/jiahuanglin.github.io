Video world models

Compress observed trajectories, model branching visual futures, and make actions causally control those futures—without confusing a plausible movie for a faithful simulator.

The dependency
Lesson 11 made geometry explicit. This lesson takes a complementary path: learn state and dynamics from video-scale data with fewer geometric commitments. The linear path is trajectory distribution → representation → temporal generator → action interface → persistent memory → objectives → controlled evaluation.

1 · Start from the decision the video must support

Imagine a delivery robot approaching a temporary fork in a road. The same camera prefix can be followed by three commands: steer left around a barrier, continue straight and brake, or steer right into a side lane. A useful model should generate different consequences from the same observed past. It should preserve the barrier, other vehicles, road layout, and the robot’s speed; respect the chosen control; represent several possible pedestrian reactions; and remain uncertain where evidence is insufficient.

The object to model is therefore a conditional distribution over future trajectories, not “the next pretty frame”:

pθ(xt+1:t+H | x≤t, at:t+H−1, g, mt),    mt = persistent belief from earlier evidence

x may be pixels or compressed visual latents, a contains low-level controls or higher-level actions, g is goal/context, and mt retains facts that may no longer be visible. The output is distributional because other agents, hidden surfaces, and stochastic events allow multiple futures.

From first principles, a video world model must answer four separate questions:

  1. What information survives compression? A codec defines the smallest event the dynamics can represent.
  2. How is time factorized? Token order, block size, and temporal stride determine compute and error propagation.
  3. Where does control enter? Action alignment and conditioning determine whether alternative interventions actually diverge.
  4. What is remembered beyond the context window? Long-lived identity cannot depend only on the most recent visible frames.

2 · The tokenizer sets the simulator’s resolution

Raw video is enormous. A 16-frame, megapixel clip contains tens of millions of color values, and planning may require hundreds of candidate rollouts. Most systems first encode video into a smaller grid or sequence. This is not neutral preprocessing: whatever the encoder discards cannot be recovered by the dynamics.

A spatial image codec maps each frame to a lower-resolution feature grid. A spatiotemporal codec maps a short tube of frames to tokens, compressing both space and time. Larger patches and temporal strides cut compute, but the delivery robot may lose a thin bicycle spoke, a traffic light transition, or the exact instant its tire contacts a curb. A codec optimized for perceptual reconstruction may remove tiny details humans barely notice even when those details determine the next state.

There are two broad latent types:

  • Discrete tokens: an encoder selects codebook IDs. They support categorical likelihood and familiar language-model machinery. Compression is explicit, but a limited codebook causes quantization error or dead codes; one symbol change can produce a visually large discontinuity.
  • Continuous latents: an encoder produces real-valued tensors. Nearby codes can represent nearby images and work naturally with diffusion, flow, or Gaussian transitions. Their density is harder to score directly, and an overpowered decoder may hide state inconsistency behind smooth outputs.
z1:T = Eφ(x1:T),    x̂1:T = Dψ(z1:T),    Lcodec = Lrecon + λpercLperceptual + λrateR(z)

The rate term rewards compression. Reconstruction and perceptual terms reward visible fidelity. A world-model codec needs additional preservation tests: can a probe recover object count, pose, speed, contact state, text/signals, and rare safety cues? Can the transition predict the next latent without inventing decoder details? Evaluate these before scaling the temporal model; a missing variable cannot be repaired by a larger transformer.

3 · Temporal factorization determines speed, context, and drift

After encoding, the model must turn a joint future distribution into computable pieces. Different architectures make different conditional-independence and sampling choices.

Rather than memorize architecture names, derive them from how the simulator is allowed to reveal the future:

  1. Reveal one token after another: autoregression. The joint distribution becomes ∏k p(zk | z<k, a). This gives a direct likelihood, flexible sequence and context handling, and a natural fit for discrete tokens. The naive idea is that an accurate next token automatically yields an efficient trajectory. In video, however, one frame contains many tokens, so sampling requires many serial steps. An early mistake then becomes part of the context and is treated as fact. Measure wall-clock latency and open-loop error growth, not only next-token likelihood.
  2. Reveal a region together: masked or block prediction. Instead of committing to a strict token order, hide a future block and iteratively fill its regions. Parallel updates reduce serial sampling and let locations use within-block context. But no true future observation exists at inference, so the mask schedule becomes part of the model. A poor confidence order can fill a late consequence before its early cause and create inconsistent time. Diagnose by changing the schedule while holding weights and data fixed; large causal changes show that the inference procedure, not merely the learned distribution, needs attention.
  3. Start with a whole noisy trajectory and refine it: diffusion or flow. Global denoising can coordinate a lane change across every future frame and naturally express several coherent modes. This solves the blurry-average failure of deterministic regression. The cost is several network evaluations over the future block, and a powerful visual prior may still weaken exact action response. The implementation consequence is a quality–latency schedule plus explicit action-contrast tests at every denoising budget.
  4. Carry one compact state forward: recurrent latent dynamics. A single transition per time step creates a natural belief bottleneck and supports cheap long rollouts. This is attractive when a planner must compare thousands of futures. The compression that creates speed can discard fine photorealistic detail or collapse diverse futures. Diagnose state and risk prediction before judging decoded texture; if the compact rollout is decision-correct, decode only selected candidates.
  5. Separate slow intent from fast detail: a hierarchical hybrid. Long-horizon layout, route, or keyframes can evolve slowly while a lower level fills local motion and appearance. This reduces the impossible demand that one clock capture both minutes of context and millisecond contact. The new failure is disagreement between levels: the slow plan says “turn right” while local frames drift left. Training and scheduling become more complex, so test cross-level consistency and action timing explicitly.

The Generative Continuous track derives diffusion and flow; the Computer Vision video track covers the observed-video side and shares this lesson's p(zt+1:t+H | z≤t, at:t+H−1) notation. Here the important distinction is trajectory generation. Denoising all future latent frames jointly can coordinate the shape of a lane change better than predicting isolated frames, but each denoising iteration adds latency. Autoregression can react step by step, yet a rollout of thousands of patch tokens may be far too slow for planning.

Factorization also defines causal availability. If a block model attends bidirectionally across the whole target clip during training, it must have an explicit inference procedure when future observations are absent. If an autoregressive model orders all spatial tokens of frame t+1 before frame t+2, motion is causal but sampling is long. Interleaving space and time changes which dependencies are easy. There is no architecture-only winner; compute the latency per rollout, number of rollouts per decision, and error at the decision horizon.

Architecture from first principles
First choose the decision horizon and deadline. Then count how many future variables must be generated, which of them must agree globally, and where uncertainty should branch. Only then select a serial, blockwise, trajectory-level, recurrent, or hierarchical factorization. The architecture name is the consequence of the dependency pattern.

4 · Action conditioning must be aligned, identifiable, and impossible to ignore

The delivery robot’s steering command is not a caption. It is an intervention applied over a known time interval through a physical actuator. Represent action at the model’s clock. If controls arrive at 20 Hz and video tokens represent four-frame tubes at 5 Hz, aggregate the controls or integrate them through a dynamics substep. A one-frame timestamp shift can teach braking to correlate with deceleration after it has already happened.

Actions can enter as tokens interleaved with video, embeddings added to temporal blocks, cross-attended control sequences, or parameters of an explicit transition. The mechanism matters less than three tests:

  1. Sensitivity: from the identical prefix and random seed, changing action must change the appropriate future variables.
  2. Selectivity: steering should change ego trajectory and induced reactions, not randomly alter weather, vehicle identity, or scene layout.
  3. Magnitude and timing: response should scale and occur with realistic actuator delay rather than merely match action direction.

Observational training data creates a shortcut. Drivers brake because a pedestrian appears; therefore “brake” correlates with pedestrians. A model can render a pedestrian whenever it sees a brake token, reversing cause and effect. Diverse behavior, randomized or exploratory actions where safe, known policies, counterfactual pairs, and causal regularization help. At evaluation, replay the same initial state under matched alternative commands and measure state differences.

Action may be hierarchical. “Take the right lane” is a goal; steering angle and torque are controls. A high-level model can predict a route-level latent plan while a low-level dynamics model handles actuator consequences. Confusing these levels yields vague conditioning: a language command may influence scene semantics without providing the temporal precision needed for control.

5 · Visual fidelity and control fidelity are different axes

A polished video can fail as a world model in several independent ways. Separate the evaluation targets:

  • Image fidelity: individual frames contain natural texture, lighting, and detail.
  • Temporal fidelity: motion is smooth and local appearance does not flicker.
  • State fidelity: objects keep identity, count, geometry, and hidden persistence across time.
  • Action fidelity: changing an intervention produces the correct conditional consequence with correct timing.
  • Physical fidelity: contact, collision, speed, conservation, and rare events obey the environment sufficiently for the task.
  • Decision fidelity: a policy or plan selected using generated rollouts succeeds in the real system.

These axes can conflict. A pixel loss averages uncertain futures, producing blur but reasonable expected motion. A perceptual or adversarial loss makes sharp frames, perhaps by sampling visually convincing but state-inconsistent detail. Diffusion can generate exquisite alternative futures while weak conditioning makes them barely respond to steering. A compact recurrent model may look crude yet preserve lane position and reward accurately enough for control.

For the fork example, compare the predicted ego path, barrier clearance, braking distance, and other-agent tracks under left/straight/right actions. Then evaluate appearance. Human preference alone overweights texture and cinematic coherence; it rarely notices that an occluded bicycle became a different bicycle or that braking caused the obstacle.

6 · Rolling generation requires a belief beyond the sliding window

Video context is finite. Suppose a red car passes behind a truck and remains hidden for ten seconds. If the corresponding evidence leaves the attention window, a pure next-window generator can forget the car. When it reappears, the model may create a new vehicle, change its color, or omit it. Increasing context delays the problem and raises attention cost; it does not define what should persist.

A robust system combines memory scales:

  • Recent high-resolution latents preserve exact motion and appearance for immediate prediction.
  • Recurrent belief summarizes history into a fixed-cost state updated by observations and generated transitions.
  • Object tracks or spatial memory retain identities and world-frame facts through occlusion.
  • Keyframes or retrieval memory recover detailed appearance when a place or entity returns.
  • Slow scene latents hold layout, weather, goals, and long-lived context while fast latents model motion.

Memory update must distinguish observed from imagined evidence. During open-loop rollout, generated frames are samples from the model, not new measurements. Feeding a sampled red-car position back as certainty makes the model self-confirm its own hallucination. The belief transition should grow uncertainty with horizon. When a real camera frame arrives, a posterior update can collapse or reweight hypotheses.

observe:   bt = U(b̄t, xt)    ≠    imagine:   b̄t+1 = T(bt, at),   H[b̄t+1] generally increases

Long-horizon consistency therefore connects directly to lessons 2 and 6: video generation is an observation decoder around a belief process, not a replacement for belief.

7 · Multimodality requires futures, not indecisive averages

At the fork, a pedestrian may wait or cross. Predicting the average—half a pedestrian in the lane—is useless. A generative model should represent coherent hypotheses over the entire trajectory. One sample might contain “wait, then cross”; another “cross immediately.” Within each sample, positions and reactions should remain consistent across frames.

Diffusion, flow, discrete token sampling, mixture latents, or branching recurrent states can represent alternatives. But diversity alone is not calibrated uncertainty. A model that invents ten cinematic outcomes while missing the rare collision is diverse and unsafe. Evaluate whether event frequencies and spatial distributions match data under controlled conditions, including tail events important to decisions.

For planning, share randomness when comparing actions. Generate paired rollouts from the same initial belief and exogenous seed so that differences are attributable mainly to action rather than unrelated sampled weather or pedestrian intent. Sample enough hypotheses to estimate risk, while recognizing that generative compute competes directly with decision latency.

8 · Objectives teach different—and sometimes conflicting—behaviors

A video world model is often trained with a weighted objective:

L = Lcodec + λgenLgen + λstateLstate + λactLaction + λconsLconsistency + λtaskLtask

Each term has a distinct job:

  • Codec reconstruction/rate determines representable detail and compression cost.
  • Generative likelihood, denoising, or flow loss learns the conditional future distribution in token or latent space.
  • State probes supervise depth, segmentation, flow, objects, ego motion, contact, or reward so causal variables survive compression.
  • Action-contrast loss penalizes rollouts that ignore or mis-time control; paired interventions are especially valuable.
  • Temporal/identity consistency rewards track persistence and cross-view agreement without forcing all futures to be identical.
  • Task loss evaluates value, safety cost, or policy success so the model preserves decision-relevant facts.

Teacher forcing trains on true past tokens, while deployment conditions on generated ones. This exposure gap causes compounding error. Scheduled sampling exposes the model to its rollouts but can destabilize learning and blur the likelihood interpretation. Multi-step latent losses, block prediction, noise injection, and training on model-generated prefixes each address part of the gap. None substitutes for open-loop evaluation.

Data composition is equally important. Passive internet video teaches broad appearance and common motion but rarely exposes action labels or controlled counterfactuals. Robot logs provide actions but are narrow and policy-biased. Simulation supplies controlled interventions and state labels but has a domain gap. Combine them deliberately: pretrain visual priors broadly, align representations to sensor/action logs, and fine-tune dynamics on interventions. Balance common cruising against rare collisions and recoveries so frequency does not erase decision importance.

9 · Training and inference have different clocks

Training can process many clips in parallel; control must sample futures within a deadline. Count end-to-end operations, not model parameters alone. For an autoregressive model, cost scales with generated token count even with a key/value cache. For diffusion, it scales with denoising steps times future block size. A recurrent latent transition may roll out cheaply, with only selected trajectories decoded to video.

This suggests a two-stage planner. Roll out thousands of candidates in compact latent state, score them with reward and constraint heads, then decode a few finalists for inspection or refined collision checks. Video decoding every candidate wastes compute if the decision queries only lane occupancy and risk. Conversely, a creative media product may allocate most compute to pixels because appearance is the task.

Temporal stride creates another tradeoff. Coarse steps extend horizon cheaply but skip fast events. A hierarchical system can evolve slow scene state at low frequency and use a local fine model near contacts or control changes. Adaptive horizon and resolution make more sense than generating every patch at uniform fidelity.

10 · Failure diagnosis with causal tests

Video errors are especially easy to misdiagnose because a plausible decoder can hide a broken state. Move from the earliest interface to the latest: codec → memory → transition → action alignment → planner. Stop at the first place where the controlled expectation fails.

  1. Left and right commands produce almost the same road. The initial scene is identical, so the physical futures should diverge mainly in ego path and induced reactions. Reuse the exact prefix and generative seed, then change only the action. If the paths remain similar, conditioning is ignored or the data contains too few action contrasts. Strengthen action injection and add counterfactual data; pass the test only when the response is selective rather than a wholesale scene change.
  2. The brake response appears one second late. The model may know braking physics but receive the control at the wrong video token. Run an impulse-action calibration sequence with known command, actuator, and frame timestamps. Locate whether delay enters during control aggregation, the temporal codec, or the physical actuator model. Correct the alignment there; adding model capacity cannot fix a shifted clock.
  3. Frames stay sharp while vehicles change identity. A perceptual objective can make every local frame convincing without carrying a persistent entity. Hide a tracked vehicle for a long occlusion and inspect which history the reappearing vehicle inherits. If identity resets while texture remains sharp, add object or spatial memory and an identity loss. The diagnostic is track continuity, not human preference for the clip.
  4. Rollouts blur at intersections. Several futures are physically possible, but deterministic regression minimizes error by averaging them. A pedestrian halfway between waiting and crossing is not a coherent future. Introduce a trajectory-level stochastic latent and generate complete branches. Then score calibrated branch frequencies; sharp samples alone do not prove that probability mass is correct.
  5. Rollouts are diverse, but the steering effect is random. Stochasticity solved mode averaging but now overwhelms control. Use the same exogenous noise for paired left and right rollouts. If unrelated scene changes dominate the action difference, regularize selective action response and strengthen causal control pathways. Useful uncertainty changes hidden events; it does not erase the intervention.
  6. Rare pedestrians disappear. Before blaming temporal dynamics, reconstruct the observed input through the codec. If the pedestrian is already absent, coarse stride, compression, or frequency-weighted training discarded the cue. Rebalance the data, reduce spatial or temporal stride, or add state supervision. Only if the codec preserves the pedestrian should transition training become the suspect.
  7. The closed-loop policy exploits an impossible gap. The planner searches more aggressively than the data-collection policy and finds model error outside familiar actions. Run an adversarial planning audit and compare proposed trajectories with trusted execution. Penalize epistemic uncertainty, correct with real data, and replan over a shorter validated horizon. The real diagnostic is whether exploitation disappears without merely forbidding all useful novel actions.
  8. A long video begins to repeat scenery. Recent frames still look plausible, but the sliding window no longer knows which place it occupies. Leave a location, travel beyond the context window, and return. If the scene resets or loops, add a map, keyframe retrieval, or slow scene state. Persistence should be tested by return and loop closure, not by extending a single forward-looking clip.

Always test the representation before blaming dynamics. If a reconstructed input already loses the pedestrian signal, the temporal model is innocent. Test the sensor decoder separately by reconstructing held-out frames. Test transition separately with latent state probes. Test action causality with paired interventions. Finally test the closed loop, where a planner may seek precisely the model’s weakest regions.

11 · Build an evaluation ladder, not one video score

Begin at the codec. Reconstruct held-out clips and measure both perceptual quality and decision probes: small-object recall, speed, signal state, contact timing, text where relevant, and identity. Stratify by object size and motion. If the encoded representation loses a brake light, future prediction should not receive the blame.

Next evaluate one-step transition under observed context. Report latent likelihood or denoising loss, but also decode state variables and calibrate their uncertainty. Then increase open-loop horizon while withholding real future frames. Plot error, identity survival, collision recall, and calibration against simulated seconds rather than only frame count, because temporal strides differ. Separate static scenes, ego motion, object motion, interaction, occlusion, and rare events so an easy majority cannot hide the actual weakness.

The third rung is intervention. Construct groups with the same or closely matched initial state and alternative actions. Use common random seeds for generative noise. Measure a response curve: predicted trajectory or speed as steering/brake magnitude varies. The correct sign alone is insufficient; slope, delay, saturation, and collateral changes matter. Check action null tests too: a zero steering perturbation should not change unrelated scene attributes.

The fourth rung evaluates distributions. For genuinely ambiguous pedestrian intent, draw many rollouts and compare event frequencies, time-to-event, spatial coverage, and tail risk to held-out outcomes. Proper scoring rules reward both sharpness and calibration; best-of-many metrics can hide a model that assigns tiny probability to the truth among mostly wrong samples. Evaluate diversity conditionally so random texture changes do not count as useful future modes.

The final rung is decision transfer. Let the actual planner choose actions using the model, then execute in a trusted simulator, replay system, or carefully bounded real environment. Compare task reward, constraint violations, and regret against planning with ground-truth dynamics where available. Search for model exploitation by allowing an optimizer to propose unusual action sequences. A video model can have excellent average prediction and still contain one exploitable blind spot that the planner repeatedly seeks.

Report serving cost alongside accuracy: milliseconds per encoded observation, per transition or denoising step, memory per active environment, candidates evaluated before deadline, and energy where deployment constrains it. These metrics determine whether a nominally superior model can actually participate in closed-loop control.

12 · Product and system tradeoffs

Use case determines the appropriate fidelity. For entertainment, diverse coherent pixels and controllable camera/story semantics may dominate; exact hidden-state calibration may not. For robotics, low latency, conservative uncertainty, geometry, and action response dominate; photorealism is useful for diagnostics but can be secondary. For synthetic training data, label consistency and coverage matter as much as realism. For forecasting, probability calibration and rare-event recall matter more than one impressive sample.

Memory and serving architecture follow. Long-context transformers require cache memory proportional to history. Diffusion rolls can saturate accelerators. Token codecs save bandwidth but introduce a versioned interface: changing the codebook invalidates stored latents and temporal checkpoints. Separate observed and generated caches, store action timestamps, and make rollout seeds reproducible for debugging.

Safety adds guardrails. Limit planning horizon to where validation supports accuracy; replan when new observations arrive; penalize epistemically uncertain regions; combine learned video with hard geometric constraints where possible; and monitor action sensitivity. A system should be able to say “this rollout looks plausible but is not trusted for braking distance.”

Language can condition scene, goals, and high-level action, but it is context rather than a law of physics. A strong text prior can insert a stereotypical outcome that contradicts sensors. Ground every named entity to persistent visual state, distinguish instruction from actuator command, and let new evidence correct the narrative.

13 · Bridge: video must become embodied belief

The delivery robot does not only see. It measures wheel speed, joint position, inertial motion, sound, touch, and sometimes language instructions. These streams arrive at different rates and expose variables video cannot identify. Lesson 13 first turns these passive video predictors into playable environments — action-conditioned, real-time, and memory-consistent enough to act inside — and Lesson 14 then ties the belief to a body: proprioception defines what the agent did, touch disambiguates contact, language specifies goals, and vision supplies broad scene evidence.

Takeaway
A video world model is an action-conditioned distribution over persistent trajectories, implemented through a codec, temporal generator, and memory. Discrete versus continuous and autoregressive versus diffusion are engineering choices; the world-model standard is harder: preserve causal details, branch coherently, respond selectively and on time to intervention, represent uncertainty, and improve real decisions.

Interview prompts

  • What does the video tokenizer decide? It sets the spatial and temporal resolution of representable events, the compute budget, and which decision-relevant cues are irreversibly discarded.
  • Discrete tokens or continuous latents? Discrete tokens give categorical likelihood and explicit rate but quantize; continuous latents preserve smooth neighborhoods and suit diffusion/flow but are harder to density-model. Choose using codec probes and rollout cost.
  • Why can a strong video generator be a weak world model? Image plausibility can coexist with identity drift, wrong causality, ignored actions, uncalibrated uncertainty, or poor closed-loop decisions.
  • How do you test action fidelity? Hold initial observations and exogenous randomness fixed, vary only the action, then measure selective state changes, magnitude, and timing against real matched interventions.
  • Why does rolling context forget the world? Long-occluded facts leave the finite window. Persistent recurrent, object, spatial, or retrieval memory is needed, with uncertainty that grows during imagination.
  • How do teacher forcing and deployment differ? Training conditions on true prior tokens, while rollout conditions on model samples. Small errors become future inputs and accumulate; multi-step training and open-loop tests expose the gap.
  • When would you prefer a compact recurrent model over photorealistic diffusion? When control needs many low-latency candidate rollouts and decision-state accuracy matters more than decoded texture. Decode only finalists if necessary.
  • How should multimodal futures be evaluated? Check coherent trajectory samples, calibrated event frequencies including tails, state and action fidelity, and downstream policy transfer—not just diversity or human visual preference.
