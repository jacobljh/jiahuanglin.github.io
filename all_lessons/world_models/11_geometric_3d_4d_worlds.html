Geometric world models

Put persistent state in a world coordinate system, separate sensor motion from environmental change, and use a differentiable sensor to connect geometry back to observations.

Boundary with the 3D track
The 3D Vision track derives projection, calibration, SE(3), multiview reconstruction, NeRF, and Gaussian Splatting in depth. This lesson does not repeat that material. It uses those mechanisms as components and asks a different question: when does geometric representation become the evolving, action-conditioned state of a world model?

1 · Derive the split from an ambiguity in pixels

Continue with the kitchen robot and its blue mug. The robot rotates its head ten degrees to the right. In its image, the counter, mug, bowl, and cupboard all move left. Nothing in the kitchen moved. A moment later, the robot holds its camera still while a person slides the mug right. The mug again moves in the image, but now the world did change. Pixel displacement alone does not tell these causes apart.

A useful model factors three mechanisms:

st+1 ∼ Tθ(st, at, wt),    ct+1 = C(ct, atsensor),    ot ∼ Oψ(st, ct, ηt)

st is the environment state in a stable coordinate system; ct is the sensor pose and calibration; T changes the world under action and disturbance w; O renders or senses that world from a viewpoint with noise η. Moving only c changes observations without changing the environment. Moving the mug changes s, so all cameras should agree on its new world position.

This separation is not cosmetic. It gives the model the right invariance: the answer to “where is the mug in the kitchen?” should not change when the robot turns its head. It also gives the right equivariance: if the robot’s own coordinate frame moves by a known transform, represented positions should transform predictably rather than be relearned from examples.

2 · Geometry is useful because it makes certain queries direct

Why pay to maintain 3D rather than leave everything in a latent vector? Return to the query-cost principle. The robot repeatedly asks whether its gripper trajectory intersects the cupboard, how far the mug is from the counter edge, which surface occludes the mug, and what a new camera pose would see. In an explicit metric state, these become geometric operations. In an unconstrained embedding, a learned network must approximate each query and may fail outside its training distribution.

Geometry is not all knowledge. It describes spatial arrangement and often surface appearance. It does not automatically contain mass, friction, fragility, motor delay, social intent, or “this mug belongs to Alice.” A practical state therefore has explicit spatial variables and learned hidden features:

st = (Gt, Kt, ht),    Gt = geometry,    Kt = kinematic entities,    ht = unobserved physical/semantic factors

The right question is not “should the whole model be geometric?” It is “which high-value constraints and queries should be guaranteed by the representation, and which should remain learned?” Explicit coordinates can make collision and visibility reliable while latent features carry material and intent.

3 · A coordinate frame is a contract

Every position must answer “relative to what?” A camera frame is convenient for immediate perception but moves with the sensor. A robot base frame is useful for manipulation but changes as the robot drives. A local world or map frame supports persistence across motion but can drift when localization is uncertain. Object-local coordinates are ideal for articulated shape and deformation.

Use transforms as typed interfaces. If WTC maps camera coordinates into world coordinates and a point projects from depth as pC, then pW = WTCpC. The notation matters less than the discipline: never combine states unless their frames and timestamps are known. Many apparent “model failures” are actually stale poses, incorrect transform direction, or asynchronous sensors.

Localization uncertainty should propagate into world state. If camera pose is uncertain, repeatedly fusing measurements as exact can blur static surfaces or make them look dynamic. A probabilistic system maintains a joint belief over map, objects, and pose:

p(st, ct | o1:t, a1:t−1) ∝ p(ot | st, ct) ∫ p(st, ct | st−1, ct−1, at−1) bt−1 d(s,c)

This equation says that sensing can correct both the world and the sensor pose. A cupboard edge that appears shifted may mean the cupboard moved, the pose estimate drifted, or the depth sensor was noisy. The posterior must arbitrate using priors and multiple observations.

4 · Choose a 3D state by the operations it must support

Make the choice by walking from the physical operation backward to the state it needs. Each representation below is a sensible answer to one bottleneck and a poor answer to another:

  1. If the arm must ask “is this volume safe?” thousands of times, use occupancy or signed distance. A naïve planner that repeatedly decodes an unconstrained latent has to relearn collision for every query. A grid makes collision, free space, ray traversal, and local convolution native. The implementation consequence is an excellent planning substrate whose memory and resolution scale cubically with represented extent unless it is sparse. Fine surfaces over large volumes and named identity are awkward. Diagnose the tradeoff by shrinking cell size and measuring both memory and thin-object collision recall.
  2. If most motion lies on the ground, collapse the right dimension. Driving and navigation repeatedly query ground-plane motion, so a bird’s-eye-view or height map makes multi-camera fusion and path planning fast. The naïve extension is to use it for every 3D task. That fails on overhangs, shelves, and stacked objects because distinct heights project to the same planar neighborhood. The derived consequence is clear: BEV is an efficient navigation state but a lossy manipulation state. Test it on height-separated obstacles rather than only open roads.
  3. If control needs surfaces, normals, and joints, expose them. Contact with a known cupboard door is awkward to infer afresh from pixels. A mesh or articulated model gives surface contact, normals, and deformation handles directly, making the state editable and controllable when structure is known. The failure appears when topology changes or point correspondence is unknown. A torn bag or newly separated object cannot be repaired by simply moving existing vertices. Diagnose with topology-changing events and use a residual or remeshing path rather than pretending the template still fits.
  4. If the state must absorb measurements incrementally and render quickly, keep explicit primitives. Point sets, surfels, and 3D Gaussians can fuse local observations and splat them into views efficiently. That makes them strong sensing-model substrates. The naïve assumption is that rendering primitives automatically provide persistent physical parts. In fact, watertight collision is difficult, and primitives can split, merge, or drift across time. The implementation therefore needs a lifecycle and correspondence rule; long-rollout primitive identity is the diagnostic, not one-frame render quality.
  5. If arbitrary continuous spatial queries and compact high fidelity dominate, learn a field. An implicit neural field maps a coordinate to density, color, or distance without storing every voxel. This avoids a dense grid’s uniform memory cost. But planning and rollout may require many sequential network evaluations, and online updates can be expensive. The consequence is compact fidelity whose query and update cost may dominate the control deadline. Measure total queries per candidate plan, not only model size or rendered PSNR.
  6. If actions target named rigid things, factor pose, shape, and latent properties. A mug can keep a stable identity and shape while a six-degree-of-freedom pose evolves. This makes rigid motion and entity-specific action compact and compositional. The naïve idea is to force the entire world into the same object template. Fluids, cluttered background, and novel topology violate that assumption. Residual dense state is therefore an implementation requirement, and unexplained sensor error is the diagnostic that the object bias has become a mismatch.

For the kitchen robot, a hybrid is natural: a coarse signed-distance field for arm collision, an articulated robot model, explicit mug and bowl poses, and a dense appearance map for view prediction. Planning may first reject actions using the coarse field, then use an accurate mesh near contact. Hierarchy saves compute because not every query needs millimeter precision.

Representation comparison must use common axes. A static neural field can be optimized for minutes; a world model may need a new state at 20 Hz. A mesh may answer contact precisely but resist topological repair. The derivations above explain why; this compact map makes the resulting trade-offs easy to retrieve:

The practical answer is often a hierarchy that rejects most actions with a cheap representation and invokes expensive precision only near contact or uncertainty.

The recurring derivation
Ask what physical query dominates. Try to answer it with the current state. Locate the expensive or unreliable step. Add a representation that makes that step native. Then test the failure created by its assumptions. A hybrid is not indecision; it is the consequence of several high-value queries having incompatible computational shapes.

5 · From 3D snapshots to 4D identity

A 3D reconstruction at every time step is not yet a coherent 4D world. The robot may reconstruct the mug at t and again at t+1 without knowing which surface point or entity persisted. Prediction under action requires correspondence.

Several factorizations impose it:

  • Rigid entities: store a shape in object coordinates and evolve a six-degree-of-freedom pose. Every surface point inherits the object transform.
  • Articulated models: store parts and joints; dynamics evolve joint angles and root pose. A cupboard door rotates rather than becoming a new surface each frame.
  • Canonical space plus deformation: map a persistent canonical point to its time-dependent position. Useful for cloth and bodies, but deformation must remain physically and topologically plausible.
  • Scene flow or persistent primitives: predict motion for observed points, surfels, or Gaussians. This is flexible, though identities can drift, split, or merge.
  • Eulerian fields: represent quantities at fixed spatial locations, appropriate for fluids; identity belongs to transported material, not fixed grid cells.

The running mug is well served by rigid pose plus shape. The person’s shirt needs deformation. Steam above the sink is better treated as a field. A single representation forced across all three either wastes compute or encodes the wrong persistence assumption.

Simply adding time as an input to a radiance field, F(x,y,z,t) → (σ,c), can reproduce a recorded performance. But it may learn “at second 3 the mug is here” without a transition that accepts a push action. A stronger world model carries a state forward under intervention and renders from that state. Clock-conditioned interpolation answers what was recorded; dynamics answers what would happen if we acted differently.

6 · Differentiable sensing closes the learning loop

Explicit 3D labels are costly. A differentiable observation model lets ordinary sensors supervise hidden geometry. Given state s and camera c, it predicts an RGB image, depth map, silhouette, optical flow, event stream, or tactile contact. A residual between prediction and measurement sends gradients back to geometry, appearance, pose, and sometimes dynamics.

previous belief + action │ ▼ dynamics Tθ ─────────────▶ predicted 3D/4D belief │ sensor pose + calibration │ ▼ differentiable sensor Oψ │ RGB / depth / flow / touch │ compare to measurement ▼ posterior state correction

For a ray-based camera, the sensor accumulates color and opacity along a ray; for a splat renderer, projected primitives contribute according to depth and footprint; for a mesh, rasterization determines visible triangles. The exact renderer belongs to 3D vision. In the world-model loop, its conceptual role is an observation likelihood: “if this were the state and camera, how probable would the measurement be?”

Multiple sensor heads constrain different ambiguities. RGB supplies texture and semantics but confounds illumination. Depth directly constrains visible surface range. Optical flow constrains projected motion but mixes object motion with egomotion. Silhouettes constrain occupancy without interior depth. Touch is sparse but strongly constrains contact and local geometry. Predicting several views and modalities makes it harder for a latent to hide a physically inconsistent shortcut.

Differentiability does not guarantee identifiability. A wrong camera pose can be compensated by warped geometry; lighting can be baked into texture; a moving object can be explained as view-dependent appearance. Geometry priors, calibrated sensors, temporal correspondence, loop closure, and intervention data are needed to select the intended explanation.

7 · Geometry lets the agent choose informative viewpoints

An observation model is useful not only for explaining existing images but also for asking what a candidate sensor motion would reveal. The robot is uncertain whether the mug is directly behind the bowl or farther back near the counter edge. Both hypotheses explain the current camera because the bowl occludes the mug’s base. A small sideways head motion produces parallax and different predicted silhouettes. The robot can choose that view before attempting a grasp.

This is active perception. For candidate camera action ac, predict the observations each state hypothesis would produce and prefer a view expected to reduce decision-relevant uncertainty:

ac* = arg maxac 𝔼o′∼p(o′ | bt, ac)[H(bt) − H(bt+1 | o′, ac)] − λ cost(ac)

The entropy difference is expected information gain. In practice, the utility may target only uncertainty that affects the grasp: mug depth, handle visibility, or free clearance. A full scenic view can contain more bits yet be less valuable. Geometry makes candidate visibility and parallax computable before the motion.

Active sensing exposes whether the model really separated camera and world. If the agent predicts that turning its head will physically move the mug, it will choose nonsensical sensing actions. If its renderer is accurate but uncertainty does not update, it may repeatedly look at the same uninformative surface. Evaluate both predicted observation and posterior contraction.

8 · Separate egomotion, object motion, and apparent motion

Dynamic scenes combine motions. A background point has nonzero optical flow when the camera moves, even though its world velocity is zero. A car may be static in the world but move relative to an accelerating ego vehicle. A rotating object produces different point velocities across its surface. The decomposition should be explicit enough for the downstream task:

pC(t) = TCW(t) TWO(t) pO,    ṗimage = Jπ(pC) ṗC

The first transform is world-to-camera egomotion; the second is object-to-world motion; pO is a persistent object-local point; projection Jacobian Jπ converts 3D motion into image motion. Thus image flow is an observation of combined causes, not the world dynamics itself.

A common pipeline estimates ego pose from static regions, explains their image motion, and assigns residual motion to dynamic entities. But this can fail when most of the view is moving, an object dominates the image, or the “static” ground is an escalator. Joint inference over pose and entity motion is safer. Semantic priors can propose likely static structures, while temporal and multiview constraints decide whether the evidence agrees.

The distinction matters for forecasting. If an oncoming car appears to expand because the ego robot moves forward, a camera-frame extrapolation may double-count motion after a braking action. World-frame velocity plus planned ego trajectory produces the correct relative observation. Conversely, stabilizing everything as camera motion can erase a genuinely moving obstacle. Controlled sequences—camera-only motion, object-only motion, and both—should be basic unit tests.

9 · Dynamics should preserve constraints while admitting hidden variables

Once the state is geometric, a naïve network can still predict impossible geometry. Useful parameterizations enforce some structure: rotations stay on the rotation group, occupancy probabilities stay bounded, joints respect limits, and contact candidates arise only near surfaces. Residual learned dynamics can then model friction, compliance, or unmodeled effects.

Suppose the robot pushes the mug. Its future depends on pose and contact geometry, but also mass distribution, friction, liquid content, and whether the counter is wet. These properties may not be visually identifiable. Store them as uncertain latent variables h, update them from observed response, and predict a distribution:

p(st+1 | st, at) = ∫ p(st+1 | Gt, Kt, ht, at) p(ht | o1:t, a1:t−1) dht

A planner should then consider several plausible slides rather than trust a mean trajectory that may pass through no actual future. Geometry tells the model where interaction can happen; hidden physical belief tells it how the interaction may unfold.

Constraint losses help but should not become theater. Penalizing surface penetration in predicted meshes is useful. Declaring a rollout “physical” because it has smooth motion is not. Validate with intervention outcomes: contact timing, displacement under controlled forces, conservation where appropriate, and closed-loop task success.

10 · Training: separate what each signal teaches

A geometric world model often combines static scene data, passive video, and action trajectories. Each constrains different components:

  • Multiview reconstruction teaches the sensor model and spatial consistency, especially when camera pose is known.
  • Temporal photometric/depth loss teaches correspondence and motion, but may confuse lighting or occlusion with geometry.
  • Pose, flow, or scene-flow supervision anchors coordinates when labels exist.
  • Action-conditioned prediction trains the transition and distinguishes intervention from passive correlation.
  • Geometric regularizers encourage valid occupancy, smooth surfaces, rigid or articulated structure, and nonpenetration.
  • Task losses test whether collision, visibility, grasp, or navigation queries are actually accurate.

Use held-out cameras to test O, held-out times to test correspondence, and held-out actions to test T. This three-way split prevents a model from passing because one subsystem masks another. A perfect renderer with wrong dynamics fails action prediction; strong dynamics with a miscalibrated sensor fails image alignment; both can look acceptable on training trajectories.

Training schedules usually benefit from bootstrapping: calibrate and learn an initial geometry/renderer, introduce temporal state, then learn action-conditioned dynamics and jointly fine-tune. Fully joint learning from scratch is possible but allows too many compensating errors. Keep diagnostic heads for pose, depth, visibility, and object motion even if the final planner uses a compact latent.

11 · Failure diagnosis by controlled contrasts

Geometry makes failures measurable, but one symptom can still have several causes. The correct habit is not to guess from the final image. Hold two mechanisms fixed, vary the third, and inspect the earliest violated contract.

  1. A static cupboard swims while the camera moves. Because the cupboard is physically fixed, the error must enter through pose, depth, or a representation that is not equivariant to viewpoint. Use a calibrated static sequence. First freeze geometry and optimize only pose; then freeze pose and optimize only geometry. Whichever intervention removes the apparent motion identifies the faulty subsystem. The repair belongs there, not in dynamics.
  2. The mug leaves a duplicate after it moves. The naive mapper fused the mug into the static world and also created a dynamic track. Mask the tracked mug during map fusion and replay the same move. If the duplicate vanishes and vacated occupancy clears, dynamic/static separation—not rendering—was the mechanism. The implementation consequence is layered state with explicit clearing when an entity moves.
  3. Novel views look good, but a push is wrong. Good view synthesis shows that the observation model learned to render a fixed state; it says nothing about action-conditioned transition. Reset to the same initial state, match the camera, and apply different controlled pushes. If images remain plausible but displacement does not follow action, train and evaluate T separately from O.
  4. Rollout geometry slowly melts. One-step pixels can hide primitive correspondence drift or unconstrained dynamics. Choose canonical surface points and distances that should remain rigid, then plot their error with horizon. A systematic change derives the need for persistent correspondence or a rigidity-preserving parameterization rather than a stronger perceptual loss.
  5. Only RGB fails under new lighting. Before changing geometry, compare predicted depth and silhouette. If they remain correct while color changes, the world’s shape is intact and the appearance model has entangled illumination with texture. Condition illumination separately and keep geometric state fixed during the test.
  6. The collision checker misses thin handles. A coarse representation cannot answer a finer query, however well it was trained. Group test objects by feature size and measure clearance error. If error rises below the grid or primitive resolution, add local fine geometry near contact rather than increasing resolution everywhere.
  7. Every sensor shifts together. A modality-specific decoder is unlikely to move RGB, depth, and touch in the same direction. The shared world state or shared pose is the common cause. Compare against independent localization or fixed landmarks; then correct the shared transform instead of separately calibrating each sensor head.

The general sequence is physical invariant → observed violation → competing mechanisms → controlled contrast → local repair. Orbit the camera around a static scene to isolate sensing. Hold the camera fixed and intervene on an object to isolate dynamics. Replay the same action from several viewpoints to test coordinate consistency. Probe geometry directly rather than inferring it only from image quality.

12 · Product and system tradeoffs

Geometric state consumes memory, bandwidth, and synchronization budget. Dense 3D grids grow rapidly with volume and resolution. Neural fields save storage but turn every collision or render into many network queries. Dynamic primitives render quickly but require lifecycle management. Object models make control cheap but need perception and a fallback for unmodeled matter.

A real-time product often separates rates. Camera and proprioception arrive quickly; local tracking and collision state update at control frequency; global map optimization and dense appearance update more slowly. The planner reads an immutable, timestamped snapshot so geometry does not change midway through a query. GPU rendering, dynamics rollout, and mapping contend for the same accelerator, so a beautiful sensor model can directly reduce the number of action candidates explored.

Accuracy must be allocated by risk. A game generator may tolerate geometry that changes subtly if frames are entertaining. Augmented reality needs stable surfaces under camera motion. A warehouse robot needs conservative free space and accurate contact near the gripper, but not photorealistic texture. An autonomous vehicle needs low-latency occupancy and calibrated uncertainty at long range. These are different Pareto points, not a single leaderboard.

Coordinate and calibration contracts also shape maintenance. Record frame names, transform provenance, timestamps, sensor versions, and uncertainty. A silent calibration shift can corrupt months of learned dynamics. Online monitoring should include reprojection residuals on static landmarks, map consistency, and action-response residuals, not only end-task success.

Rendering quality can be allocated selectively. Maintain high geometric precision near predicted contact and coarse geometry in distant free space. Decode RGB only for candidate trajectories a human or a visual policy must inspect. Cache static scene contributions while updating dynamic layers. These choices preserve the semantic contract of one world state while avoiding uniform maximum detail.

Out-of-distribution geometry needs an escape path. A rigid-object model encountering a hanging cable should not confidently force it into a mug-like pose. Track residual sensor error, allow an “unknown deformable region,” and fall back to conservative occupancy. The system can gather a better view or slow down rather than invent a precise but wrong surface. Explicit geometry is valuable partly because its violations—penetration, reprojection error, unexplained depth—are measurable at runtime.

Finally, version state interfaces. If a mapping update changes voxel size, coordinate origin, or field semantics, recorded transitions and planner thresholds may no longer be compatible. Treat representations like production APIs: specify units, frames, update frequency, uncertainty meaning, and query guarantees, then regression-test them on static-camera, moving-camera, and controlled-intervention sequences.

13 · Bridge: geometry and video solve complementary problems

Geometry builds in spatial consistency and makes planning queries explicit, but maintaining it can be expensive and it does not explain every visual or physical factor. Internet-scale video offers abundant trajectories without 3D labels. Lesson 12 studies models that compress and generate those trajectories directly. The key comparison will not be “explicit versus learned.” It will be whether the resulting state preserves identity, responds correctly to action, represents multiple futures, and supports the decisions we need.

Takeaway
A geometric world model is an evolving belief in stable coordinates, plus a sensor-pose-conditioned observation model. Explicit geometry earns its cost when it makes high-value visibility, collision, contact, or viewpoint queries reliable. It still needs correspondence through time, uncertain hidden physics, and action-conditioned evaluation; renderability alone is not controllability.

Interview prompts

  • Why separate camera pose from world state? Because sensor motion changes pixels without changing the environment. The split creates a stable belief and allows one world state to predict observations from many viewpoints.
  • When does a 3D reconstruction become a world model? When it persists through time, updates from observations, advances under actions, represents uncertainty, and supports prediction beyond replaying captured views.
  • Why is a time-conditioned dynamic NeRF not automatically controllable? It can interpolate what occurred at each recorded time without learning a transition conditioned on alternative interventions.
  • What does differentiable rendering provide? A trainable observation likelihood from hidden geometry, appearance, and sensor pose to measurements, allowing 2D sensor residuals to correct 3D state.
  • How do you choose voxels, fields, primitives, or objects? Compare native query cost, update rate, memory, required precision, and representation mismatch. Collision favors grids or meshes; view synthesis favors fields or primitives; entity control favors objects.
  • What is the main challenge in going from 3D to 4D? Correspondence: deciding what persisted and how it moved, deformed, appeared, disappeared, split, or merged.
  • What important variables does geometry omit? Mass, friction, compliance, intent, affordance, ownership, and other hidden physical or semantic factors. Hybrid latent state and uncertainty are still required.
  • How would you distinguish renderer failure from dynamics failure? Test held-out views of a fixed state for the renderer, and matched initial states with controlled alternative actions for dynamics; probe depth and state trajectories separately from RGB.
