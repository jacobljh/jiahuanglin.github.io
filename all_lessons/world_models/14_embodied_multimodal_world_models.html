Embodied multimodal worlds

An embodied model must bind words and pixels to one persistent world, fuse body state and touch with vision, and predict contact-rich consequences quickly enough to close a control loop.

The dependency
Lesson 13 turned a video model into a playable environment you can steer — but a controllable movie still is not a body. We now add proprioception, force, touch, audio, language goals, multiple sensor clocks, and body-relative action. The question changes from “what video looks plausible next?” to “what will this particular body experience if it takes this action for this goal?”

1 · Begin with the decision, not the sensors

From first principles, a modality is useful only when it reduces uncertainty about a variable that changes a decision. More cameras are not automatically more intelligence. Begin with the action, its deadline, and the failure cost; then ask which hidden facts determine the consequence. The resulting belief state should be a sufficient statistic: once the agent has that state, raw history should add little information needed to predict task-relevant futures.

We will use one running product example through the final three lessons: Countertop Assistant, a mobile manipulator asked, “Put the red ceramic mug on the serving tray.” The mug may be partly hidden behind a plate, the counter may be wet, a person may cross the arm’s path, and the robot’s gripper has a small camera, joint encoders, wrist force/torque, tactile pads, and microphones. Success means the correct mug reaches the tray upright within 20 seconds, without a spill, collision, or excessive grip force.

The decision immediately reveals hidden state. The robot needs object identity and pose, but also mug occupancy, mass, friction, whether its fingers have made stable contact, human motion, its own joint configuration, actuator delay, and the meaning of “serving tray.” RGB directly reveals only some of these. A world model therefore maintains a distribution over state rather than a single visual scene:

history hₜ = {language g, observations o₁:ₜ¹…ᴹ, executed actions a₁:ₜ₋₁, timestamps} belief bₜ(s) = p(sₜ = s | hₜ) prediction p(oₜ₊₁, rₜ, cₜ, sₜ₊₁ | bₜ, aₜ)

Here r is task progress and c is safety cost. This factorization is more than notation. It says that sensing corrects belief, action changes the world, and the model must predict both useful and unsafe consequences. If the system instead concatenates sensor features and emits an action, it may be a strong policy, but it has not necessarily learned an inspectable predictive world.

2 · Each modality resolves a different ambiguity

Do not begin by memorizing a sensor catalog. Begin with a hidden fact that can change the action, ask why the current evidence cannot settle it, and only then choose the next measurement. For Countertop Assistant, that reasoning unfolds in five steps:

  1. First establish the external scene with RGB and depth. These streams directly expose appearance, external geometry, and motion, usually at 15–60 Hz. From that evidence the model can reduce uncertainty about object identity, pose, free space, and human location. But an image of fingers around a mug still cannot prove that force is actually being transferred.
  2. Then locate the robot from inside its own body. Proprioception reports joint angle, velocity, and motor effort at roughly 100–1000 Hz. It resolves body configuration even when the arm is occluded or visually blurred. This matters because the same camera image can correspond to different joint configurations, and those configurations permit different safe motions.
  3. At contact, replace visual inference with local physical evidence. Force and touch measure contact, pressure pattern, shear, and slip at about 100–2000 Hz. Those signals reduce uncertainty about friction, compliance, grasp stability, and incipient collision. The higher rate is not decorative: slip can become unrecoverable between two video frames.
  4. Listen for events the camera cannot see. Audio directly carries impacts, motor sounds, speech, and distant events. Its waveform is typically sampled at 16–48 kHz and then summarized over windows. It can reveal both an event outside the field of view and a material response, such as the difference between a dull plastic tap and a sharp ceramic impact.
  5. Finally, let language specify what the physical evidence is for. Language supplies goals, names, constraints, and procedures. It is event driven rather than sampled on a fixed high-rate clock. It reduces ambiguity about which outcome matters and which prior knowledge applies, but it does not measure the mug's present pose or prove that a grasp is stable.

These signals are complementary, not interchangeable. Vision can show that fingers surround a mug, while tactile shear reveals that it is sliding. Joint encoders can localize a visually occluded elbow. Audio can report a ceramic impact outside the camera view. Language can identify which of two geometrically identical red vessels the user calls “the mug,” but language cannot measure its current pose. Good fusion lets evidence compete: if vision predicts a grasp while touch reports no contact, belief in “grasped” must fall.

A practical identifiability test
Remove one modality at a time and name the hidden variable that becomes unidentifiable. Then inject a contradiction. If the action does not change when force or proprioception strongly contradicts vision, the architecture is probably concatenating features rather than maintaining a fused belief.

3 · Put every event on a physical timeline

Multimodal fusion is first a timing problem. A sensor packet’s arrival time is not when the world was measured. A camera integrates light over an exposure interval; audio represents a window; a force sample may be almost instantaneous; an action command passes through networking, a controller, and actuator dynamics before motion begins. Let τ denote physical event time, tᵐ a modality clock, δᵐ clock offset, and ℓᵐ transport/preprocessing latency. A simple mapping is:

τᵐ = tᵐ − δᵐ − ℓᵐ observation event time τᵃ,executed = tᵃ,command + actuator delay + response dynamics aligned input at τ = {interpolate continuous state, pool events over support, mark missingness}

Offsets and delays should be calibrated, stored, and sometimes estimated online. Continuous proprioception can be interpolated to a query time. An image should retain exposure start and end. Tactile events can be pooled over a short causal window. Language persists as goal context rather than being repeated as though it were a new observation. Never use a future sensor sample when constructing a state intended for real-time deployment; that creates silent look-ahead leakage.

Worked timing example

Suppose Countertop Assistant’s overhead camera runs at 30 Hz with 10 ms exposure and 35 ms processing delay. Proprioception arrives at 200 Hz after 4 ms, tactile pressure at 1 kHz after 2 ms, action commands at 50 Hz, and the gripper begins responding 18 ms after a command. At wall-clock 1.200 s, a camera frame arrives. Its exposure midpoint describes physical time 1.200 − 0.035 − 0.005 = 1.160 s. The freshest proprioceptive packet describes about 1.196 s, not 1.160 s. Naively concatenating them places the body 36 ms ahead of the image.

During a 0.8 m/s wrist motion, 36 ms corresponds to 0.8 × 0.036 = 0.0288 m, almost 2.9 cm—far larger than a few-millimeter grasp tolerance. The correct training record queries joint state at 1.160 s, pairs the image with actions actually executed before that time, and retains newer tactile/proprioceptive data for the next belief correction. If the command was sent at 1.150 s but execution begins at 1.168 s, the 1.160 s frame is evidence from before the intervention. Labeling it post-action teaches reversed causality.

A robust pipeline tests alignment with known impulses: flash an LED when issuing a motion, tap the force sensor in camera view, or correlate motor acceleration with encoder velocity. Plot cross-correlations by session and embodiment version. If the estimated peak drifts, fix the data before tuning the model.

4 · Ground language in persistent entities and relations

“Put the red mug behind the plate on the tray” contains a referent, spatial relation, destination, and desired transition. The model must bind “red mug” to an object track that survives occlusion, “behind” to a coordinate frame, “tray” to a receptacle with capacity, and “put” to a controlled contact sequence. Grounding is not a one-frame segmentation label; it is a durable correspondence between symbols and changing world state.

A useful hierarchy separates four coupled layers:

  1. Semantic task state: goal predicates such as on(mug, tray), constraints, and object roles.
  2. Persistent scene state: object identity, 3D pose distributions, occupancy, articulated relations, and human tracks.
  3. Interaction state: grasp mode, contact graph, estimated mass/friction, slip probability, and local geometry.
  4. Body state: joints, velocities, controller mode, saturation, and recent executed action.

Updates should flow both ways. Pixels and touch update object/contact state; semantic identity tells the visual system which occluded track deserves memory; the goal changes which prediction errors matter. However, language should not overwrite physical evidence. If a user calls a steel cup “the ceramic mug,” the referent can be grounded while material belief remains uncertain.

Spatial language also needs an explicit coordinate frame. “Behind the plate” may mean farther from the user, farther from the robot, or behind in the plate’s intrinsic orientation. Store transforms among camera, gripper, robot base, map, object, and human-centric frames with timestamp and covariance. Compose them rather than learning a separate opaque relation for every camera. A 3D point measured in camera coordinates becomes a base-frame point through pᵇ = Tᵇ꜀pᶜ; uncertainty in depth and extrinsic calibration must propagate into the grasp distribution. Test equivariance by moving the camera while holding the scene fixed: world-frame belief should remain stable even though pixels change. If predicted actions rotate incorrectly with the camera, the model has confused image coordinates with physical coordinates.

5 · Fusion architecture is a choice about conditional dependence

Early fusion converts all modalities to tokens and applies shared attention. It can discover rich cross-modal interactions, but high-rate signals dominate token count and missing sensors can destabilize it. Late fusion builds a state per modality and combines distributions or decisions. It is modular and debuggable but may miss fine timing, such as the relation between a tactile spike and a particular image edge. Cross-attention fusion keeps specialized encoders and lets a belief query attend to timestamped modality memories. This is often a useful middle ground.

From a probabilistic view, independent sensor evidence would update a prior as p(s|o¹,…,oᴹ) ∝ p(s)∏ₘp(oᵐ|s). Real sensors are not conditionally independent—two cameras share lighting errors, and force and motor current share actuator effects—so multiplying confident experts can become overconfident. Learned fusion needs correlated-noise training, sensor dropout, reliability indicators, and calibration by modality combination. It should distinguish “no tactile contact” from “tactile sensor unavailable.”

For Countertop Assistant, visual/depth tokens update object and free-space slots at 15 Hz; proprioception updates body state at 200 Hz; tactile summaries update a contact latent at 500 Hz; and the language goal initializes semantic predicates. A recurrent belief core consumes timestamped deltas rather than forcing every stream onto one enormous dense grid. During a camera dropout, body and contact beliefs still evolve, but object-pose uncertainty expands.

Train the same combinations the product will face. Random modality dropout teaches graceful degradation, but independent random masks are too easy: real failures occur in bursts and are correlated with the event, such as a gripper camera becoming occluded exactly at contact. Simulate long outages, saturation, stale packets, calibration jumps, and mutually inconsistent sensors. Add an auxiliary objective that predicts one modality from the others, but do not require perfect cross-modal reconstruction; touch contains local friction information that distant RGB genuinely cannot recover. The desired output is a broader posterior, not a hallucinated tactile signal. Evaluate every sensor subset as its own operating regime and define which subsets permit normal operation, reduced speed, or mandatory stop.

6 · Match hierarchy to physical bandwidth

No single model rate fits embodiment. Human intent and room layout change slowly; a reaching motion evolves over tenths of seconds; slip can become irreversible within milliseconds. The naive idea is to run one large model for everything. It fails in both directions: a 2 Hz foundation model cannot safely close a 1 kHz torque loop, while a 1 kHz language-conditioned transformer would spend nearly all its computation reconsidering a goal that has not changed.

The derived requirement is a ladder whose abstraction decreases as its update rate increases. Follow one command—“put the mug on the tray”—down that ladder:

  1. Task layer: decide the phase. At about 0.2–2 Hz over tens of seconds, this layer selects “approach, grasp, carry, place” and chooses recovery when a phase fails. Language, predicates, and object relations are the right representation because the question is semantic: what subgoal should happen next?
  2. World-prediction layer: compare consequential futures. At roughly 5–20 Hz over a 1–5 second horizon, it branches action chunks and compares their human and object futures. Objects, geometry, and a stochastic latent are appropriate because uncertainty about occlusion, motion, and contact must affect the comparison.
  3. Motion layer: turn the chosen future into a trackable target. At 50–200 Hz over 50–500 ms, it tracks collision-free pose and force targets. It reasons with robot state, a local signed-distance representation, and contact mode because small geometric deviations now matter more than linguistic detail.
  4. Reflex layer: prevent irreversible local failure. At 500–2000 Hz over milliseconds, verified local control state limits torque, arrests slip, and triggers an emergency stop. It does not wait for a new semantic plan: its physical deadline is shorter than that plan's inference time.

This gives a concrete implementation consequence: information flowing downward becomes more specific—subgoal, then action chunk, then pose/force target, then actuator limit—while evidence flowing upward reports execution, violation, and changed belief. A useful diagnostic is to inject a disturbance at each timescale. A changed instruction should alter the task layer; an approaching person should alter the world-prediction or motion layer; a sudden tactile slip should trigger the reflex before a new high-level plan is available.

The layers exchange contracts, not raw thought. The slow model proposes a grasp pose, force range, expected contact time, and uncertainty. The fast controller reports execution and deviations. When measured force violates the contract, the reflex acts immediately and the world model later revises its belief. Action chunks reduce planning cost, but an interrupt channel must remain available; otherwise a 500 ms open-loop chunk ignores a person entering the workspace.

7 · Contact makes hidden physics decision-critical

At lift time, two RGB images may be indistinguishable even though one grasp is safe and the other will slip. A simple friction condition illustrates why. If the mug has mass m, each of two opposing fingers supplies normal force N, and friction coefficient is μ, a quasi-static vertical lift requires 2μN ≥ mg. For a 0.45 kg mug and μ = 0.35, minimum force per finger is N ≥ (0.45×9.81)/(2×0.35) = 6.31 N. If water lowers friction to 0.18, the requirement becomes 12.26 N, perhaps above the safe pressure for a fragile mug.

Vision may provide a prior over material and wetness, while tactile shear updates it during a small test lift. Suppose belief assigns 20% probability to low friction. A micro-lift produces a slip signature that occurs with probability 0.8 under low friction but 0.1 under normal friction. Bayes’ rule gives:

P(low friction | slip signal) = (0.8 × 0.20) / (0.8 × 0.20 + 0.1 × 0.80) = 0.16 / 0.24 = 0.667

The observation raises low-friction belief from 20% to 66.7%. A rational agent now lowers acceleration, replans the contact, or asks for help rather than merely squeezing harder. This is active perception: choose a low-cost action because its observation separates hypotheses. Contact prediction should include discrete modes—free, touching, sticking, sliding—and continuous state inside each mode. Averaging across modes invents physically meaningless “half contact.”

8 · A VLA policy and a world model solve different bottlenecks

A vision–language–action policy amortizes (observation, instruction) → action into one forward pass. It is fast and learns human-like behavior from demonstrations. A world model predicts consequences under alternative actions and can support planning, explanation, and goal changes. Neither universally dominates.

Hybrid hierarchy

In our product, the VLA might propose three familiar grasp trajectories. The world model branches them, predicts mug stability and human collision probability, and selects one under a risk constraint. The motion controller tracks it, while a tactile reflex stops slip. This uses expensive prediction where alternatives matter rather than at every motor tick.

9 · Simulation is a prior; deployment is continual belief correction

Simulation can vary mass, friction, texture, lighting, camera pose, delay, and wear, but it cannot enumerate the real world. Domain randomization broadens support; system identification estimates explicit parameters; residual models learn the gap between simulated and observed transitions. All three should lead to an online procedure: detect mismatch, widen uncertainty, shorten the trusted horizon, choose information-gathering actions, and fall back outside the calibrated envelope.

The Synthetic Vision Data track develops the complementary data pipeline: branch controlled engine interventions from matched states, render the deployed sensor chain rather than ideal RGB, and use real failure slices to decide what the simulator must vary next.

Useful diagnostics are causal and layer-specific. Replay the same executed action with sensors masked one at a time. Plot prediction error against time offset. Check whether object identity survives occlusion. Compare predicted and actual contact time. Bucket calibration by material, speed, lighting, and embodiment revision. Inspect attention only as a clue, never proof of grounding. Most importantly, intervene: change grip force while holding visual context similar and verify that predicted slip changes in the correct direction.

10 · Embodied models should choose what to sense

When uncertainty blocks action, the agent need not passively wait. It can move the camera, rotate the object, tap a surface, close the fingers gently, ask a question, or pause for an occluder to pass. These are epistemic actions: their immediate task reward may be low, but their observations improve later decisions. Embodiment turns perception into control.

The value of information can be written as the difference between the best expected decision after an observation and the best decision under current belief, minus sensing cost:

VOI(aˢᵉⁿˢᵉ) = Eₒ[maxₐ E[U | b updated by o]] − maxₐ E[U | b] − cost(aˢᵉⁿˢᵉ)

Suppose Countertop Assistant assigns 40% probability that the visible red object is the requested mug and 60% that it is a similar container. Grasping immediately yields utility +10 if correct and −8 if wrong, so its expected utility is 0.4×10 + 0.6×(−8) = −0.8. A 0.5-second viewpoint change costs 1 utility unit and reveals identity with 95% accuracy. After it, the robot acts only when the observation says “mug.” The approximate expected action utility is 0.4×0.95×10 + 0.6×0.05×(−8) = 3.56; subtracting sensing cost gives 2.56. The viewpoint action is rational even though it makes no direct task progress.

Information gain alone is not enough. A perfect close-up might require moving through a person’s space, while a weaker side view is safe. The world model should predict both how an action changes physical state and which observation it will produce. Evaluate active sensing by final task risk and efficiency, not by entropy reduction in isolation. Also test whether the model invents information: after a camera move that still leaves the mug occluded, uncertainty should remain high rather than collapse because the action usually helps in training.

11 · Design checklist for an embodied model

  1. State the decision, control deadline, horizon, and unacceptable outcomes.
  2. List hidden variables that change consequences; map each to evidence and remaining ambiguity.
  3. Define clocks, exposure windows, action execution semantics, coordinate frames, and calibration versions.
  4. Choose persistent semantic, spatial, interaction, and body state; specify how missingness changes uncertainty.
  5. Assign each prediction/control responsibility to a physical rate and an interrupt path.
  6. Train with cross-modal contradictions, dropouts, delays, interventions, and rare contact transitions.
  7. Measure modality ablations, timing sensitivity, causal action effects, uncertainty calibration, and closed-loop recovery.

Where this points next

Embodied intelligence turns one episode into a dense, asynchronous trajectory: multiple cameras, high-rate proprioception and touch, actions, language, calibration, and safety labels. Resolution × views × frame rate × context already produces many tokens; planning multiplies inference by candidates, horizon, refinement, and uncertainty samples. Lesson 15 follows this exact Countertop Assistant record through data curation, tokenization, distributed training, rollout accounting, and a latency-constrained service.

Takeaway
An embodied world model is a time-aligned belief over one persistent, actionable world. Ground language to durable entities, let modalities resolve distinct hidden variables, preserve the difference between commanded and executed action, and place each layer at the bandwidth its physics requires. The system is intelligent only when contradictory evidence changes belief and action.

Interview prompts

  • Why start from the decision instead of listing sensors? The required action, horizon, deadline, and failure cost determine which hidden variables matter; sensors are valuable only insofar as they reduce uncertainty about those variables.
  • What ambiguity does proprioception remove? It reveals body configuration and motion when external vision is occluded, delayed, or geometrically ambiguous, and distinguishes environmental motion from self-motion.
  • Why can timestamp alignment be more important than model size? A 36 ms mismatch at 0.8 m/s creates almost 3 cm of false geometry and can reverse action–effect order, so a larger model simply fits corrupted causality.
  • What makes language grounding persistent? A phrase binds to an object track, relation, coordinate frame, and goal predicate that survive occlusion and are revised by later physical evidence.
  • When would you prefer cross-attention over early fusion? When modalities have different rates and specialized structure, and you want a shared belief to query timestamped memories without making high-rate signals dominate one token stream.
  • Why use a control hierarchy? Semantic planning changes slowly while contact reflexes need millisecond response; layered contracts preserve rich reasoning without missing physical deadlines.
  • How can a VLA and world model cooperate? The VLA cheaply proposes in-distribution action chunks, the world model predicts and compares their consequences, and fast verified controllers enforce local safety.
  • What does sim-to-real uncertainty do operationally? It shortens the trusted horizon, favors information gathering or conservative actions, triggers fallback, and selects real trajectories that should be added to training.
