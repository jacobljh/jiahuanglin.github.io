Partial observability and belief state

A sensor reading does not identify one world. Derive a distribution over possible states, propagate it through actions, correct it with evidence, and preserve the ambiguity that changes decisions.

1 · Current observation fails as current state

A state is Markov when it contains all information from the past that matters for predicting the next state. Formally, once st and action at are known, adding older history should not change the next-state distribution:

p(st+1 | st, at, history) = p(st+1 | st, at)

This property generally does not hold for the current image. When a ball passes behind a wall, the visible frame contains no ball pixels. Its earlier position and velocity still predict when and where it will emerge. Two histories ending in the same “empty wall” observation have different futures. Mapping both observations to the same frame-only representation is state aliasing: physically or decisionally different states receive the same internal label.

Aliasing comes from several sources:

  • Occlusion: objects continue to exist when another surface blocks the line of sight.
  • Projection: multiple 3D scenes can produce the same 2D image. The 3D Vision track studies these geometric ambiguities.
  • Hidden derivatives: one position does not reveal velocity or acceleration.
  • Unobserved properties: mass, friction, charge, intent, and articulation may only become evident through interaction.
  • Sensor limitations: noise, finite resolution, exposure, missing modalities, and asynchronous measurements erase information.

The formal environment is a partially observable Markov decision process, or POMDP. The physical process can be Markov even while the agent’s observation process is not:

p(ot+1 | ot, at, history) ≠ p(ot+1 | ot, at)

The inequality says older evidence can change what the next observation means. It does not say the image is useless. It says an image must be combined with remembered evidence and a model of how the world evolves.

2 · A belief is the agent’s information state

If the true state cannot be read, the mathematically complete answer is a posterior distribution over what it could be:

bt(s) = p(st = s | o1:t, a1:t−1)

Read this from right to left. The conditioning information includes every observation through the present and every action already executed. The left side, bt(s), assigns probability to each candidate current state. It is not uncertainty about a fixed neural output; it is the agent’s current information about the environment.

A belief can be sufficient even though no single physical hypothesis is known. Two full histories that induce the same posterior over current state lead to the same future distribution under the same action sequence, provided the environment model is correct. The belief therefore converts a partially observed problem into a fully observed problem in belief space. The controller’s “state” is now a distribution.

This distinguishes two kinds of uncertainty. Aleatoric uncertainty is unresolved ambiguity or randomness that remains even with the current model: the object may truly be behind either door. Epistemic uncertainty is lack of knowledge about the model itself: perhaps the transition behavior in this warehouse region was barely represented in training. Both affect safe decisions, but they demand different remedies. More observations may resolve hidden location; more diverse data or an ensemble may be needed to expose model ignorance.

3 · Derive filtering as predict, then correct

Suppose the agent has belief bt−1 after processing the previous observation. To incorporate an executed action and a new observation, it performs two logically separate operations.

Step A: predict through the transition

b̄t(s′) = ∑s T(s′ | s, at−1) bt−1(s)

The bar over b means “prior before seeing the new observation.” For each old state s, multiply how much probability the old belief assigned it by the probability that action at−1 moves it to candidate new state s′. Sum over all old states. For continuous states, the sum becomes an integral. Prediction usually spreads uncertainty because several next states may follow each current hypothesis.

Step B: correct with the new observation

bt(s′) = η · O(ot | s′) · b̄t(s′)

O(ot | s′) is the likelihood: how compatible the received observation is with candidate state s′. Multiplication increases states that explain the evidence and decreases states that do not. η is a normalization constant chosen so the probabilities sum to one. Correction often concentrates uncertainty, but a noisy or ambiguous observation may barely change it.

The order matters. The action happened before the new measurement, so belief must first move according to that action. Correcting yesterday’s state with today’s observation and only then transitioning confuses time indices. This small bookkeeping issue becomes a major implementation bug when sensors have delay or actions span different rates.

4 · Running example: which aisle contains the forklift?

A robot approaches two blind aisles, left (L) and right (R). A forklift occupies one aisle. Before looking, routing history gives prior P(L)=0.60 and P(R)=0.40. A noisy acoustic sensor reports the correct aisle 80% of the time. Thus P(report L | L)=0.8 and P(report L | R)=0.2.

First report: left

Unnormalized left probability is prior × likelihood: 0.60 × 0.80 = 0.48. Unnormalized right probability is 0.40 × 0.20 = 0.08. Their sum is 0.56, so normalization gives:

P(L | report L) = 0.48 / 0.56 = 0.857;    P(R | report L) = 0.143

The report raises left belief from 60% to about 86%, but does not establish certainty because the sensor sometimes lies.

Second independent report: left again

Use the previous posterior as the new prior. The left weight becomes 0.857 × 0.8 = 0.686; the right weight becomes 0.143 × 0.2 = 0.0286. Normalize:

P(L | L,L) = 0.686 / (0.686 + 0.0286) ≈ 0.96

Repeated independent evidence accumulates. An equivalent odds view is even cleaner: prior odds are 0.6 / 0.4 = 1.5. Each left report has likelihood ratio 0.8 / 0.2 = 4. Two reports produce posterior odds 1.5 × 4 × 4 = 24, or probability 24 / 25 = 0.96.

Prediction: the forklift can switch aisles

Now the camera is blocked for one time step. Suppose the forklift stays in its aisle with probability 0.9 and switches with probability 0.1. Prediction produces:

P(L next) = 0.96 × 0.9 + 0.04 × 0.1 = 0.868

Uncertainty increases without evidence: left probability falls from 0.96 to 0.868 because motion could have switched the hidden state. This is not “model confusion”; it is the correct consequence of stochastic transition.

Correction: a contradictory right report

The next acoustic report says right. For left, that report has likelihood 0.2; for right, likelihood 0.8. The unnormalized weights are 0.868 × 0.2 = 0.1736 and 0.132 × 0.8 = 0.1056. Normalize them:

P(L | L,L, motion, R) = 0.1736 / 0.2792 ≈ 0.622

The correct belief is still slightly left. One noisy contradiction should not erase two prior consistent reports, yet it should reduce confidence sharply. A rule that stores only the latest report flips to right; a rule that permanently commits after two reports stays at 96% left. Bayesian filtering makes the evidence–dynamics tradeoff explicit.

Decision consequence

If entering an occupied aisle costs 100 and waiting for a clear visual check costs 8, immediately taking the right aisle has expected collision cost P(R) × 100 = 0.378 × 100 = 37.8. Entering left costs 62.2. Waiting costs 8, so information gathering is rational. If waiting instead cost 50, taking right would have lower expected cost. The belief does not choose by itself; it provides the probabilities the objective needs.

5 · Exact beliefs become approximations in real systems

The two-aisle belief has only two numbers. A visual world can contain continuous poses, velocities, identities, shapes, intentions, and maps. Enumerating every possible state is impossible. Practical systems approximate the posterior.

There is no useful rule that says “neural is better” or “classical is better.” Begin instead with the shape of ambiguity you must preserve, then choose the cheapest approximation that can preserve it:

  1. Start with one smooth cloud. If state evolves linearly and all relevant noise is Gaussian, a Kalman filter needs only a mean vector and covariance matrix. Under those assumptions it is exact, efficient, and interpretable: the mean says where the cloud is centered and the covariance says how it spreads and tilts. The naive extension is to use this single cloud even when the forklift could occupy two separated aisles. Its mean then lies between the aisles, so the representation destroys the very alternatives the decision needs.
  2. If the cloud bends moderately, propagate a local approximation. Extended and unscented filters still carry a mean and covariance, but move them through nonlinear dynamics using a local linearization or selected sigma points. This avoids enumerating continuous state and works for moderate nonlinearities. It fails when a transition folds one cloud into sharp or separated modes: a few local summaries cannot preserve topology they were never designed to represent.
  3. If the belief truly branches, keep multiple concrete hypotheses. A particle filter represents the posterior as weighted state samples. Particles can follow nonlinear motion and occupy several modes at once, so “left aisle” and “right aisle” remain physically coherent possibilities rather than averaging into a wall. The price follows directly: high-dimensional worlds require many particles, and resampling can accidentally delete a rare, low-weight mode that later becomes safety-critical.
  4. If hand-written state is the bottleneck, learn how history is compressed. An RNN, GRU, or LSTM folds observations and actions into a deterministic hidden vector. It can be fast and adaptively retain evidence across long histories. But a deterministic vector does not automatically expose a probability distribution. Uncertainty may be only implicit, inaccessible to the controller, or badly calibrated even when predictions are accurate on average.
  5. If learned memory must branch, add explicit stochastic state. A stochastic state-space model combines deterministic memory for temporal context with random latent variables for sampled alternatives. This creates distinct places to store “what the history says” and “which unresolved future occurs.” Yet the chosen posterior family still limits which ambiguities fit, and the training objective can collapse modes until the random variable is ignored.
  6. If the decisive clue may be far back in a rich history, retrieve it by content. Transformer memory stores tokens in a finite context or persistent cache and can attend directly to the past evidence relevant now. That flexibility does not create free memory: context length, compute, positional organization, and the removal or correction of stale tokens become concrete system constraints.

The chain above turns representation choice into a diagnosis. One approximately Gaussian mode suggests mean and covariance; curved but still local uncertainty suggests nonlinear Gaussian filtering; separated hypotheses require samples, mixtures, or stochastic latents; long learned histories require recurrence or retrieval. A more expressive mechanism is justified only when the simpler one destroys a distinction that changes a future decision.

A learned architecture does not have to reproduce Bayes’ rule symbol by symbol. It must nevertheless implement the same information flow. Before a new observation, advance memory using the previous state and action. After the observation arrives, compare prediction with evidence and revise. In recurrent state-space models these are often called the prior and posterior pathways. Training can make their distributions agree when observations are predictable while allowing the posterior to correct surprises.

Diagnostic before architecture
Construct the smallest controlled case with two separated, decision-relevant hypotheses. If the internal state averages them, the posterior approximation is too restrictive. If it preserves both but assigns unjustified confidence, the inference or calibration is wrong. If it begins correctly and loses the rare branch over time, inspect transition noise, resampling, memory update, and training mode coverage.

6 · A point estimate can be a physically impossible average

Suppose the forklift is equally likely to be 4 m down the left aisle or 4 m down the right aisle. Averaging coordinates might place it in the wall between aisles, a state with nearly zero probability. This is the central danger of compressing a multimodal posterior to a mean. Mean-squared error encourages exactly such averages because the conditional mean minimizes expected squared loss.

Different downstream questions require different summaries:

  • The mean can be appropriate for nearly Gaussian tracking and quadratic costs.
  • The maximum-probability hypothesis gives one coherent world but discards alternatives.
  • Multiple modes or particles preserve mutually exclusive futures for risk-sensitive planning.
  • Quantiles or tail probabilities directly support safety constraints such as “collision chance below 0.1%.”
False certainty
A sharp decoder output is not evidence of a sharp posterior. A generator can select the most likely image while hiding discarded hypotheses. Evaluate calibration: among events predicted with 70% probability, about 70% should occur. Slice calibration by horizon, occlusion, class, action, and distribution shift; aggregate calibration can hide the cases that matter.

7 · Memory length is not memory quality

Keeping 10,000 tokens does not guarantee the right evidence survives. A belief representation needs selective persistence: remember a briefly observed pedestrian behind a bus, but forget sensor noise that no longer changes any future. It also needs selective revision: do not cling to a stale object pose after strong contradictory evidence.

Useful training and evaluation probes include:

  1. Occlusion duration sweep: measure state and outcome error as an object remains invisible longer.
  2. Contradictory evidence: verify confidence falls before the estimate flips.
  3. Action-conditioned update: make sure executed ego motion shifts predicted geometry before the next image arrives.
  4. Distractor insertion: add visually salient but dynamically irrelevant events and test whether memory of the target persists.
  5. Information probe: decode hidden position, velocity, identity, and uncertainty from the frozen state at each time.

If a hidden vector supports accurate prediction but a simple probe cannot read its uncertainty, the planner may still fail to use that uncertainty. Representation and interface must be designed together.

8 · Active perception: actions can buy information

In a fully observed problem, actions are evaluated for how they change physical outcomes. Under partial observation, actions can also change what becomes knowable. A robot can move its camera around an occluder, slow down to gain more frames, tap an object to estimate mass, illuminate a dark area, or choose a question that separates two hypotheses.

The value of information is not uncertainty reduction for its own sake. It is the improvement in expected decision quality after receiving the possible observation, minus the cost and risk of acquiring it. In the aisle example, waiting was worth at most the difference between the best immediate expected cost (37.8) and the expected cost after the check. If the perfect check lets the robot always choose the empty aisle at zero collision cost, information is worth up to 37.8 units; an 8-unit wait is worthwhile, a 50-unit wait is not.

This creates a second loop: use the current belief to choose an informative action, receive evidence, update the belief, then choose the task action. A world model that buries ambiguity inside visually varied samples but cannot attach probabilities or expected consequences makes this loop difficult to optimize.

9 · Sensor fusion requires more than multiplying scores

Multiple sensors can reduce ambiguity because they constrain different aspects of state. Cameras provide dense appearance and geometry cues but degrade under glare or darkness. Radar measures range and relative velocity with different resolution. Proprioception measures the agent’s own motion. Fusion should occur in a common time and coordinate system, and each measurement should influence belief according to its reliability.

Under a conditional-independence assumption, two observations o¹ and o² satisfy p(o¹,o² | s)=p(o¹ | s)p(o² | s), so their likelihoods can be multiplied during correction. But this assumption is often false. Two camera detectors may share training data and fail together in fog. Two location estimates may both already incorporate the same GPS reading. Treating correlated evidence as independent counts the same information twice and produces an overconfident posterior.

A simple numerical example shows the effect. Start with 50/50 odds for a pedestrian crossing. A vision classifier and a “second” classifier each report cross with nominal likelihood ratio 4. If they were conditionally independent, posterior odds would be 1 × 4 × 4 = 16, giving probability 16/17 ≈ 94.1%. If both classifiers are copies driven by the same image features, the second report contains almost no new evidence; the justified probability remains closer to 4/5 = 80%. Correlation modeling is therefore safety-critical, not statistical polish.

Asynchronous sensing adds a temporal problem. Suppose a camera frame is 100 ms old while proprioception is current. Fusing both as if simultaneous places observed objects in the wrong ego frame. A consistent system timestamps observations, transforms them through estimated ego motion, updates the belief at the measurement time, and propagates forward again. The same principle applies to delayed network messages and rolling-shutter cameras.

Missing sensors should widen belief rather than be replaced by a falsely certain default. Modality dropout during training, explicit validity masks, sensor-specific likelihoods, and tests with systematic failure help a learned filter degrade gracefully. Measure not only average accuracy with all sensors but calibration when one modality is absent, delayed, corrupted, or in conflict.

10 · Planning in belief space

A physical action changes the distribution of future physical states; an observation changes the distribution of beliefs. Consequently a policy under partial observability is conceptually a function of belief, at = π(bt), not merely of the most likely state. Two beliefs with the same mean but different variance may demand different speeds. Two multimodal beliefs with the same variance may demand different information-gathering maneuvers.

Exact belief-space planning is expensive because every candidate future action branches into possible states and possible observations. Practical systems approximate: optimize over sampled hypotheses, use certainty-equivalent control with a safety margin, learn a policy from belief features, or alternate task planning with targeted perception. The crucial interface is that the controller receives uncertainty in a form its objective can use.

11 · Design tradeoffs and failure cases

  • Overconfident transition: the model fails to spread belief during unobserved evolution. Remedy with stochastic dynamics, ensembles, and calibration under longer gaps.
  • Overreactive correction: one noisy observation erases strong historical evidence. Train with sensor corruption and expose likelihood/reliability where possible.
  • Underreactive correction: recurrent memory ignores surprising evidence and becomes stale. Test abrupt changes and use explicit update gates or posterior correction.
  • Mode collapse: several plausible states become one mean or one arbitrary sample. Evaluate mode coverage and decision tails, not only best-sample quality.
  • Filter divergence: a wrong model becomes confident, so new evidence appears impossible and is rejected. Monitor innovation—the difference between predicted and observed measurements—and maintain recovery mechanisms.
  • Confusing epistemic with aleatoric uncertainty: sampling more futures from one confident but wrong model does not reveal model ignorance. Ensembles, out-of-distribution indicators, and coverage tests address a different axis.
  • Ignoring sensor timing: applying delayed observations to the current state creates ghost motion. Track timestamps and, when necessary, update the historical state then replay transitions.

12 · Practical system and interview reasoning

For a partially observed design question, answer in a fixed sequence. First name hidden variables and aliasing examples. Second define what history arrives, including action and sensor timing. Third choose a belief representation capable of the expected posterior shape. Fourth specify predict and correct paths. Fifth say how uncertainty reaches the planner. Finally propose calibration, occlusion, contradiction, and downstream decision tests.

For example, a robot with cameras and proprioception might maintain deterministic recurrent memory for long-range context, stochastic object states for ambiguous positions and intentions, and an explicit ego-pose covariance from geometric localization. The predict step uses commanded and measured motion; the correct step fuses new image features. An occupancy head returns probabilities at future horizons. The planner optimizes expected progress subject to a collision-probability constraint, executes only the first short action segment, observes again, and replans. This is a coherent system because each component has an information role.

13 · Explicit takeaway

Takeaway
Partial observability makes inference over history unavoidable. The principled state for an agent is a belief: predict the old posterior through action-conditioned dynamics, correct it with the new observation likelihood, preserve competing hypotheses when they change decisions, and expose calibrated uncertainty to control. Memory capacity is not the goal; a sufficient, revisable information state is.

The exact posterior over an entire visual world is far too large. Lesson 03 therefore asks how to compress history into a learned latent while preserving the future distinctions and uncertainty the query contract requires.

Interview prompts

  • Why is video usually non-Markov in pixel space? Current pixels omit velocity, occluded entities, 3D causes, and hidden properties, so older evidence still changes the next-frame distribution.
  • What are the two Bayesian filtering steps? Predict the previous belief through the action-conditioned transition, then correct the resulting prior using the new observation likelihood.
  • What does the normalization constant do? It rescales likelihood-weighted prior masses so the posterior sums or integrates to one.
  • When is a particle belief preferable to a Gaussian? When the posterior is nonlinear, skewed, or has separated modes such as mutually exclusive locations.
  • Why can the posterior mean be invalid? For separated hypotheses it may lie in a low-probability or physically impossible state between the modes.
  • How do you test uncertainty calibration? Group predictions by reported probability and compare confidence with empirical frequency, sliced by horizon and difficult conditions.
  • What is an information-gathering action? An action chosen partly because its possible observations improve a later decision enough to justify its cost.
  • How are aleatoric and epistemic uncertainty different? Aleatoric uncertainty is unresolved outcome variability under the model; epistemic uncertainty reflects ignorance about the model and can shrink with better coverage.
