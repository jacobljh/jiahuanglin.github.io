One future is a lie

A present state does not select one inevitable future. A useful world model must preserve separated possibilities, assign honest probabilities, expose what it does not know, and let a decision rule act on the whole distribution.

Dependency recap
Lesson 04 built an RSSM with an imagination prior p(zt|ht) and an observation-informed posterior q(zt|ht,ot). The KL aligned their latent languages. But adding random noise does not automatically create meaningful uncertainty. We now derive why the future is multimodal, where uncertainty comes from, how probability should be trained and calibrated, and how a planner must consume it.

1 · Begin with the information available at prediction time

Let It=(o≤t,a<t) denote everything known now, and let at:t+H−1 be a candidate action sequence. The world model is asked for

p(yt+1:t+H | It, at:t+H−1)

The target y might be future latent states, images, object tracks, rewards, or constraint violations. The conditioning bar is crucial. A future can be deterministic given the complete physical microstate and still be uncertain given cameras with occlusions and finite resolution. Probability describes what is unresolved by the model’s information, not necessarily metaphysical randomness.

Several mechanisms create branches. A pedestrian’s goal is hidden. Friction is only approximately observed. Another agent chooses an action after seeing ours. Sensor noise leaves the initial state uncertain. A rare disturbance may occur. Model parameters may be unsupported in this region. All produce multiple plausible consequences, but they do not all demand the same response. Before choosing a distribution family, we must distinguish the structure and source of uncertainty.

2 · Derive why squared error invents an impossible world

Suppose a scalar future Y is −1 for “go left” and +1 for “go right,” each with probability one half. A deterministic predictor chooses a number m and minimizes expected squared error:

R(m) = E[(Y−m)²] = ½(−1−m)² + ½(1−m)² = 1 + m²

Differentiate: dR/dm=2m, so the optimum is m=0. Zero is not a noisy version of either future; it is the midpoint between them. If Y is a vehicle trajectory around a barrier, the MSE-optimal path runs through the barrier. If Y is an image, averaging different edges produces blur. If it is object identity, the representation becomes a fractional object that never exists.

The general result is that squared error learns the conditional mean f*(x)=E[Y|X=x]. That is appropriate when the mean itself is the desired query or when residuals form one approximately symmetric mode. It is not a representation of the distribution. A low average MSE can therefore coexist with wrong event frequency, missing rare hazards, and physically invalid samples.

3 · A branch variable must persist through time

A common mistake is to add independent noise to every predicted pixel or every time step. That creates diversity, but not coherent worlds. If a pedestrian’s latent intent is “cross,” that choice should influence position, pose, future reactions, and collision risk across many frames. We need a persistent branch variable u:

u ∼ p(u|It)    and    yt+1:t+H ∼ p(yt+1:t+H|It,at:t+H−1,u)

The marginal future integrates over alternatives: p(y|I,a)=∫p(y|I,a,u)p(u|I)du. One sample of u should create one internally consistent rollout. In an RSSM, stochastic latents at successive steps may represent both persistent intent and new surprises; the deterministic memory carries sampled choices forward. Hierarchical models make the distinction explicit with a slow “scenario” latent and faster local noise.

The probability of a complete trajectory also has sequential structure:

p(zt+1:t+H|zt,a) = ∏k=1H p(zt+k|zt+k−1,at+k−1)

Each factor conditions on the branch sampled so far. Resampling an independent left/right identity at every step makes an agent teleport between modes. Temporal coherence is therefore a testable property: object identity, intent, topology, and conserved quantities should remain consistent within a sample even while different samples disagree.

4 · Aleatoric and epistemic uncertainty are conditional, not intrinsic labels

Imagine that the model assigns a wide distribution to stopping distance. A naive system turns that width into one generic “low confidence” alarm. But the same numerical width can describe two different physical situations, and those situations demand different actions.

In the first situation, the model has seen abundant examples of this road, knows the relevant dynamics, and still cannot know which of several outcomes will occur because a hidden cause has not been observed. Perhaps the pedestrian’s intention is unresolved or a gust has not happened yet. This is aleatoric uncertainty at the current information level: several outcomes remain possible even for a well-learned model. More copies of the same camera-only data do not reveal the missing cause. The derived requirement is to preserve the outcome distribution rather than pretend one branch is correct. The implementation integrates over branches or uses a risk-sensitive decision rule. The diagnostic asks whether predicted event frequencies match repeated comparable cases. Additional sensing can still reduce the uncertainty if it reveals the hidden cause; for example, gaze or gesture may reveal pedestrian intent.

In the second situation, the road surface is unfamiliar and several learned models disagree about braking. The physical outcome may be nearly deterministic, but the correct mapping from state and action to outcome is not known from the training evidence. This is epistemic uncertainty. More representative data usually can reduce it. The derived requirement is not merely to average the candidate models, because the average can hide ignorance. The system should explore safely, collect data, abstain, shorten its planning horizon, or fall back to a conservative policy. A practical diagnostic holds out regions or actions during training, then checks whether disagreement or an out-of-support score rises before prediction error does.

Why the label can change
The category belongs to a prediction conditioned on a particular sensor set and dataset—not permanently to the scene. Pedestrian intent may be aleatoric to a camera-only model before a gesture is observed, then nearly resolved after gaze and map context are added. An unfamiliar road surface is epistemic until representative experience makes its dynamics known. Calling a data point “aleatoric forever” confuses a property of the world with a limitation of current information.

The linear decision rule is therefore: first ask what information is missing? Next ask would representative experience with the same inputs remove the spread? If no, carry the possible outcomes into risk-aware planning. If yes, treat the spread as evidence that the operating point is unsupported and change behavior or gather knowledge. A single confidence scalar erases this distinction.

The law of total variance clarifies the decomposition. Let model parameters W be uncertain after training data D:

Var(Y|x,D) = EW|D[Var(Y|x,W)] + VarW|D(E[Y|x,W])

The first term is expected outcome variance within a model—an aleatoric component. The second is disagreement between plausible models—an epistemic component. Deep ensembles approximate the second by training several models and comparing their predictions. This is not a perfect Bayesian decomposition: shared architecture, dataset, and optimization bias can make every member confidently wrong. Disagreement is evidence of ignorance; agreement is not proof of knowledge.

5 · Distribution families encode different assumptions

Heteroscedastic Gaussian outputs. A network predicts μ(x) and σ(x), trained with

−log p(y|x) = (y−μ)²/(2σ²) + log σ + constant

The residual term asks the mean to fit; dividing by variance lets noisy cases tolerate larger residuals; log σ prevents the model from declaring infinite uncertainty. This works for one continuous mode but still fills the space between separated modes.

Mixture density outputs. Predict weights, means, and scales for K components: p(y|x)=ΣkπkN(y;μk,σk²). Mixtures expose explicit mode probabilities and allow fast sampling. They require a useful component count; components may collapse onto the same mode, swap identities across time, or assign tiny variances to memorize examples.

Categorical latents and tokens. Cross-entropy over a codebook naturally represents separated alternatives. Autoregressive tokens can express complicated joint densities through conditioning. Quantization introduces distortion, token ordering affects latency, and rare codes can be poorly trained. A token probability is only meaningful if the tokenizer preserves the event distinctions the planner cares about.

Diffusion and flow models. These transform noise into samples through iterative denoising or continuous transport. They can represent rich continuous multimodality without specifying mixture components. Likelihood or probability calibration can be indirect, sampling is expensive, and mode probabilities may be hard to query inside a real-time planner.

Ensembles or approximate parameter posteriors. Multiple models expose epistemic disagreement and support pessimistic planning. Compute and memory grow roughly with ensemble size, and members trained on identical data can share blind spots. Bootstrap resampling, randomized priors, and architectural diversity encourage—but do not guarantee—meaningful diversity.

Quantiles, expectiles, and conformal sets. If a decision only asks for a tail boundary, learning the 95th percentile can be cheaper than generating the full world. Quantile losses target marginal coverage; conformal calibration can give finite-sample coverage under exchangeability. Neither automatically provides a coherent joint trajectory distribution, so per-step intervals can combine into impossible paths.

6 · Worked example: probability changes the decision

A delivery robot approaches a partly occluded crossing. Its world model predicts three mutually exclusive branches for the next four seconds:

  • u=clear with probability 0.70: nobody enters the path.
  • u=cross with probability 0.25: a pedestrian steps out after one second.
  • u=blocked with probability 0.05: an unseen cart already occupies the lane.

Under action continue, the model assigns costs 0, 20, and 100 respectively. Under slow, costs are 3, 5, and 12. Expected costs are

E[C|continue] = 0.70·0 + 0.25·20 + 0.05·100 = 10 E[C|slow] = 0.70·3 + 0.25·5 + 0.05·12 = 3.95

So expectation favors slowing. More importantly, a chance constraint requiring collision probability below 1% rejects “continue” even if its average progress reward were high: the 5% blocked branch alone violates the constraint. A conditional-value-at-risk rule would average the worst tail and also strongly prefer slowing. The example shows why returning only the mean trajectory or expected reward destroys the information the decision rule needs.

Now suppose the “blocked” event is predicted at 5% across 1,000 comparable encounters but occurs 120 times. The observed frequency is 12%, so the model is sharply overconfident in a safety-critical tail. Its ranking may still be good—higher scores may correlate with danger—but the number 0.05 cannot be used as a probability. Calibration is a separate property from discrimination.

7 · Calibration turns numerical scores into beliefs

For a binary event with forecast p and outcome y∈{0,1}, calibration means

P(Y=1 | p(X)=r) = r   for forecast levels r

Among cases forecast near 0.8, about 80% should occur. A reliability diagram bins forecasts and compares mean confidence with empirical frequency. Expected calibration error summarizes bin gaps, but depends on binning and can hide rare regimes. Report counts and confidence intervals, especially for tails.

Proper scoring rules reward both accurate location and honest uncertainty. Binary Brier score is (p−y)². Negative log-likelihood is −[y log p + (1−y)log(1−p)]; it strongly punishes confident mistakes. For continuous outcomes, inspect likelihood, probability-integral-transform histograms, interval coverage, and interval width. Coverage without sharpness is trivial: an interval spanning every possible outcome is calibrated but useless.

Calibration must be sliced by horizon, because uncertainty should generally widen; by action, because aggressive or unseen actions may be less supported; by scene, weather, geography, and object class; and by event rarity. Aggregate calibration can be excellent while a rare pedestrian class is dangerously overconfident. Distribution shift tests should ask whether uncertainty rises before error, not only whether average accuracy falls afterward.

Post-hoc temperature scaling can calibrate categorical logits on a fixed validation distribution. Isotonic regression is more flexible but data-hungry. Neither repairs missing modes or produces epistemic awareness under novel conditions. Calibration begins with coverage in training data and a distribution family capable of expressing the truth; scalar rescaling is the last adjustment, not the foundation.

8 · Marginal calibration does not guarantee a coherent world

A model may be calibrated for every variable separately and still assign the wrong joint future. Suppose it predicts a 50% chance of rain and a 50% chance of a wet road. Those marginals can match frequencies, yet a sampler that draws them independently produces “dry road during rain” and “wet road with no recent rain” too often. World models are used for coupled consequences, so cross-variable and cross-time dependence matters.

Let A be “pedestrian crosses” and B be “robot must brake.” Knowing P(A)=0.2 and P(B)=0.2 does not determine P(A∩B). If braking is needed exactly when crossing occurs, the joint probability is 0.2; an independence assumption gives 0.04. A planner using the latter underestimates the linked safety event by a factor of five. Persistent scenario latents and autoregressive conditioning are mechanisms for learning this dependence.

Test joints at the level decisions consume them. Measure whether predicted collision co-occurs with the relevant object path, whether audio impact aligns with visual contact, whether termination agrees with state constraints, and whether interval coverage holds simultaneously across a trajectory. Simultaneous 90% coverage is harder than 90% coverage at each time step: per-step misses accumulate. A conformal band or trajectory-level score must be calibrated for the complete query if that is the promised interface.

This also explains why evaluating generated futures only against the one recorded continuation is delicate. The record reveals one valid branch, not every branch that could have happened. Likelihood scores the probability of that observation; repeated starts, simulators, human annotations of plausible modes, and decision-event metrics provide additional evidence about coverage. “Different from the recording” is not automatically “physically wrong.”

Sampling is not uncertainty
Generating visually different futures proves only that the sampler can vary. Useful uncertainty requires mode frequencies that match reality, coherent samples across time and modalities, widening beliefs when evidence is removed, and increased caution under unsupported states or actions. Diversity without probability semantics is a special-effects generator, not a risk model.

9 · Training procedure: learn modes, probabilities, and ignorance separately

  1. Define decision-relevant events. In addition to dense latent or pixel targets, label or derive collision, contact, route choice, termination, and threshold-crossing events. Calibration needs countable outcomes.
  2. Preserve complete trajectory samples. A training continuation is one draw from the conditional future. Do not average repeated or aligned futures before learning; that erases modes at the dataset level.
  3. Condition on all available causal context. Include action, timing, map, and sensor context that deployment will possess. Missing known inputs inflate apparent aleatoric noise.
  4. Choose a coherent stochastic factorization. Use a persistent scenario latent or sequential state whose sampled branch survives through the rollout. Decide which variables are global, per-object, and per-step.
  5. Optimize a proper probabilistic objective. Use negative log-likelihood, cross-entropy, a diffusion score objective, or quantile loss as appropriate. Combine event heads carefully; an auxiliary MSE must not force modes back toward their mean.
  6. Prevent mode collapse. Track component usage or code entropy, inspect conditional samples, and ensure rare modes appear in batches. Reweighting may improve rare-event learning, but correct for the changed sampling prior when reporting probabilities.
  7. Estimate epistemic uncertainty. Train ensembles or another parameter-uncertainty approximation with genuinely different seeds, bootstraps, priors, or data partitions. Validate disagreement on controlled held-out regions.
  8. Calibrate on untouched data. Fit any temperature or conformal threshold without contaminating the test set. Recalibrate when the deployment mixture or policy changes.
  9. Evaluate the decision interface. Feed branches into expected-cost, chance-constrained, and tail-risk planners. A small density improvement matters only if relevant decisions or safety estimates improve.

Data balance requires special care. Oversampling rare hazards helps representation learning but changes their empirical frequency. If the likelihood sees a 50/50 resampled dataset when the real rate is 1/99, raw output probabilities inherit the artificial prior unless importance weights or prior correction are applied. Ranking metrics can improve while calibration becomes unusable.

10 · Failure modes and targeted diagnostics

Uncertainty failures are easiest to debug as causal chains rather than as isolated scores. Begin with what a person can observe in a rollout, propose the mechanism that could create it, perform one test that separates that mechanism from its alternatives, and only then change the model.

  1. The samples differ only in texture. At first glance the generator looks diverse: shirts change color and pavement grain moves. Yet every pedestrian follows the same path and every reward is identical. The naive conclusion is that many visual samples imply many possible worlds. The actual mechanism is that noise bypasses dynamics and enters only the detail decoder. Measure diversity in object paths, discrete events, and predicted rewards rather than pixels. If semantic diversity is absent, inject a persistent latent before the dynamics and supervise branch-relevant heads. The repair succeeds only when a sampled choice changes coherent downstream consequences across time.
  2. Mixture components overlap. Several components were allocated, but they converge to the same mean or inflate their scales until every component covers every mode. The model then has multiple parameter labels without multiple explanations. Plot component usage, means, entropy, and posterior responsibilities. Improve initialization, regularize usage, or use discrete or hierarchical latents whose structure better matches separated modes. Re-test whether components specialize in distinct event topologies without assigning vanishing variance merely to memorize examples.
  3. Identity switches inside one sample. A pedestrian is “crossing” at one step, “waiting” at the next, then “crossing” again because the model resamples intent independently. This is not legitimate uncertainty; it is a failure to preserve a causal choice. Track object identity and intent consistency over each rollout. Carry the sampled mode in recurrent memory or draw a trajectory-level latent once. The diagnostic should show disagreement between samples while each individual sample remains internally consistent.
  4. The model is confident on unfamiliar states. A single network has no mechanism for parameter ignorance, or all ensemble members share the same data and architectural blind spot. Hold out geographic regions, object classes, or action ranges, then compare error with ensemble disagreement or support scores. If confidence does not degrade before accuracy does, add genuinely diverse ensembles, out-of-distribution features, explicit support constraints, and a fallback policy. Agreement must never be promoted from “no detected disagreement” to “proof of correctness.”
  5. Uncertainty grows everywhere. A model can inflate variance to make residuals less costly, masking bias or underfitting. Compare residual magnitude, the residual and scale terms of negative log-likelihood, and interval width by regime. If routine cases receive the same broad intervals as ambiguous ones, improve the model or data and let the likelihood penalize unjustified scale. Honest uncertainty should grow where information is missing, not serve as a universal excuse for error.
  6. Overall calibration looks good while the safety tail is wrong. Common, easy events dominate the aggregate and hide rare pedestrian or collision failures. Draw reliability plots by class, horizon, action, and event rarity, including counts and confidence intervals. Add tail-specific heads, targeted data, group calibration, or a conservative threshold. Then verify the promised slice directly; an improved global calibration number is not evidence that the rare tail was repaired.
  7. Sample diversity is high but mode coverage is low. Many variations may remain inside one route while a second valid route never appears. Best-of-N distance alone can improve just by buying more near-duplicates. Pair it with event-mode recall and predicted frequency. If a topology is absent, represent that branch explicitly and train on examples that contain it. The goal is coverage of qualitatively different consequences, not maximal visual motion.
  8. The ensemble agrees and still fails. All members can inherit the same dataset gap or model-class error. Random train/test splits often preserve that shared blind spot, so test controlled shifts that remove a region, mechanism, or intervention. Broaden both data and model class when every member fails together. Ensemble disagreement is a useful alarm; ensemble agreement is never a safety certificate.

Best-of-N metrics need interpretation. Giving a model many samples increases the chance that one lands near truth, even if probability mass is badly allocated. Report both coverage and probability quality. Likewise, visual realism does not prove physical correctness: measure conservation, collision, object permanence, cross-modal consistency, and event timing within each sampled world.

11 · Architecture and objective tradeoffs are planner tradeoffs

A sampling planner may evaluate M action sequences with K stochastic branches over horizon H, yielding roughly M·K·H transition steps. Doubling ensemble size or branch count doubles cost. Diffusion adds denoising steps inside each transition. Probability fidelity must therefore be designed with the compute envelope, not added after model training.

Explicit mixtures allow a planner to retain named high-probability modes and allocate samples stratified by weight. Implicit generators can cover richer futures but may waste samples on redundant branches. Quantile heads are efficient for one-dimensional risk constraints but cannot answer arbitrary counterfactual queries. Ensembles may be run only on high-risk or low-support states, while a single model handles familiar regions.

Training objectives also choose behavior. Maximum likelihood covers the data distribution and heavily penalizes assigning near-zero density to observed events. Adversarial or perceptual losses can sharpen samples while dropping modes. Best-of-many objectives encourage at least one accurate sample but may not learn correct probabilities. Risk-weighted losses improve safety-event recall but deliberately distort the natural distribution unless a separate calibrated head is maintained.

12 · System-design implications

Expose structured uncertainty. The model interface should return samples or modes together with weights, event probabilities, epistemic scores, and horizon. A single “confidence” scalar mixes incompatible meanings and is difficult to validate.

Separate prediction from risk preference. The model estimates what may happen. The planner chooses expectation, variance penalty, chance constraint, CVaR, or worst-case behavior. Baking one risk attitude into the latent model makes reuse and calibration harder.

Track support under candidate actions. A planner can exploit optimistic errors by proposing actions absent from training. Measure action-conditioned density or ensemble disagreement, penalize unsupported plans, and send unfamiliar cases to a conservative controller.

Preserve rare branches through the pipeline. Beam pruning, sample budgets, cache compression, and dashboard averaging can each silently remove a 0.5% catastrophe. Safety-relevant modes need explicit retention rules even when they contribute little to expected loss.

Monitor calibration after policy changes. Once a planner uses the model, it changes visited states and interactions with other agents. A forecast calibrated under the collection policy may become wrong under the new policy. Log predicted probabilities and eventual outcomes, with delayed labels, to detect drift.

Design fallback behavior before deployment. Epistemic uncertainty is valuable only if the system can respond. Define thresholds, minimum-risk maneuvers, operator escalation, or sensing actions. Otherwise uncertainty is an unused dashboard statistic.

Record the probability before the outcome. Calibration monitoring requires immutable, timestamped forecasts joined later with outcomes. Recomputing a forecast after new evidence leaks the answer and makes reliability look artificially good. Store the model version, conditioning sensors, proposed action, chosen action, horizon, and event definition so an apparent calibration shift can be traced to a changed model, policy, or label.

13 · Bridge: uncertainty must survive self-composition

We can now represent separated modes, distinguish unresolved outcomes from model ignorance, train probabilities with proper scores, and test their calibration. Yet all of these may look convincing at one transition and still fail when the model consumes its own samples repeatedly. A slight mean bias shifts the next input; a missed low-probability branch can never reappear; overestimated variance can explode; an inconsistent branch variable can switch identities.

Lesson 06 studies that self-composition directly. We will contrast teacher-forced one-step prediction with open-loop rollout, derive how errors propagate through a transition Jacobian, and show why multi-step objectives, state perturbations, posterior correction, and receding-horizon control are complementary rather than interchangeable.

Takeaway
A world model should predict a conditional distribution, not a visually plausible average. Preserve coherent modes with persistent latent choices; treat aleatoric outcome variation and epistemic ignorance differently; train with proper probabilistic objectives; calibrate by horizon, action, regime, and rarity; and pass the full decision-relevant distribution to a planner whose risk rule is explicit.

Interview prompts

  • Why does MSE blur a multimodal future? Its population optimum is the conditional mean, which lies between modes and may correspond to no valid outcome.
  • Why is independent decoder noise insufficient? It creates local variation but not a persistent causal branch; intent, identity, geometry, and events must remain coherent across the sampled trajectory.
  • How do aleatoric and epistemic uncertainty differ? Aleatoric variation remains among outcomes at the available information level; epistemic uncertainty comes from not knowing the correct model and can usually be reduced with representative data.
  • Does ensemble agreement prove the model is in distribution? No. Members can share data and architecture blind spots; disagreement signals ignorance, while agreement is not proof of knowledge.
  • What is calibration? Among events assigned probability r, approximately fraction r should occur, evaluated within relevant horizon and regime slices.
  • Why report sharpness with coverage? An extremely wide interval can cover the truth while being useless; sharpness asks for the narrowest uncertainty consistent with correct coverage.
  • How can class rebalancing damage probabilities? Oversampling changes the training prior. Without importance weighting or prior correction, predicted frequencies reflect the resampled dataset rather than deployment.
  • How should a planner use rare catastrophic branches? Through explicit chance constraints, tail-risk measures such as CVaR, or safety envelopes—not by averaging them away in expected reward.
