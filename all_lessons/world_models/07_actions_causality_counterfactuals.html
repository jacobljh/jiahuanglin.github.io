Actions and causality

Watching what agents usually do reveals correlations. Predicting what your action will cause requires interventions, coverage, and a model that does not confuse the policy with the world.

The dependency
Lessons 01–06 gave us a belief state, action-conditioned dynamics, uncertainty, and stable rollouts. One assumption remained hidden: that the action effect learned from logged trajectories is the effect the agent will obtain when it deliberately chooses that action. This lesson derives when that assumption is justified—and what to do when it is not.

1 · Begin with the decision, not the dataset

A world model is useful to an agent because it can compare futures. At time t, the agent has history Ht = (o≤t, a<t), compresses it into a belief bt, and considers candidate actions. The planner's primitive question is not “What usually followed action a in the log?” It is:

If I deliberately set At = a from this situation, how will the distribution of St+1, Ot+1, and reward change?

Here uppercase S,O,A denote random variables whose possible values are uncertain, while lowercase s,o,a denote particular realized or deliberately chosen values. This convention lets us distinguish “the logged variable At happened to equal a” from “the agent intervened to set At=a.”

This wording matters. A logged action is an observation: it tells us what a previous controller chose after seeing its information. A candidate action is an intervention: the new controller sets it. Those operations can induce different distributions. A model may predict held-out trajectories extremely well by learning the old controller's habits, yet fail when a new planner chooses unusual actions.

From first principles, separate three mechanisms:

  1. The environment evolves according to latent state and action: St+1 = f(St, At, εt). Here εt is exogenous process noise: influences not caused by variables inside our model.
  2. The sensor emits an observation: Ot+1 = g(St+1, ηt+1), with sensor noise η.
  3. The behavior policy produced the log: At = πb(Ht, ξt), where ξ captures policy randomization.

The behavior policy is a data-collection mechanism, not a law of physics. A causal world model should learn the first two mechanisms without baking the third into them. Otherwise changing the policy also appears to change “the world.”

2 · Why passive trajectories mix policy and physics

Suppose a hidden road condition U affects both the driver's braking action A and stopping success Y. An experienced driver brakes hard when the road is icy. Ice also makes stopping harder. The causal graph contains U → A, U → Y, and the genuine action edge A → Y. The path A ← U → Y is a backdoor path: it creates association between action and outcome that is not transmitted by the action.

This is confounding. A confounder is a common cause of a treatment/action and an outcome. It need not be literally invisible. It is enough that the representation supplied to the transition model omits it or fails to distinguish its relevant values. In a partially observed environment, road friction, another person's intention, battery health, or object mass can all play this role.

There is an important qualification. If St is the complete causal state—Markov, fully observed, and containing every common cause of At and St+1—then the conditional transition p(st+1 | st, at) can equal the interventional transition p(st+1 | st, do(at)). The problem is not that every action-conditioned model is noncausal. The problem is that learned latents and finite histories may not be causally sufficient, and prediction loss alone does not prove sufficiency.

3 · Conditioning and intervention are exact, different operations

Conditioning filters an existing joint distribution to cases in which an event was observed. p(Y | A=a) asks: among trajectories where the behavior policy happened to choose a, how frequent was each outcome? Seeing the action provides evidence about its causes. If hard braking is mostly chosen on ice, observing hard braking raises the probability of ice.

Intervention modifies the data-generating process. Pearl's do(A=a) operator replaces the structural equation A = πb(H,ξ) with the constant assignment A := a. Graphically, it cuts every arrow entering A while preserving arrows leaving it. The distribution of road conditions does not change merely because our experimenter sets the brake command.

p(Y | A=a) = Σu p(Y | a,u) p(u | a)    observational p(Y | do(A=a)) = Σu p(Y | a,u) p(u)    interventional

Read every term. Y is the outcome. A is the chosen action. U is the common cause. The first expression weights terrain by p(u|a), the terrain mix selected by the old policy. The second weights by p(u), the terrain mix in the target population. The two are equal only under extra conditions—for example, if action was randomized, if U does not affect action, or if the outcome does not vary with U.

In a sequential model, we usually want a conditional causal transition:

p(St+1 | bt, do(At=a))

Conditioning on bt says “start from this belief.” Intervening on At says “then force this action.” Writing p(s'|a) without the starting state is usually too crude: the same throttle command has different effects uphill, downhill, or against a wall.

4 · A worked example: hard braking looks harmful

Let U ∈ {dry, icy}, A ∈ {hard, gentle}, and let Y=1 mean “stops within 20 metres.” Rather than place the probabilities in a grid, build the situation in the same order that nature and the driver generate it:

  1. Nature chooses the road. Eighty percent of situations are dry and twenty percent are icy. This is the population we ultimately care about.
  2. The driver sees evidence about that road and chooses a brake. Hard braking is rare on dry road—probability 0.10—but common on ice—probability 0.90. Therefore the action itself becomes evidence about difficulty.
  3. Physics turns road and action into an outcome. On dry road, success is 0.90 under hard braking and 0.70 under gentle braking. On ice, both actions work less well, but hard braking still has probability 0.40 of success versus 0.10 for gentle braking.

Notice the physical fact before doing any arithmetic: hard braking is better on each road type. A naive comparison of logged actions can nevertheless reverse that conclusion because hard braking was sent disproportionately into the harder road condition. Now follow the two computations separately so their different meanings remain visible.

First compute what the log reports. Hard braking occurs with probability 0.8×0.1 + 0.2×0.9 = 0.26. Successful hard-brake cases have probability 0.8×0.1×0.9 + 0.2×0.9×0.4 = 0.144. Therefore:

P(Y=1 | A=hard) = 0.144 / 0.26 ≈ 0.554

Gentle braking occurs with probability 0.74. Successful gentle cases have probability 0.8×0.9×0.7 + 0.2×0.1×0.1 = 0.506. Thus:

P(Y=1 | A=gentle) = 0.506 / 0.74 ≈ 0.684

The observational comparison says hard braking is 13 percentage points worse. Now intervene while holding the population's road mix fixed:

P(Y=1 | do(hard)) = 0.8×0.90 + 0.2×0.40 = 0.80 P(Y=1 | do(gentle)) = 0.8×0.70 + 0.2×0.10 = 0.58

The causal comparison says hard braking is 22 points better. Nothing paradoxical happened. We answered two questions using two different terrain mixtures. The old policy reserved hard braking for difficult cases, so action identity leaked difficulty.

If road condition is observed and satisfies the backdoor criterion, we can adjust for it with the second formula. If it is hidden, the effect is not automatically recoverable. A larger transformer can estimate the observational distribution more precisely while remaining precisely wrong about the intervention.

5 · Identification: when does data determine the causal answer?

A causal effect is identifiable when the observed-data distribution plus stated assumptions uniquely determines that effect. Identifiability is not the same as statistical precision. With too few samples, an identifiable estimate has wide error bars. With hidden confounding, infinite observational data may still leave several causal explanations equally compatible with the log.

Common routes to identification are:

  • Randomized actions. If an experiment assigns action independently of pre-action causes, then p(u|a)=p(u). Randomization is conceptually clean but can be unsafe or wasteful.
  • Backdoor adjustment. Observe a pre-action set X that blocks every noncausal path from A to Y, then estimate Σxp(y|a,x)p(x). Do not adjust for descendants of the action; doing so may erase part of the effect.
  • Multiple behavior policies. Data from controllers with different action tendencies can help separate stable dynamics from policy-specific correlations. Policy identity may act as an environment variable, but diversity alone does not guarantee identification.
  • Instrumental variables. An instrument changes action, has no direct path to outcome, and shares no hidden cause with outcome. These assumptions are strong and domain-specific, not a generic neural-network trick.
  • Known mechanisms or simulators. Conservation laws, geometry, rigid-body contact, and calibrated experiments can restrict possible causal models. Simulation adds interventions cheaply, but its effects transfer only to the extent that its mechanisms match reality.
Adjustment trap
More conditioning is not always safer. Conditioning on a collider—a variable caused by both action and another cause—can create a spurious association. Conditioning on a mediator can remove the indirect effect you meant to estimate. Choose adjustment variables from a causal story, not from feature importance alone.

6 · Positivity: no model learns an untried choice for free

Even without confounding, estimation needs support, also called positivity or overlap. For every starting situation the target policy may visit, the behavior data must assign nonzero probability to each action whose effect we want:

π(a | b) > 0 ⇒ πb(a | b) > 0

Here π is the proposed target policy and πb is the behavior policy. The implication says: if the new policy might choose a at belief b, the logger must sometimes have chosen it there. “Nonzero” is only the mathematical minimum. If the probability is tiny, estimates have enormous variance and neural models extrapolate rather than infer.

This is why expert demonstrations are both valuable and dangerous. Experts provide competent outcomes but deliberately avoid collisions, unstable grasps, extreme torques, and recovery states. A planner optimizing against the learned model searches far more aggressively than an ordinary test set. It may discover that an unsupported action receives a fantastically high predicted reward. This is model exploitation, not intelligence.

The naive response is “collect more data.” But volume is not the missing variable; the missing variable is which alternatives were tried from which situations. That requirement determines a staged data strategy:

  1. Begin with expert logs to learn the ordinary operating region. They contribute high-quality behavior in common states, so they are an efficient foundation. Their competence is also their weakness: action support is narrow and failures are intentionally absent.
  2. Add controlled random exploration where consequences are reversible. Randomization breaks the link between hidden difficulty and action, producing locally clean intervention effects. Pure random exploration is unsafe and inefficient in the physical world, so it should not be treated as a universal collection policy.
  3. Mix multiple purposeful policies to widen coverage. Different controllers visit more states and choose a broader set of actions than one expert. Yet each can still use hidden information when selecting an action, so policy diversity reduces one weakness without automatically removing confounding.
  4. Use a simulator to repeat dangerous or rare interventions cheaply. Controlled resets make matched-start comparisons possible at scale. The new failure mode is mechanism mismatch: if contact, friction, sensing, or human response differs, the intervention effect inherits a simulation-to-reality gap.
  5. Finish with guarded online exploration aimed at decision-relevant uncertainty. The current model identifies the state–action regions that matter to the proposed task, and the collector probes those regions rather than exploring uniformly. Because these trials touch reality, they require constraints, monitoring, and a fallback controller.

The order matters. Experts ground normal behavior; controlled diversity exposes alternative effects; simulation expands the safe experimental envelope; guarded reality checks correct simulator and model bias. No source is sufficient alone because every source exchanges one kind of evidence for one kind of risk.

Inverse-propensity weighting can correct a known action-selection imbalance: weight a sample by roughly π(a|b)/πb(a|b). But when πb is near zero, the weight explodes; when it is zero, no weighting creates the missing outcome. Propensity methods address observed selection, not hidden confounding.

7 · Counterfactuals are more specific than interventions

An intervention asks a population-level hypothetical: what outcome distribution would result if action a were set? A counterfactual asks about the same particular unit or episode: given that this vehicle actually braked gently and stopped after 24 metres, what would its stopping distance have been had it braked hard?

In a structural causal model, counterfactual reasoning has three steps:

  1. Abduction: infer a posterior over exogenous variables ε from the factual history. These variables capture this episode's particular friction, reaction delay, and disturbances.
  2. Action: replace the action equation with A:=a'.
  3. Prediction: run the modified mechanisms using the same inferred exogenous variables.
Ya′(u) = outcome produced for the same unit u when A is set to a′

The “same noise” requirement gives counterfactual consistency. Sampling a fresh road and fresh disturbances under each candidate action compares two different possible episodes, which can be sufficient for expected planning but does not answer the individual counterfactual. Individual effects are generally harder to identify than average intervention effects because we never observe both potential outcomes Ya and Ya′ for the same real episode.

8 · Some actions change the world; others reveal it

Actions can affect utility through two routes. A world-changing action changes physical state: push the box, apply the brake, deliver medicine. An information action changes what the agent will observe: move the camera to expose an occluded face, tap an object to estimate stiffness, ask a clarifying question, or run a diagnostic test. One action can do both.

For active perception, write the transition and sensor mechanisms separately:

St+1 ~ p(St+1 | St, do(At));   Ot+1 ~ p(Ot+1 | St+1, At)

A camera rotation may barely change the scene state but strongly change the next observation. That observation updates belief:

bt+1(s′) ∝ p(ot+1 | s′,at) Σsp(s′|s,at)bt(s)

The right side says: predict the next state from the old belief, then reweight predicted states by how well each explains the new observation. An information action is valuable when this posterior enables better later decisions. Its immediate physical reward may be negative—it costs time or energy—while its value of information is positive:

VOI(a) = Eo~p(o|b,a)[maxa′ Q(b′(o),a′)] − maxa′Q(b,a′) − cost(a)

Term by term: the first term is expected value after seeing the action-dependent observation; the second is value if we act now without that evidence; the third is sensing cost. A world model that predicts physical reward but not belief change systematically undervalues looking, probing, and asking.

9 · Train and plan without pretending uncertainty is knowledge

A practical system combines causal design with uncertainty-aware control. First learn a belief representation from history, but pressure it to retain variables that predict outcomes under varied policies—not merely variables that identify the logger. Train ensembles or Bayesian approximations to expose disagreement in poorly supported regions. Record behavior-policy probabilities when possible. Add intervention data strategically rather than uniformly.

At planning time, score reward while penalizing unsupported or disputed transitions. A compact design is:

belief = infer_belief(observation_history, action_history)
for candidate_sequence in action_sequences:
    imagined = belief
    score = 0
    for action in candidate_sequence:
        next_dist = world_model.predict(imagined, do=action)
        uncertainty = ensemble_disagreement(next_dist)
        support = behavior_density(imagined, action)
        if violates_hard_constraint(next_dist):
            score = -infinity
            break
        score += expected_reward(next_dist)
        score -= beta * uncertainty
        score -= gamma * support_penalty(support)
        imagined = next_dist
    record (candidate_sequence, score)
choose first action of the highest-scoring candidate_sequence
execute, observe reality, update belief, and replan

The explicit do=action is a reminder about semantics; an API flag cannot make observational training causal. β governs epistemic caution. γ discourages departure from demonstrated support. Hard constraints protect outcomes too costly to trade for reward. Receding-horizon execution limits how long the agent trusts an uncorrected model.

These safeguards introduce real tradeoffs. A large support penalty is safe but may prevent improvement beyond the demonstrator. A small penalty allows useful discovery but invites exploitation. Random exploration identifies effects but spends safety budget. Rich physical structure improves extrapolation but can impose the wrong inductive bias. The right setting depends on reversibility, consequence severity, monitoring latency, and how quickly real observations correct the belief.

10 · Diagnostics: ask whether the model learned control

Ordinary held-out likelihood is necessary but insufficient because train and test logs can share the same policy. Add tests that isolate action effects:

  • Matched-start intervention test. Start from the same measured or inferred state, execute different randomized actions, and compare predicted versus actual outcome distributions.
  • Action-swap test. Hold initial belief and exogenous seed fixed, swap only the candidate action, and verify that predicted differences have the right sign, scale, delay, and affected variables.
  • Policy-shift test. Evaluate on data from a controller whose action frequencies differ from training. Stable physics should transfer after accounting for changed state coverage.
  • Negative control. Shuffle action labels or provide an action known to have no effect on a chosen outcome. Unexpected predictive power indicates leakage or confounding.
  • Support map. Plot state–action density, ensemble disagreement, calibration error, and planner visitation together. Average uncertainty can hide a dangerous, narrow blind spot.
  • Constraint test. Under adversarially optimized action sequences, check conservation laws, collision geometry, actuator limits, monotonic relationships, and causal delay.
  • Information-action test. Measure whether sensing actions reduce posterior uncertainty and improve later decisions, not merely whether they make the next frame easy to predict.

A particularly revealing test freezes the starting belief and optimizes actions against the model, then executes only in a high-fidelity simulator or guarded real setup. If predicted return rises while actual return falls, the planner found a model error. Track this optimization gap as model pressure increases.

11 · Common failure modes and what they imply

Read a failure as a causal trace, not as a label to match. Start with the visible symptom, ask what shortcut or missing mechanism could produce it, then choose a test that can falsify that explanation before applying the repair:

  1. Different actions produce nearly identical rollouts. The likely chain is that passive state already predicts most frames, so the easiest loss-minimizing model ignores action. Balance intervention data, add action-sensitivity tests, and give task-relevant action consequences enough weight in the loss. The diagnostic succeeds when changing only the action produces the correct sign, scale, delay, and affected variables.
  2. An action looks effective only under the training controller. Policy identity or hidden state has probably remained confounded with action. Collect multi-policy or randomized data, improve the causal state, and test transfer under a controller with different action frequencies. If the effect vanishes after policy shift, the model learned the logger rather than stable physics.
  3. The planner selects extreme, unnatural controls. Optimization has probably crossed the data-support boundary and found optimistic extrapolation. Constrain actions, penalize epistemic uncertainty, and collect targeted safe data around the proposed region. Compare predicted and realized return as optimization pressure increases; a widening gap is the diagnostic signature.
  4. Prediction relies on camera shake or dashboard indicators. A post-action cue is leaking the command through an easier route than physical reasoning. Mask nuisance channels, alter overlays, randomize camera effects, and test temporal ordering. A genuine action model should keep its consequence prediction after those correlates are broken.
  5. Counterfactual videos change unrelated background details. Each candidate branch is probably drawing fresh latent noise, so it represents a different episode rather than a different action in the same episode. Share inferred exogenous variables across branches. Background facts unrelated to the action should then remain fixed while true descendants change.
  6. The agent never looks before a risky manipulation. The model probably omits action-dependent observations or the downstream value of information. Model belief updates and train sensing actions on later decision value, not only immediate reward. Test whether a look or probe reduces posterior uncertainty and improves the subsequent manipulation choice.
  7. Offline metrics are strong but online return collapses. The planner has induced a new state–action distribution that the passive test set never measured. Evaluate closed-loop, map support along selected plans, re-observe reality, and iterate collection. The decisive diagnostic is not another random holdout; it is whether error concentrates where optimization chooses to go.
Shortcut warning
Action tokens often correlate with dashboard overlays, controller sounds, camera shake, or future frames that leak the action. A network can use these cues to continue video without learning how the command changes the scene. Remove leakage and test interventions from matched beliefs.

12 · A first-principles checklist for a new system

  1. Name the causal query. Is the product estimating an average intervention, an individual counterfactual, or merely forecasting the current policy? Specify outcome, horizon, starting population, and action.
  2. Draw the mechanisms. Separate latent environment, sensor, behavior policy, reward, and possible hidden common causes. Mark which variables are pre-action, post-action, or selected into the dataset.
  3. Audit identification. State why the desired effect follows from available data: randomization, sufficient adjustment, a valid instrument, multiple environments plus invariance, or trusted structure.
  4. Audit overlap. Compare target-policy actions with logger support in the states the target will visit. Treat weak support as epistemic uncertainty, not as a license to interpolate confidently.
  5. Design interventions. Spend experiment budget where action effects are both uncertain and decision-relevant. Respect constraints and use simulation or guarded trials when consequences are high.
  6. Evaluate under optimization. Test matched starts, action swaps, policy shifts, long rollouts, and adversarial plans. Prediction on untouched logs does not simulate deployment.
  7. Close the loop. Execute a short prefix, observe, update belief, detect mismatch, and fall back safely. Causal learning reduces model error; feedback limits the cost of what remains.

Where this points next

We can now state the contract required for imagination: a belief-conditioned model must predict the consequences of chosen actions, preserve uncertainty, and signal when a proposed plan leaves identifiable support. Lesson 08 uses that model to generate synthetic trajectories and train a policy inside imagination. The crucial caution carries forward: imagined experience is valuable only where the learned causal mechanism is trustworthy.

Takeaway
Action-conditioned prediction is not automatically causal. Conditioning preserves the behavior policy's selection; do(a) replaces that selection and asks what the action itself changes. Recovering the answer requires a causally sufficient state or a valid identification strategy, plus state–action support. Then planning must still respect epistemic uncertainty, value information-gathering actions, test matched counterfactuals, and re-observe reality before model errors compound.

Interview prompts

  • Why can expert logs estimate the wrong braking effect? Experts choose strong braking in dangerous states, so danger causes both action and outcome. The observational comparison weights road conditions by p(u|a); the intervention weights them by p(u). Adjust for a sufficient pre-action state, randomize safely, or state that the effect is not identified.
  • What exactly does do(A=a) change? It replaces the action-generating structural equation with A:=a and cuts incoming causal arrows to A. It does not erase action's effects, resample the starting state merely because the old policy preferred another action, or mean “filter logged rows where A=a.”
  • When is p(s′|s,a) a valid causal transition? When s is a causally sufficient pre-action state that blocks common causes of action and next state, the action has adequate support at s, and the mechanism is stable across the policy change. A learned latent is not sufficient merely because it predicts held-out logs well.
  • Distinguish identifiability, overlap, and uncertainty. Identifiability asks whether assumptions and infinite observed data uniquely determine the effect. Overlap asks whether relevant actions occur in relevant states. Statistical uncertainty asks how precisely finite data estimates an identifiable effect; epistemic uncertainty should grow when overlap is weak.
  • How does a counterfactual differ from an intervention? An intervention describes outcomes under a forced action for a population. An individual counterfactual holds the factual episode's inferred exogenous conditions fixed, changes only the action equation, and predicts that same episode's alternative outcome.
  • Why will a planner expose errors missed by test likelihood? Optimization actively searches action space for high predicted return and therefore selects rare combinations where approximation error is favorable. The induced distribution differs from passive test logs; support penalties, ensembles, constraints, adversarial evaluation, and replanning reduce the risk.
  • What is an information action, and how should a world model value it? It is an action whose observation changes belief, such as moving a camera or probing stiffness. Its value is the expected improvement in later decisions after the belief update, minus sensing cost—not just immediate physical reward.
  • Design a causal action-effect evaluation. Match or randomize starting states, execute several supported actions, compare calibrated outcome distributions, and repeat under a shifted policy. Add shared-noise action swaps, negative controls, constraint checks, support maps, and an optimization-gap test using plans selected by the model.
