Why one-step accuracy fails

A transition is not deployed once on a clean state. It is composed with itself, often thousands of times, and every output becomes a future input. We will derive the resulting distribution shift and design training, evaluation, and control around it.

Dependency recap
Lesson 05 replaced the single average future with a calibrated conditional distribution over coherent branches. That solved a one-transition problem: which futures are possible and how much probability belongs to each? A planner needs paths, not isolated edges. It repeatedly samples p(zt+1|zt,at), so small state, probability, and mode errors are fed back into the model. This lesson separates teacher-forced accuracy from free-running reliability.

1 · Teacher forcing changes the problem being measured

Assume a dataset trajectory contains latent states inferred from real observations, z0:T, and actions a0:T−1. One-step training minimizes a loss such as

L1(θ) = E(z,a,z′)∼D[d(Fθ(z,a), z′)]

Every input z comes from the data distribution ddata(z). The target is the next observation-informed state z′. This setup is called teacher forcing: at every step, reality supplies the prefix. The model can make an error at time t without suffering its effect at t+1, because training replaces the wrong prediction with the clean true state.

An open-loop rollout does something else:

ẑt=zt,    ẑt+k+1∼pθ(·|ẑt+k,at+k)

After the first step, inputs come from the model-induced distribution dθ,k(ẑ), not from ddata. The transition is evaluated precisely where it may have received no supervision. If its first output is slightly off manifold, the next output is conditioned on that error; this new error changes the following input; and the mismatch compounds. The name exposure bias describes this mismatch between clean histories seen in training and self-generated histories encountered in use.

Formally, low Ed data[d] does not imply low Ed θ,k[d]. Generalization requires either that the induced states remain close to data states, that the transition be accurate in a neighborhood around data, or that observation correction resets the error before the model leaves its reliable region.

2 · Derive error propagation from the local transition

For clarity, consider deterministic true dynamics zk+1=F(zk,ak) and learned dynamics ẑk+1=F̂(ẑk,ak). Define state error ek=ẑk−zk. Add and subtract F̂(zk,ak):

ek+1 = [F̂(ẑk,ak)−F̂(zk,ak)] + [F̂(zk,ak)−F(zk,ak)]

The first bracket is amplification of existing state error. The second is fresh one-step model error on a clean state; call it δk. Linearize the first bracket around the true state using the Jacobian Jk=∂F̂/∂z:

ek+1 ≈ Jk ek + δk

This equation is the core mechanic. If directions in J contract, errors shrink. If its dominant singular value exceeds one, errors amplify. Fresh bias is injected at every step. Repeated substitution gives

eH ≈ (JH−1···J0)e0 + Σi=0H−1(JH−1···Ji+1)δi

Each local error is transported through all later transitions. This is why one-step MSE alone is incomplete: two models with equal δ can have radically different rollout error because one learns stable local dynamics and the other has an expansive Jacobian.

3 · Worked numerical example: 3% becomes a different state

Consider one error-sensitive coordinate with constant amplification J=1.08 and a small systematic one-step bias δ=0.03. Start exactly on the true state, so e0=0. The recurrence is

ek+1=1.08ek+0.03

After one step, error is 0.03. After two it is 1.08·0.03+0.03=0.0624. The closed form after H steps is a geometric sum:

eH=0.03(1+1.08+···+1.08H−1) = 0.03(1.08H−1)/(1.08−1)

At ten steps, 1.08¹⁰≈2.159, so e10≈0.03·(1.159/0.08)=0.435. A validation report saying “three hundredths one-step error” concealed a fourteen-times larger ten-step displacement. If a contact boundary sits 0.4 units away, the learned rollout enters the wrong contact mode while the true system does not. From then on the relevant transition law changes discontinuously, and the linear approximation understates the damage.

If the bias alternated sign randomly in a contracting system, errors might partly cancel. But learned model errors are rarely independent: the same unmodeled friction, timing offset, or geometry bias persists across a sequence. Systematic error is especially dangerous because it accumulates coherently.

4 · Distributional errors compound too

Stochastic dynamics replaces a point error with divergence between trajectory distributions. The true and learned rollouts factor as

p(τ|zt,a)=∏kp(zk+1|zk,ak),    p̂(τ|zt,a)=∏kp̂(ẑk+1|ẑk,ak)

A missed branch at step two receives zero descendants at steps three through H; no later sampling can recover it. A slightly wrong mode probability is multiplied through conditional branches. Excess variance may spread samples into unsupported states, where the transition becomes epistemically unreliable. Too little variance creates overconfident, narrow rollouts that look stable but omit valid outcomes.

Open-loop uncertainty should usually widen with horizon, but monotonic entropy is not a universal rule. A future observation-independent event may resolve uncertainty: two routes can merge, or a stable attractor can contract states. Diagnose the shape of predicted distributions, event coverage, and calibration by horizon rather than rewarding variance growth itself.

For an RSSM, the gap between a k-step prior and the posterior at the destination is especially informative. The posterior sees the real future observation; the multi-step prior has only the start state and intervening actions. Their divergence measures how much the free-running model failed to anticipate what evidence later revealed.

5 · Open loop, closed loop, and receding horizon are different products

“How accurate is the model?” is incomplete until we specify when reality is allowed to correct it. Start with the same physical system and progressively remove access to evidence. Each removal creates a new product with a new requirement.

  1. Teacher-forced one-step prediction: reality replaces the state every step. This is the local training and filtering test from Section 1. The model receives a real observation-informed state, predicts one transition, and is immediately returned to the data manifold. The requirement is accurate dynamics near recorded states. The tempting but failed inference is that repeated success here proves simulator quality. Correction can hide weak dynamics because the model never has to consume its own error. Diagnose that gap by withholding the next correction and measuring the first horizon at which error accelerates.
  2. Open-loop rollout: reality supplies the start, then disappears. Simulation, plan scoring, and generated video often operate this way. Every predicted state becomes the next input, and every sampled branch determines its descendants. The derived requirement is stable self-composition together with calibrated branching. The dominant failures are drift, lost modes, and uncertainty that either collapses or explodes. Diagnose them with horizon curves from a common inferred start, comparing predicted events and state distributions with the real continuation.
  3. Closed-loop prediction: reality returns after each executed action. A robot or vehicle acts in the physical world, receives a new observation, and uses the posterior to correct its prior. This does not make dynamics irrelevant: the prior must bridge sensor intervals and provide a belief when objects are occluded. But it shifts the implementation requirement toward fast inference and robust posterior correction. Latency, sensor loss, or a mismatch between prior and posterior can dominate. Diagnose the loop by logging posterior innovation—the distance the new evidence moves belief—and checking whether large corrections align with genuine surprises rather than timestamp errors.
  4. Receding-horizon control: imagine many steps, execute only a short prefix, then correct and repeat. Model-predictive control deliberately combines open-loop imagination with closed-loop evidence. Its requirement is local accuracy over the chosen planning horizon, plus enough speed to replan before the world changes. The naive choice of a very short horizon can produce short-sighted plans; a long horizon or expensive model can make replanning too slow. Diagnose the tradeoff at fixed wall-clock budget by varying horizon and execution chunk, then measuring closed-loop cost rather than only predicted cost.
A linear way to choose the product
Ask how long the system must act without new trustworthy evidence → determine how many self-generated states it will consume → train and validate at least that composition depth → attach a validity horizon → shorten the open-loop interval when uncertainty or latency violates the envelope.

There is no honest claim that a model is simply “good at prediction” without naming both mode and horizon. A weather simulator generating months open loop, a robot replanning at 20 Hz, and an anomaly detector corrected every frame face different physical information constraints; they therefore need different transition stability, latency, and metrics.

6 · Multi-step objectives train the composition you will use

The direct response to exposure bias is to unroll the model during training. Starting from a real inferred state, roll under recorded actions and score several future steps:

Lroll = Σk=1H wk d(G(ẑt+k), yt+k)

G maps latent state to a target such as observation features, objects, reward, or events. Weights wk decide how much far horizons matter. Uniform weights emphasize long-run stability but can swamp local learning. Discounted weights align with control returns and acknowledge diminishing relevance, but may neglect rare late failures.

Latent overshooting avoids decoding every imagined step. Infer posterior states along the real sequence, start a prior rollout at time t, and require its k-step distribution to match the posterior at t+k:

Lover = ΣtΣk=1K αk KL(stopgrad[q(zt+k|o≤t+k)] || pθ(k)(zt+k|zt,at:t+k−1))

The k-step prior p(k) is formed by repeated transition. The destination posterior acts as an evidence-informed target; stopping its gradient prevents both sides from moving together merely to make the KL small. Overshooting directly trains the imagination path, though posterior latents can themselves be imperfect targets.

Scheduled sampling sometimes replaces a real previous input with the model’s output, with replacement probability increasing during training. This exposes the model to its mistakes, but creates an ambiguous objective: the target continuation came from the real prefix, while the sampled prefix may describe a different valid world. In multimodal domains, punishing that alternative continuation can bias the model back toward averages. Scheduled sampling is safest when perturbations are small and future alignment remains semantically valid.

State perturbation and recovery add bounded noise or adversarial deviations around inferred states, then train the transition or posterior to return to plausible trajectories. This expands the supervised tube around the data manifold without pretending a large wrong branch shares the original target. Perturbations must respect state geometry; arbitrary pixel noise may teach denoising artifacts rather than dynamical recovery.

Constraint and invariant losses penalize mass creation, identity switches, interpenetration, map violations, or impossible termination. They restrict where errors can travel. Hard constraints can stabilize rollout but become harmful when the encoded rule is approximate or incomplete; soft penalties expose the tradeoff and allow data to override the prior.

7 · Objective and architecture tradeoffs

Long-horizon pixel loss is dense but unforgiving. A one-frame phase shift makes every moving pixel wrong even when object paths, collision risk, and reward are correct. The resulting gradient can encourage blur. Feature, object, event, reward, or occupancy losses are more tolerant and decision-aligned, but can miss visual details needed for new tasks. Multi-scale systems often predict compact task state at long horizons and render pixels only for short horizons.

Autoregressive models naturally train with teacher forcing because each true token is available. Free-running token errors can cascade within a frame and through time. Masked blocks, span corruption, or rollout fine-tuning expose imperfect contexts at extra compute. Diffusion trajectory models predict an entire horizon jointly, avoiding stepwise temporal exposure bias, but fixed horizon and iterative sampling complicate long or interactive plans. RSSMs make recurrent rollout cheap and latent overshooting natural, yet their compressed state can conceal slow drift.

Direct multi-step models map (zt,at:t+H−1) to selected future horizons without applying one transition repeatedly. They can reduce compounding for a fixed query, but lose a reusable one-step simulator and may be inconsistent across overlapping horizons. Hybrid systems use a recurrent core plus direct event/value heads at longer timescales.

Horizon curriculum is often practical: learn reliable one-step grounding, then lengthen unrolls as the induced state distribution becomes meaningful. Starting with very long unrolls produces noisy gradients through an untrained transition. Stopping at short unrolls yields the original exposure problem. The curriculum should be driven by validation curves, not epoch count: extend when current-horizon rollouts remain within a calibrated reliability envelope.

8 · A linear training procedure for reliable rollouts

  1. Specify use mode and maximum useful horizon. Decide whether the product runs open loop, re-observes every step, or replans periodically. Choose state, event, and risk targets accordingly.
  2. Train and validate one-step inference first. Verify action timing, posterior reconstruction, prior prediction, and stochastic calibration. Multi-step losses cannot repair a broken data pipeline.
  3. Create a burn-in context. Feed several real observations to form (ht,zt). Do not score rollout before the state contains velocity and hidden context.
  4. Remove observation correction. For the rollout segment, use only prior samples and recorded actions. Accidentally feeding future encodings defeats the test.
  5. Score multiple representations. Combine latent overshooting with selected object, geometry, reward, continuation, event, and short-horizon perceptual losses. Log each by horizon.
  6. Add small on-manifold perturbations. Train recovery from errors similar to those the model actually produces. Refresh the perturbation distribution as the model changes.
  7. Increase horizon gradually. Balance gradient stability and memory with chunking or truncated backpropagation, while ensuring dependencies longer than the truncation have another training signal.
  8. Stratify difficult transitions. Sample contacts, occlusions, mode switches, rare terminations, and high-curvature actions often enough to learn them. Correct probability priors if sampling is rebalanced.
  9. Fine-tune on policy-induced states carefully. Collect real trajectories from the current planner or a safe exploratory policy. Pure model-generated states have no guaranteed correct target.
  10. Freeze an untouched rollout test. Evaluate horizons and action regimes not used for curriculum decisions, including controlled distribution shifts.

Memory grows with unroll length. Gradient checkpointing, mixed precision, latent-only heads, and randomly sampled start/horizon pairs reduce cost. But systems optimizations must not silently reintroduce teacher forcing—for example, by recomputing each future latent from the real frame to save memory.

9 · Evaluate curves and decisions, not a single horizon

Start from the same observation-inferred belief and produce two paths: a posterior-filtered path that receives each real future observation, and a prior-only path that receives only actions. Their gap isolates transition drift from encoder and decoder error. Repeat with multiple stochastic samples.

Plot metrics at every horizon: latent or feature error; object position and identity; event precision, recall, and timing; reward and return error; constraint violation; negative log-likelihood; interval coverage; branch-mode recall; and epistemic disagreement. Include uncertainty-error correlation: unsupported rollouts should become uncertain before they become confidently wrong.

Evaluate action sequences from three sources. Dataset actions measure interpolation under the collection policy. Random or perturbed actions test local intervention coverage. Planner-selected actions expose model exploitation, because optimization searches for precisely those states where errors look beneficial. A model that succeeds only under dataset actions is not ready to guide a new policy.

Finally measure downstream regret. Let the model choose an action plan, execute it in a trusted simulator or the real system under safety controls, and compare achieved return or cost with a stronger oracle. Prediction metrics are proxies; decision error is the product-level outcome.

Metric trap
Next-frame PSNR mostly rewards copying appearance. A credible report shows error, event coverage, calibration, and constraints versus rollout horizon; separates posterior-corrected from prior-only paths; tests planner-selected actions; and states where uncertainty makes exact point prediction no longer meaningful.

10 · Failure modes and diagnostics

A horizon curve tells us when a rollout fails. The shape and location of the failure tell us why. Read each pattern as a short chain from observation to mechanism to discriminating test to repair.

  1. Step one is good, then the rollout drifts smoothly. This is the signature predicted by ek+1≈Jkek+δk: a systematic bias is repeatedly injected, an expansive Jacobian amplifies existing error, or both occur. Fit the observed error to that recurrence and inspect bias direction and local sensitivity. Then add multi-step loss, train recovery from realistic perturbations, and improve missing action or timing inputs. The repair should flatten the error-growth curve, not merely reduce its first point.
  2. Prediction is smooth until contact, then fails suddenly. A contact boundary changes the governing mode from free motion to sticking, sliding, bouncing, or separation. A single smooth transition missed that discrete boundary. Align error plots to collision or contact onset rather than averaging over clock time. Add a mode variable or contact head, oversample transition events, and impose structured constraints where trustworthy. Verify not just lower error but correct branch selection immediately after contact.
  3. The rollout freezes. A conditional mean can average opposite motions to zero, or overly contractive dynamics can pull every state toward a static attractor. The video appears stable, so a naive stability metric may reward it. Compare velocity and motion statistics, action responsiveness, and branch entropy with data. Use a probabilistic objective, add velocity or task heads, and reduce smoothing pressure. Counterfactually change one action from the same start belief; the predicted world must move differently for the right causal reason.
  4. The rollout becomes noisy. Independent noise may be sampled at each step, causing identity and intent to flicker, or the model may inflate variance to hide bias. Track object identity and invariants within each sample, then compare predicted scale with actual residual by horizon. Introduce a persistent scenario latent and coherent sampling when the branch changes arbitrarily; use the likelihood’s scale penalty and improve the mean model when variance merely masks error.
  5. Objects fade away gradually. Under pixel loss, an uncertain object at several possible locations can be made less wrong on average by becoming transparent everywhere. The mechanism is averaging, not physical disappearance. Measure object count, existence probability, and permanence under occlusion. Add object or occupancy heads, represent existence explicitly, and use a multimodal density. A correct repair keeps an object present within each branch even when branches disagree about its position.
  6. Overshooting KL falls while downstream tasks worsen. The prior and posterior may have discovered an easy agreement: both collapse to an uninformative code. The numerical alignment objective improves because there is nothing left to disagree about. Measure posterior information, decoder and task probes, and per-dimension KL. Check stop-gradient placement, retain grounding losses, and reserve an information budget. The diagnostic criterion is joint: later priors approach later posteriors and those posteriors still encode predictive facts.
  7. Recorded-action rollouts are excellent, but planned actions fail. The collection policy did not cover the interventions selected by the optimizer, which searches for precisely the model errors that look rewarding. Compare three evaluations from matched start states: dataset actions, locally perturbed actions, and optimized actions. If performance degrades in that order, add a support penalty, use a conservative planner, and collect targeted real trajectories safely. Do not manufacture labels by trusting unsupported model rollouts.
  8. Belief oscillates after every new observation. The prior repeatedly predicts one state and the posterior repeatedly snaps to another. This can indicate prior–posterior mismatch, an unmodeled delay, or sensor timestamps aligned to the wrong action. Plot the innovation q−p alongside action and observation timestamps. Correct alignment first; then train correction dynamics or represent delay explicitly. A repaired system should still react strongly to genuine surprises but should not make rhythmic corrections in routine motion.

A rollout can therefore be visually stable for the wrong reason: the transition may collapse toward a static attractor. Stability must be paired with responsiveness to actions and correct variation. The reliable diagnostic pattern is always the same: isolate a physical regime, change one causal factor while holding the start belief fixed, and check whether both the predicted state and its uncertainty respond in the direction the real system does.

11 · Re-observation is an algorithmic component

A deployed agent does not need to hallucinate indefinitely. At time t, it filters the latest observation into a posterior belief, imagines candidate action sequences for H steps, executes the first action or short prefix, receives a new observation, corrects belief, and replans. This model-predictive-control loop converts the demand for perfect long simulation into four tractable requirements: accurate local transitions, calibrated uncertainty, fast batched imagination, and robust correction.

Replanning frequency trades model error against compute and actuator smoothness. Shorter execution chunks reduce drift but may create myopic or jittery behavior. Longer chunks save compute and permit coordinated maneuvers but trust the model farther. Adaptive horizons are useful: plan farther in familiar, low-uncertainty regimes and shorten or invoke a fallback when epistemic disagreement rises.

Posterior correction is not free. Sensors have latency, occlusion, failure, and bandwidth constraints. The training pipeline should simulate missing observations and delayed measurements so the filter learns how much to trust its prior. A system evaluated with perfect frame-by-frame correction but deployed with intermittent sensors has been tested as a different product.

12 · Chaos imposes an information horizon

Some dynamics are sensitive to initial conditions even with a perfect model. If initial uncertainty has size ε0 and expands roughly as ε(t)≈ε0eλt for positive Lyapunov exponent λ, the time until uncertainty reaches acceptable tolerance εmax is

Hinfo ≈ log(εmax/ε0) / λ

Scaling the network may reduce model error but cannot recover information absent from sensors. Beyond this information horizon, the correct target changes from exact state to a distribution over coarse outcomes, conserved quantities, reachable sets, or risk. A weather model can lose the exact location of a vortex while retaining useful regional statistics. A manipulation model can lose pixel alignment while preserving whether an object remains graspable.

This distinction prevents an endless chase for sharp long-horizon video. Sometimes blur or branching reflects honest uncertainty; sometimes it reflects a weak model. Calibration, added sensing, controlled repeat experiments, and invariant metrics help tell them apart.

13 · System-design implications

Attach a validity horizon to every rollout. The model service should report horizon-dependent uncertainty, support, and calibration, not return an unqualified trajectory. Planners should reject queries beyond the validated envelope.

Optimize latency and accuracy together. A slower, more accurate model can be worse if it forces less frequent replanning. Compare closed-loop performance at fixed wall-clock and compute budgets, not only fixed imagined steps.

Keep posterior innovations observable. Log how far each new observation moves belief from the prior. Growing innovations are an early warning of drift, distribution shift, timing faults, or broken sensors.

Separate fast and slow state. Fast contact dynamics may require fine steps, while intent or map context evolves slowly. Multi-rate transitions reduce long-horizon compute and prevent thousands of recurrent updates from unnecessarily rewriting stable context.

Protect against optimizer exploitation. Penalize uncertainty and action distance from data, validate candidate plans with ensembles or higher-fidelity models, and retain hard safety constraints outside the learned reward. More planning optimization can worsen behavior when the model is imperfect.

Close the data loop. Store high-innovation states, planner disagreements, near violations, and fallback activations. Review and collect those regimes in the real environment, then re-evaluate on a frozen test before expanding the operating envelope.

14 · Bridge: accurate action effects require interventions

We now know how to measure and mitigate self-composition error under a supplied action sequence. But we have quietly assumed that the learned relation between action and next state represents what the action causes. Logged trajectories do not guarantee this. A behavior policy chooses actions from its observations; hidden hazards influence both that action and the outcome. A model may learn “braking predicts collision” because drivers brake when collision is already likely, then incorrectly imagine that braking causes the collision.

Lesson 07 separates passive association from intervention. It asks what action diversity, randomized exploration, causal structure, and counterfactual tests are required before a transition can support planning rather than merely replay the behavior policy’s correlations.

Takeaway
Teacher forcing measures transitions on clean data states; open-loop use applies them to model-induced states. Error follows ek+1≈Jkek+δk, so stability and recovery matter alongside one-step fit. Train prior-only multi-step behavior, overshoot toward later posteriors, perturb states realistically, evaluate horizon curves and planner-selected actions, and re-observe whenever the product allows. Beyond the information horizon, predict distributions and decision-relevant abstractions rather than pretend exact futures remain knowable.

Interview prompts

  • What is exposure bias in a world model? Training conditions on observation-inferred data states, while free rollout conditions on the model’s own imperfect states, creating an input-distribution shift.
  • What does ek+1≈Jkek+δk tell us? Existing error is transformed by local dynamics, while new one-step error is injected; expansive Jacobian directions amplify both over time.
  • What is latent overshooting? Roll the prior several steps from an earlier state and match its destination distribution to the later observation-informed posterior.
  • Why can scheduled sampling be biased in multimodal worlds? A sampled prefix may describe a valid world different from the recorded continuation, yet the method still forces it toward that original target.
  • Why can long-horizon pixel loss be misleading? Small phase shifts dominate pixels and encourage averaging even when objects, events, rewards, and decisions remain correct.
  • How does receding-horizon control reduce model burden? It imagines only far enough to choose a short action prefix, then uses a real observation to correct belief and replans.
  • How would you test for model exploitation? Evaluate planner-optimized action sequences, compare with dataset and perturbed actions, and validate outcomes in a trusted simulator or real system under safety controls.
  • What is an information horizon? The point beyond which initial uncertainty and intrinsic sensitivity make exact state prediction unresolvable; beyond it the model should predict distributions or coarser decision-relevant quantities.
