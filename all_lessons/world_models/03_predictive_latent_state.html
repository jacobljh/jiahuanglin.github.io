Learning a predictive latent state

Compression is not a smaller photograph. It is a decision about which histories may safely become indistinguishable—and which future-changing distinctions must survive.

Dependency recap and linear route
Lesson 01 defined a world model as state inference plus action-conditioned dynamics. Lesson 02 showed that the ideal internal state is a posterior belief over hidden worlds. That posterior is intractably large. We will now derive a compact predictive representation, trace what different losses retain in one running example, analyze collapse and architecture, and end with a measurable state-design procedure.

1 · Why compression is unavoidable

A 256 × 256 RGB image contains 196,608 channel values. At 30 frames per second, one minute contains more than 350 million observed values before adding depth, audio, touch, proprioception, or actions. Storing the sequence is not the same as understanding it. A future query usually depends on far fewer causes: object pose, velocity, identity, support, articulation, intent, camera state, and uncertainty.

We therefore introduce an encoder or state estimator E that maps the history Ht = (o1:t, a1:t−1) into a learned latent state xt:

xt = E(Ht)     and     x̂t+1 ∼ F(xt, at)

The word latent simply means not directly observed. It does not imply that the vector is mysterious, uninterpretable, continuous, or neural. A latent may be a dense vector, a spatial feature grid, discrete codes, object slots, a scene graph, particles, or a mixture of named geometry and learned features. The important question is what information its representation preserves.

Compression creates a many-to-one map. Many observation histories will share one latent. That is desirable when their differences are sensor noise, texture, or viewpoint details irrelevant to the promised queries. It is destructive when their differences are velocity, contact state, or hidden intent that changes a future under some action. Learning a state is therefore learning an equivalence relation over histories.

2 · Derive predictive sufficiency from history equivalence

Take two histories H and H′. Suppose the encoder maps both to the same latent: E(H)=E(H′). The downstream model can no longer tell which history occurred. Merging them is safe only if every relevant future query has the same distribution for both histories under every candidate action sequence in scope:

E(H)=E(H′) ⇒ p(yfuture | H, afuture, κ) = p(yfuture | H′, afuture, κ)

yfuture is the target—perhaps occupancy, reward, pixels, contact, or termination. afuture is a possible action sequence. κ identifies the class of questions the system promises to answer. This implication is predictive sufficiency relative to a query set.

It yields two complementary representation goals:

  • Sufficiency: retain every distinction that changes a required future distribution.
  • Minimality or invariance: discard distinctions that do not change those distributions.

A representation can be sufficient but not minimal: it may preserve object motion and every pixel of background wallpaper. It can be minimal but insufficient: it may store only the object class and discard velocity. The ideal is a compact sufficient statistic, but finite data, unknown future tasks, and optimization make the tradeoff approximate.

There is also no task-independent definition of nuisance. Paint color is nuisance for avoiding a rigid box but state for quality inspection. Fine texture may be irrelevant to short-horizon rigid motion but essential for friction estimation or relighting. Before choosing a bottleneck, define the query distribution, horizon, action space, and acceptable error.

3 · Running example: one image, six factors, three queries

Consider 64 × 64 videos of a small square moving across a textured floor. Each frame is generated from six factors:

  1. horizontal position x,
  2. horizontal velocity v,
  3. square identity i—fragile glass or solid rubber,
  4. floor texture τ,
  5. illumination brightness ℓ, and
  6. independent camera noise n.

An action a ∈ {push left, no push, push right} changes velocity by −1, 0, or +1 pixel per step. Position then follows xt+1 = xt + vt+1. A collision at the right wall breaks glass but makes rubber bounce. We ask three possible query sets:

  • Q1: reconstruct the current frame. Texture, lighting, and perhaps camera noise strongly affect pixel error.
  • Q2: predict position for the next five actions. Position and velocity matter; stable texture and identity may not until a wall interaction occurs.
  • Q3: plan a push without breaking the object. Position, velocity, and identity matter; texture, illumination, and fresh camera noise do not.

Suppose current position is 55, velocity is +2 pixels/step, and the right wall is at 63. Compare action sequences. With no push for three steps, positions are 57, 59, 61. A right push first changes velocity to +3, producing positions 58, 61, then 64: the wall is hit on step three. A left push first changes velocity to +1, producing 56, 57, 58. If identity is glass, the right push is unsafe; if rubber, the hit may be acceptable or useful.

Now consider an encoder with a severe three-scalar capacity. If training minimizes current-frame mean-squared pixel error and the textured background occupies 95% of pixels, it may allocate capacity to τ and ℓ, then store approximate x. It can reconstruct most pixels well while discarding velocity and identity. Two sequences ending at x=55—one moving right at +2, one left at −2—receive similar latents even though their rollouts differ. Q1 is served; Q2 and Q3 fail.

If training instead minimizes five-step position error under varied pushes, the representation is pressured to retain x and v. The third scalar might store identity only if training includes wall interactions and a loss that distinguishes break from bounce. Otherwise identity is predictive neither of the short observed motion nor of the objective and will be discarded. The dataset’s action and event coverage therefore decides whether the learning signal ever reveals a factor’s relevance.

This example makes an important point: capacity alone does not choose the right state. A three-scalar latent is enough for Q3 in principle if it stores x,v,i. A 1,024-dimensional latent can still fail if the objective rewards texture shortcuts and the training trajectories never reach walls.

4 · Reconstruction learns an observation code

An autoencoder maps observation ot to latent xt and decodes ôt. A typical squared reconstruction loss is:

Lrecon = average over pixels of (ot − ôt)²

Every term says: penalize the squared difference between a true pixel channel and its decoded value, then average. This objective has useful properties. It provides dense supervision without labels, pressures the latent to retain broad scene information, and yields a decoder that supports visualization. It is appropriate when observation generation itself belongs to the query contract, as in controllable video or simulation.

But reconstruction weights information by its contribution to the chosen pixel likelihood. Large textured regions can dominate small moving objects. Random details are expensive to reproduce even when they cannot be predicted or controlled. Under multiple possible futures, a deterministic squared-error decoder tends toward their conditional mean, producing blur. A more expressive stochastic decoder can model multiple modes, but the state may still waste capacity on appearance.

Reconstructing future observations is stronger than reconstructing the present because it pressures the latent to retain dynamics. Yet it still entangles predictable semantics with rendering. This is where knowledge from the Computer Graphics track helps: pixels result from geometry, materials, lighting, and camera. A model asked to render must retain or regenerate those factors. A model asked only for collision need not.

5 · Contrastive objectives learn distinctions through negatives

A contrastive predictive objective takes a context representation, predicts an embedding of the future, and asks that prediction to score the true future higher than negative examples. The signal is relational: “this future belongs to this context more than those alternatives do.”

This avoids spending decoder capacity on every pixel and can emphasize high-level regularities. However, the negative distribution defines what distinctions are useful. If negatives come from different videos with different floor textures, the easiest solution may be to match texture rather than motion. If all negatives share the same video but occur at nearby times, position and temporal detail become more valuable. False negatives—different observations that are actually equivalent for the query—push together-apart decisions in the wrong direction.

Design questions include where negatives come from, how many are used, which augmentations remove nuisance, and whether the score can exploit timestamps, borders, camera identity, or compression artifacts. Contrastive loss does not magically discover causal state; it makes selected classification distinctions cheap to learn.

6 · Predictive embedding avoids pixel decoding—but must avoid collapse

A joint-embedding objective predicts the target encoder’s representation of a future observation or masked region:

Lembed = distance(P(xcontext, a), stopgrad(xtarget))

P is a predictor; the target is produced by another encoder; distance might compare directions or component differences. stopgrad means the target side is treated as fixed for this update. Because no pixel decoder is required, the target embedding can discard unpredictable texture and emphasize features that remain stable or predictable.

The trivial solution is collapse: map every input to the same constant. The predictor then matches perfectly without encoding the world. Systems prevent this using one or more asymmetries:

  • a frozen or slowly moving teacher supplies targets that cannot instantly chase the predictor;
  • stop-gradient updates only one side through the matching loss;
  • variance constraints require each embedding dimension to vary across a batch;
  • covariance penalties discourage dimensions from copying one another;
  • contrastive negatives force distinct examples apart;
  • quantization or balanced code use prevents all inputs choosing one code;
  • auxiliary reconstruction or task heads demand recoverable information.

Collapse can be complete or partial. A representation may have healthy numerical variance yet vary only with camera brightness. Training loss can still be low if brightness predicts the target data. Detecting semantic collapse requires controlled probes and interventions, not just singular values or loss curves.

7 · Task- and value-equivalent representations preserve decisions

If the model exists for a fixed control task, training can preserve rewards, values, policies, or search results rather than the entire observation. Two latent states are value-equivalent when the available action sequences have the same expected return from both, even if their pixels and physical details differ.

In the square example, floor texture and brightness can be discarded for “avoid breaking glass.” Position, velocity, and identity remain because they change which pushes are safe. This can produce an extremely efficient planning representation.

The cost is task blindness. If a later task asks the robot to inspect the floor texture, the representation may have irreversibly removed it. Reward itself can also be sparse: before any trajectory reaches the wall, identity does not affect observed return, so the encoder receives no reason to preserve it. Multi-task targets, auxiliary predictive losses, coverage of consequential events, or a larger general-purpose state can hedge against future task changes.

8 · Combine objectives by assigning each a role

A loss is not a descriptive label placed on a model after training. It is the pressure that decides which mistakes consume capacity. To understand a hybrid objective, imagine presenting the same history to the model and ask, one loss at a time, exactly what gradient would follow:

  1. Current reconstruction asks, “Can you recover what the sensor sees now?” Every image region supplies dense supervision, so the representation tends to retain broad observation detail and the decoder gives a useful visual diagnostic. But nothing in this question demands motion. If texture dominates pixel count, the latent can become nuisance-heavy while velocity disappears. Diagnose it with identical last frames reached through opposite motion histories.
  2. Future reconstruction adds, “Can you render what the sensor will see later?” Because the target lies ahead in time, representation and dynamics must cooperate, which is stronger than copying the present. The failed naive idea is to equate rendered quality with useful state: appearance may still dominate, multiple futures may blur under a point loss, and generating full observations may be too expensive for planning. Check structured outcomes separately from pixels.
  3. Contrastive prediction asks, “Which candidate future belongs to this context?” Removing the decoder can produce strong discriminative features without reconstructing every channel. Yet the negative sampler writes the hidden curriculum. Easy negatives distinguished by texture, timestamp, or camera identity reward shortcuts; false negatives punish two futures that are equivalent for the intended query. Change the negative distribution and see whether the learned state changes for the right reason.
  4. Predictive embedding asks, “Can you match an abstract target feature?” The target encoder may discard unpredictable low-level detail, allowing the state to focus on stable structure. The apparent shortcut is catastrophic: if every input receives the same feature, matching becomes trivial. Stop-gradient, a teacher, variance control, or related asymmetry prevents full collapse, but partial collapse and teacher bias remain. Probe decision variables rather than trusting feature variance alone.
  5. Inverse or action prediction asks, “Which action connected these states?” To answer, the representation must emphasize controllable change, a valuable correction when passive appearance dominates other losses. It can still ignore passive dynamics—motion that occurs without agent action—and may read a visible actuator trace instead of understanding the state change. Remove such traces and test exogenous motion.
  6. Reward, value, and task heads ask, “Which information changes the decision?” They can produce an efficient state for a known objective because facts irrelevant to return need not consume capacity. That same efficiency is the boundary: a fact irrelevant to the training task is discarded even if a later task needs it. Evaluate transfer under changed rewards rather than assuming a task state is a general world state.
  7. Geometry, flow, and depth heads ask for structured spatial quantities. Their gradients ground motion and viewpoint reasoning in variables that many downstream queries reuse. The supervision is not free: it requires labels, auxiliary estimators, or geometric assumptions, each of which can be biased. Validate the geometry under camera motion and calibration shift rather than treating an auxiliary target as truth.

The list is a causal map of complementary failures. Current reconstruction supplies breadth but not motion; future prediction adds time but may retain rendering burden; action prediction emphasizes control but misses passive change; task heads focus decisions but narrow transfer; geometric heads add structure but bring assumptions. Hybrid training is common because one pressure can repair another’s blind spot. A low-weight decoder can prevent broad information loss, a future-feature loss can emphasize predictable semantics, an inverse head can emphasize controllability, and reward or termination heads can expose task relevance.

However, the naive implementation—simply add all scalar losses—does not guarantee that every role survives. Their scales, gradient directions, data frequency, and head capacity determine what the shared encoder actually learns.

Loss weights should be treated as resource-allocation decisions. Measure per-objective gradient magnitude and downstream probes, not only the total scalar loss. A powerful private head can sometimes solve its target without improving shared state; conversely, a dominant dense pixel loss can drown out a rare collision target.

Linear objective audit
For each loss, name the physical distinction it should preserve, construct a pair of examples differing only in that distinction, verify the shared latent changes, and then test a nuisance-only pair that should remain equivalent. If the head succeeds while the shared state fails these pairs, the objective is being solved through a shortcut or private head rather than building the intended world state.

9 · The bottleneck has more than one dial

Latent dimension is only the most visible capacity control. The effective bottleneck also includes:

  • Temporal sampling: a coarse stride saves compute but can alias fast motion and contact.
  • Spatial pooling: global vectors gain invariance but lose metric location; feature maps retain layout at higher cost.
  • Tokenization: patches, objects, voxels, or learned codes determine which relations are easy to express.
  • Quantization: discrete codes stabilize and compress but introduce codebook limits and discontinuities.
  • Stochastic noise or regularization: limits information throughput and can encourage smooth organization, but too much erases rare details.
  • Attention window and memory update: decide whether old evidence remains accessible.
  • Decoder access: skip connections can bypass the latent, producing good reconstructions while state remains poor.
  • Training horizon: one-step loss favors local shortcuts; longer horizons emphasize slow variables but are harder to optimize.

An architecture is therefore an inductive prior over which facts are cheap to encode. A convolutional grid makes locality and translation sharing cheap. Object slots make instance persistence and pairwise interaction cheap, but segmentation and slot identity hard. A transformer makes flexible relations cheap but exact metric geometry and long memory expensive unless structure is added. A 3D voxel or point representation aligns with geometric queries but needs pose and calibration. The right choice follows the invariances and equivariances in the task.

10 · Invariance versus equivariance

It is tempting to demand invariance to every nuisance: if the camera rotates, keep the same representation. But some transformations should change state in a predictable way rather than disappear. This is equivariance. A two-meter translation of an object should translate its spatial representation by two meters; it should not leave a collision map unchanged.

Separate coordinate choices clarify the issue:

  • An image-centric feature map moves when the camera moves. It aligns naturally with pixels but entangles ego motion and object motion.
  • An ego-centric map keeps the agent at the origin and transforms the world as the agent moves. It is convenient for local control.
  • A world-centric map anchors persistent objects in a shared frame. It supports mapping but depends on localization accuracy.
  • An object-centric representation factors each entity’s pose and attributes. It supports interactions but must discover and maintain identity.

The 3D Vision lessons explain projection, pose, multiview geometry, and reconstruction; those concepts supply useful structure when a world model must reason across camera motion. Purely learned features can work, but geometric equivariance often improves data efficiency and interpretability.

11 · Preserve belief, not just a best estimate

Lesson 02 established that the ideal state is a distribution. A deterministic latent vector can still encode distribution parameters—means, variances, mixture weights, or evidence—but the training targets must require them. If a decoder is rewarded only for one best reconstruction, the encoder may drop losing hypotheses.

Three common strategies are:

  1. Parametric uncertainty: the latent or head outputs a Gaussian, categorical distribution, or mixture. Efficient, but only as expressive as the chosen family.
  2. Stochastic latent state: factor learned state as x=(h,z) and sample z from a learned posterior during training and from a transition prior during imagination. This represents branching futures, but the stochastic component may be ignored if the decoder is too strong.
  3. Ensembles or particles: maintain multiple model or state hypotheses. They expose disagreement and multimodality at additional compute cost.

A good representation should expand uncertainty during unobserved or chaotic evolution and contract it when informative evidence arrives. If every future sample differs only in texture while all share the same wrong trajectory, visual diversity is not decision-relevant uncertainty.

12 · Evaluate the representation, not only the end-to-end loss

A low training loss proves that the joint encoder–head system optimized its objective on its data. It does not prove that x contains the intended state. Use complementary tests:

  1. Frozen probes: freeze the encoder and predict position, velocity, identity, contact, action effect, reward, and uncertainty with deliberately simple heads. Easy linear readout indicates accessible organization, though failure may mean the probe is weak rather than information absent.
  2. Interventions: change background or lighting while holding dynamics fixed; then change velocity or action while holding appearance fixed. Latent distances and predictions should respond to the causal change, not the nuisance.
  3. Counterfactual pairs: construct histories with identical last frames and opposite hidden velocities. If their latents merge, memory is insufficient.
  4. Rollout tests: decode or probe imagined latents at increasing horizons. Measure drift, object permanence, constraint violations, and calibration.
  5. Nearest neighbors: inspect which samples become close in latent space. Texture-based neighbors expose shortcuts that aggregate metrics miss.
  6. Ablation and bottleneck sweeps: vary dimension, tokens, losses, and history. Look for the smallest state that preserves downstream performance, not merely the best reconstruction.
  7. Closed-loop evaluation: let a planner use the state. Compare reward, safety, and regret against model-free, oracle-state, and observation-only baselines.
A probe is evidence, not a verdict
A high-capacity nonlinear probe may recover information that the actual dynamics or planner cannot use. A low-capacity probe may miss information stored in a distributed form. Match probe complexity to the intended consumer, and pair probes with causal interventions and closed-loop outcomes.

13 · Common misconceptions and failure cases

  • “A perfect autoencoder has learned state.” It has preserved enough information to reconstruct observations. It may not organize velocity, action effects, or uncertainty for prediction.
  • “More dimensions cannot hurt.” Extra capacity enables nuisance shortcuts, increases dynamics burden, and lets the model memorize identifiers that fail under distribution shift.
  • “Predictive means semantic.” Timestamp, camera identity, or static texture can be highly predictive without representing controllable world structure.
  • “Augmentation always removes nuisances.” An augmentation may delete task state. Color jitter is safe for geometric navigation but harmful when traffic-light color determines action.
  • “Object-centric is automatically interpretable.” Slots can swap identity, split one object, merge several, or encode background. Persistence and correspondence require explicit testing.
  • “One-step accuracy implies usable rollouts.” The transition may rely on observation features unavailable after the first imagined step, causing latent drift and off-manifold states.
  • “No collapse because variance is nonzero.” Partial collapse can preserve only easy nuisance variation. Probe the variables that change decisions.
  • “A task-specific latent generalizes to new tasks.” Information discarded as reward-irrelevant during training cannot be recovered when objectives change.

14 · Practical system and interview reasoning

Given a prompt such as “design the latent state for a robot manipulation world model,” reason in this order:

  1. Write query contract: predict contact, object pose, success, and uncertainty for candidate end-effector commands over two seconds.
  2. List required factors: ego/gripper pose and velocity, object poses and shapes, articulation, support/contact, approximate material behavior, and occluded-object belief.
  3. List intended nuisances: sensor grain and lighting changes that do not affect material inference. State caveats rather than assuming all appearance is irrelevant.
  4. Choose structure: an ego-centric 3D feature field for free space plus object slots for persistent entities and a global recurrent token for unmodeled context.
  5. Choose objective roles: future feature prediction for dynamics, geometric/pose heads for grounding, contact and reward heads for decisions, and a modest decoder for diagnostics and broad information retention.
  6. Prevent degeneration: teacher/stop-gradient or contrastive separation, balanced slot use, rollout consistency, and action variation.
  7. Expose uncertainty: multimodal object states or stochastic transitions plus ensemble disagreement under unfamiliar interactions.
  8. Evaluate targeted distinctions: same image/opposite velocity, lighting swaps, long occlusion, novel pushes, contact events, calibration, and closed-loop planning.

A strong design acknowledges tradeoffs. Metric 3D state aids spatial planning but depends on localization and calibration. Object slots aid persistence but struggle with amorphous material and object discovery. A large transformer state is flexible but expensive to roll thousands of candidate action sequences. A compact task latent makes planning fast but limits transfer. The best architecture is the one whose inductive biases, compute, data, and outputs satisfy the stated contract.

15 · Explicit takeaway

Takeaway
A predictive latent is a lossy, query-relative contract. Histories may share a latent only when their relevant future distributions agree under the candidate actions in scope. Bottleneck, architecture, data coverage, and objective jointly decide which distinctions survive. Reconstruction, contrastive prediction, predictive embedding, geometry, and value losses preserve different worlds; validate the result with probes, interventions, rollout calibration, and closed-loop decisions.

This lesson defined what latent state should retain, but treated its transition F as a box. Lesson 04 opens that box: how priors, posteriors, deterministic memory, stochastic variables, and sequence architectures learn latent dynamics.

Interview prompts

  • What is predictive sufficiency? If two histories share a latent, they must induce the same distributions over every relevant future query under the candidate actions in scope.
  • Why can a perfect autoencoder be a poor world model? Pixel reconstruction may allocate capacity to appearance while failing to organize hidden velocity, action effects, or uncertainty for dynamics.
  • How do predictive-embedding methods avoid constant collapse? They use asymmetry such as stop-gradient or a slow teacher, plus variance, covariance, negatives, quantization, or auxiliary objectives.
  • Why do negative samples matter in contrastive learning? They define which distinctions solve the classification task and can reward semantic state or shortcuts such as texture and timestamp.
  • When is a value-equivalent representation attractive? For a stable known task where compact planning state matters more than preserving facts irrelevant to return.
  • What is the difference between invariance and equivariance? An invariant feature ignores a transformation; an equivariant state changes in a known corresponding way, as a spatial map should under translation.
  • How would you detect a texture shortcut? Swap texture while holding dynamics fixed, inspect latent changes and nearest neighbors, then change dynamics while holding texture fixed.
  • Why is latent width not the whole bottleneck? Temporal stride, tokens, spatial pooling, noise, quantization, attention, skip paths, horizon, and gradient routing also limit effective information.
