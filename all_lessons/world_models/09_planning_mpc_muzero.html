Planning in latent worlds

Planning asks the model a bounded question now: which candidate action sequence reaches the goal with acceptable risk? Search depth, rollout width, model bias, and real-time latency become one budget.

1 · Planning is constrained inference over future actions

A world model answers a conditional question: given current belief bt and proposed actions at:t+H−1, what distribution of future states and outcomes follows? A planner wraps an optimizer around that answer. It chooses the action sequence with the best predicted utility while respecting action, state, and compute constraints.

at:t+H−1* = arg minat:t+H−1 ∈ A E[Σk=0H−1 γk c(ŝt+k,at+k) + γH cterminal(ŝt+H)]

Read it term by term. H is planning horizon. A is the set of permitted sequences, including bounds such as steering rate or joint torque. c(s,a) is immediate cost—negative reward if one prefers maximization. γ discounts distant cost. ŝ marks model-predicted state. The expectation averages stochastic dynamics, observation uncertainty, or sampled futures. cterminal estimates cost after the finite horizon. Without it, a planner can postpone a crash until one step beyond what it sees.

This objective hides four separate design decisions: how to represent current state; how to predict consequences; how to score risk and goals; and how to search possible actions under a time budget. “Use a world model for planning” is not an algorithm until each decision is specified.

2 · Model predictive control closes the prediction–correction loop

Open-loop planning would optimize a complete sequence once and execute all of it. Every model error then persists. Model predictive control, or MPC, instead uses a receding horizon. At time t, it solves an H-step problem, executes the first action—or a short safe prefix—receives a new observation, updates belief, shifts the horizon, and solves again.

receding-horizon MPC repeat at control time t: bₜ ← state_estimator(history, newest_observation) Uₜ ← optimize_sequences(world_model, bₜ, objective, constraints, budget) execute first action aₜ = Uₜ[0] observe reality; update history warm-start next search with shift(Uₜ)

The first-action rule is not waste. The remaining actions were conditional commitments under a predicted future. Once reality supplies evidence, discarding or revising them is rational. Replanning turns feedback into a model-error correction mechanism. Disturbance pushes a drone sideways; a camera reveals an obstacle was closer than estimated; tire friction differs from the latent belief. The next optimization starts from the corrected state.

MPC does not make model quality irrelevant. Between corrections, the controller still needs reliable local action effects; some failures happen faster than the observation/control rate; and an optimizer can exploit model error within even a short horizon. But closed-loop correction permits a locally accurate model to solve tasks that would defeat a long open-loop rollout.

3 · Random shooting establishes the baseline

For continuous control, the most direct optimizer is random shooting. Sample N complete action sequences from a proposal distribution, roll each through the model, compute its cost, and select the lowest-cost sequence. It needs no derivative and parallelizes well across an accelerator.

random_shooting(bₜ, horizon H, samples N): for i = 1 ... N in parallel: Uᵢ = (aᵢ,0 ... aᵢ,H−1) ~ proposal trajectoryᵢ ~ world_model(bₜ, Uᵢ) Jᵢ = risk_adjusted_cost(trajectoryᵢ, Uᵢ) return U_argmin(J)

Random shooting is a strong sanity baseline because its failure is interpretable. If good trajectories occupy a substantial fraction of proposal space, enough samples find one. If actions are d-dimensional and horizon is H, however, search lives in roughly dH dimensions. The fraction of useful sequences can shrink exponentially with horizon. Uniform motor noise also generates jittery, dynamically implausible controls. Smooth correlated noise, action-repeat, splines, a model-free policy proposal, or a shifted previous plan can concentrate samples on realistic sequences.

Never compare optimizers without equalizing model evaluations and wall-clock latency. An apparently better method may simply execute ten times more simulated transitions. Report both the achieved objective and the cost of obtaining it.

4 · CEM learns where to sample during the current decision

The cross-entropy method, CEM, improves shooting by adapting its proposal distribution. For bounded continuous actions, start with a factorized or temporally correlated Gaussian over sequence parameters. Sample candidates, keep the best K elites, fit new mean and variance to them, and repeat for several iterations. The word “cross-entropy” refers to fitting the proposal toward the elite distribution; this is optimization, not the supervised classification loss that happens to share the name.

μj+1 = α μj + (1−α) mean(Uelite) σ²j+1 = α σ²j + (1−α) var(Uelite)

j indexes CEM iterations. μ and σ² are proposal parameters at every action time and dimension. The elite set contains candidates with lowest cost. Smoothing α ∈ [0,1) prevents abrupt collapse. At deployment, bounds can be handled by truncation, clipping, or sampling an unconstrained variable passed through tanh.

CEM(bₜ, H, N, elite_count K, iterations L): (μ, σ) ← warm_start_or_default(H) repeat L times: sample N action sequences Uᵢ ~ TruncatedNormal(μ, σ) evaluate risk-adjusted predicted cost Jᵢ from bₜ E ← K sequences with smallest J μ ← smooth(μ, mean(E)); σ ← smooth(σ, std(E)) enforce minimum σ to preserve search diversity return best evaluated sequence, or μ

CEM is derivative-free, tolerates discontinuous collision penalties, and directs later samples toward promising regions. It can still converge prematurely to one mode, miss narrow alternatives, or exploit a model hole with high confidence. An elite fraction that is too large barely concentrates; too small makes updates noisy. A minimum variance, multiple proposal modes, restarts, and uncertainty-aware costs help.

5 · Worked example: CEM steers around an obstacle

Consider a planar point robot at (x,y)=(0,0). It must reach goal (4,0) in four actions. Each action is a two-dimensional displacement limited to length one. A circular obstacle of radius 0.7 sits at (2,0). Define cost:

J(U) = ‖p4−g‖² + 20 Σk=14 𝟙[‖pk−(2,0)‖ < 0.7] + 0.1 Σk=14 ‖ak‖²

The first term is squared goal error. The second adds 20 for every predicted state inside the obstacle. The third discourages excess control. Start with a Gaussian proposal whose mean points straight right: μ0,k=(1,0), standard deviation σ0,k=(0.5,0.5). Random shooting from this distribution often collides because its centerline goes through the obstacle.

Suppose iteration one draws 64 sequences and retains the best eight. Four happen to go above the obstacle and four below it. If the proposal is a single Gaussian, their vertical components can average back toward zero—a concrete multimodality failure. Random tie-breaking or slight noise may make the top route win: the elite vertical means become (0.55, 0.45, −0.35, −0.45). This sequence rises, passes above, then descends. After smoothing with α=0.2, the new mean is 0.8 times the elite mean plus 0.2 times the old zero vertical mean, giving approximately (0.44,0.36,−0.28,−0.36). Iteration two samples around that corridor and reduces variance.

Assume the best sequence ends at (3.8,0.1), never enters the obstacle, and uses four unit actions. Its cost is (−0.2)² + 0.1² + 0 + 0.1×4 = 0.45. The straight sequence reaches the goal but enters the radius-0.7 obstacle once, at p2=(2,0), so its cost is 20 + 0.1×4 = 20.4. CEM has not learned a permanent policy; it has fitted a temporary distribution for this decision.

MPC executes only the first upward-right action. If the real robot moves less than predicted, the next observation changes p1. The old sequence is shifted as a warm start, but CEM re-solves from the measured location. That distinction—temporary plan distribution plus feedback—is why CEM-MPC can tolerate moderate model mismatch better than executing the four-step solution open loop.

6 · Gradients and policy proposals offer different structure

If dynamics and costs are differentiable, one can initialize action variables and descend the gradient ∂J/∂at:t+H−1 through the unrolled model. Gradient planning can use each model evaluation more efficiently than sampling in a high-dimensional action space. It is also sensitive to local minima, saturating action bounds, chaotic long-horizon derivatives, and inaccurate model gradients. A model can predict values reasonably while having derivatives that point the optimizer toward nonsense.

A learned actor provides another source of structure. Rather than sample around zero or hand-designed controls, draw proposals near π(a|s), then let CEM or gradient refinement adapt to the current goal. This hybrid separates habit from deliberation: the actor amortizes common solutions; planning spends compute where the current situation differs. Preserve some broad samples, otherwise a biased actor can prevent search from discovering a different route.

7 · Risk and constraints belong inside the objective

Optimizing expected cost can be inadequate when a rare collision matters more than average progress. Stochastic models permit alternatives. One can optimize a worst-case ensemble member, a high cost quantile, or conditional value at risk:

CVaRα(J) = E[J | J is in the worst (1−α) tail]

At α=0.9, CVaR averages roughly the worst ten percent of predicted costs. Another common score is E[J] + κ·Std[J], where κ encodes risk aversion. Epistemic uncertainty and aleatoric uncertainty should not be conflated: disagreement because the model lacks data argues for caution or exploration; irreducible outcome noise may require robust action even with abundant data.

Hard constraints should not be represented only as a finite reward if violation is unacceptable. Candidate rejection, control barrier functions, reachability filters, verified emergency braking, or a separate safety shield can sit outside the learned objective. A finite collision penalty always admits some predicted reward large enough to outweigh it. Model-based planning assists a safety case but does not create one.

8 · Tree search fits discrete, consequential branching

Shooting treats an action sequence as one vector. In chess, board games, routing, or symbolic decisions, actions branch discretely and many prefixes are shared. Monte Carlo tree search, MCTS, stores those shared prefixes in a tree. Each simulation repeatedly selects a child, expands a new node, evaluates its future, and backs up value along the path.

A PUCT-style selection score is:

a* = arg maxa [Q(s,a) + cpuctP(s,a) √(ΣbN(s,b)) / (1+N(s,a))]

Q(s,a) is mean backed-up value after choosing action a; P(s,a) is a learned policy prior; N(s,a) is visit count; and cpuct controls exploration. The first term exploits actions whose simulations look good. The second is large for promising prior actions that have few visits, so it allocates search to uncertainty in the tree. This is search exploration, distinct from real-environment exploration.

MCTS(root, simulation_budget B): repeat B times: node ← root; path ← [] while node is expanded: action ← argmax PUCT(node, action) append (node, action) to path node ← learned_or_known_dynamics(node, action) (policy_prior, leaf_value) ← evaluate_and_expand(node) back up rewards and leaf_value through reversed(path) return action distribution proportional to root visit counts

Unlike independent shooting sequences, MCTS spends repeated computation on promising prefixes and obtains a refined action distribution at the root. It is less natural for wide continuous action spaces unless actions are discretized or progressively widened. Its irregular, sequential memory access is also harder to batch on accelerators. Search depth, number of simulations, branching factor, and leaf-value accuracy jointly determine quality.

9 · MuZero learns a model for search, not for reconstruction

Classical model-based reasoning suggests learning the actual next observation. MuZero asks a narrower first-principles question: what must a model preserve for MCTS to choose good actions? Its answer is reward, value, and policy-relevant transition structure—not necessarily pixels, object colors, or legal-board reconstruction.

MuZero separates three learned functions:

s0 = hθ(o1:t)    representation (r̂k+1, sk+1) = gθ(sk, ak)    dynamics (p̂k, v̂k) = fθ(sk)    prediction

The representation h maps observation history to a root latent state. Learned dynamics g takes a latent and hypothetical action, then emits predicted immediate reward and next latent. Prediction f emits a policy prior and scalar value. MCTS uses g to expand hypothetical edges and f to prioritize and evaluate nodes.

Training unrolls recorded actions through g. At each unrolled step, predicted reward is supervised by observed reward, predicted value by a bootstrapped or search-enhanced return target, and predicted policy by MCTS root visit counts. In schematic form:

L = Σk=0K[ℓvalue(v̂k, zt+k) + ℓpolicy(p̂k, πt+k) + 𝟙k>0ℓreward(r̂k, rt+k)] + regularization

K is training unroll depth; z here denotes a value target, not necessarily a stochastic latent; π is the search policy from visit counts. The model is trained so search based on its latent predictions improves the decision targets that train it again.

10 · Value equivalence is precise—and task relative

A model is value equivalent for a policy class and reward family if planning or evaluation in that model gives the same relevant values as the environment, even if its internal state is not a faithful reconstruction. Two physical states may map to one latent if no allowed action and target objective needs to distinguish them. MuZero exploits this freedom.

This is not the claim that “only value matters” universally. Equivalence is relative to what is queried. Imagine two visually identical boxes: one is fragile and one reinforced. If the training reward only measures how quickly the robot touches a box, material may be discarded. Change the objective to lift without damage, and the old latent cannot recover the missing distinction. Likewise a chess model need not render wood grain, but it must retain every board distinction that changes legal moves or future value.

Now derive the representation choice from the job the model must do rather than treating model families as rows in a catalog:

  1. If the future job is not known, preserve broadly predictive evidence. Observation prediction pressures the state to retain features needed to predict sensors. This often improves reuse and makes the latent easier to interpret because appearance and physical evolution remain recoverable. The naive hope is that accurate pixels imply accurate decisions. It can fail in both directions: capacity may be spent on irrelevant texture, while a tiny reward-critical fact can have little effect on pixel error and be omitted anyway.
  2. If the job is stable and search is the bottleneck, preserve decision consequences directly. Reward, value, and policy prediction pressure the state to retain what the current decision process needs. The result can be compact and search-efficient because wood grain, lighting, and other irrelevant appearance never enter the latent. The cost appears when the question changes: a new reward may depend on information deliberately discarded during compression.
  3. If the system must decide now and transfer later, give the state several obligations. A hybrid multi-head model retains current task signals plus selected physical or semantic facts. It can preserve decision focus while transferring better to new goals. The engineering cost is real: more objectives consume capacity, interact through their gradients, and require deliberate loss balancing.

The diagnostic follows from the requirement. Test the latent with counterfactual queries outside the training reward: change the goal, ask for a physical property, or alter a constraint while holding observations fixed. If search fails because the required distinction is absent even though the optimizer is strong, the representation was sufficient for the old query and insufficient for the new one.

For a stable game, task-relative compression is a feature. For a general robot expected to receive new instructions, broader predictive, geometric, language, and affordance heads may be justified. “Minimal sufficient state” always requires naming sufficient for what.

11 · Exact distinctions among the methods

The names become intuitive if we ask four questions in order: what object is optimized, when is it optimized, what action geometry does it assume, and what survives after the current decision? Follow that chain for each method:

  1. Random shooting optimizes by selection among finite sampled sequences. At every decision it draws candidates and keeps the best. It works with continuous or discrete actions as long as sequences can be sampled. Afterward the samples disappear; the persistent object is the world model, plus an optional learned or hand-designed proposal for future samples.
  2. CEM optimizes the proposal distribution itself. During each decision it performs several sample–elite–refit rounds, naturally matching bounded continuous controls. The fitted proposal is temporary: after executing a control it is discarded or shifted forward as a warm start. The persistent knowledge remains in the world model, not in that one decision’s Gaussian.
  3. Gradient planning optimizes the action variables directly through differentiable dynamics. It runs at each decision and is most natural for smooth continuous actions and costs. What must persist is a differentiable world model whose gradients are useful to the optimizer; a merely accurate value with misleading derivatives is not sufficient.
  4. MPC optimizes no unique mathematical object of its own. It is the repeated finite-horizon control pattern: plan, execute a prefix, observe, and replan. Its inner optimizer may be shooting, CEM, gradients, or another solver, so it supports whatever action type that solver supports. The persistent system contains a state estimator, world model, objective, constraints, and optimizer.
  5. MCTS optimizes a selective discrete tree and the visit distribution at its root. Search happens at each decision; shared prefixes make it natural for discrete branching. The temporary tree may be reused briefly, but the persistent learned ingredients are the model, policy prior, and value estimator.
  6. MuZero trains the model that MCTS searches. Across experience it learns representation, latent dynamics, reward, policy, and value predictions; online, MCTS uses those predictions to search, usually over discrete actions. What persists is a value-equivalent latent model and its network weights, while the particular search tree belongs to the current decision.

Resolve the apparent competition
The category error is saying “CEM versus MPC” as though they are peers. The physical need is feedback after model error; that derives MPC. The computational need inside each MPC step is continuous trajectory optimization; that may derive CEM. Likewise, MCTS is the online tree procedure, while MuZero supplies the learned latent dynamics, policy priors, rewards, and values that make that procedure useful without a known simulator.

This decomposition also gives a debugging order. If the chosen sequence is poor even in a trusted model, improve the inner optimizer. If the first action is good open loop but failures grow after disturbances, inspect state estimation and replanning frequency. If search is systematic but reality disagrees with its rankings, inspect the world model or objective. The method name alone never identifies which link failed.

12 · Diagnostics before trusting closed-loop success

  1. Optimizer regret under a trusted simulator. On small cases where exhaustive or high-budget search is possible, compare the online optimizer’s cost with the reference. This isolates search quality from model error.
  2. Open-loop prediction by horizon. Replay recorded action sequences and measure state, reward, constraint, and uncertainty error at every depth. Planning horizon should not exceed the region where task-critical predictions remain meaningful without explicit correction.
  3. Predicted versus realized plan ranking. Execute safe candidate prefixes and test whether lower predicted cost reliably means lower realized cost. Ranking is often more important than pixel reconstruction.
  4. Width/depth/latency curves. Sweep candidates, horizon, CEM iterations, or MCTS simulations. Plot task return and tail latency. A method that misses its control deadline is not better.
  5. Warm-start ablation. Compare shifted previous plans, actor proposals, and cold starts. Stale warm starts can trap an optimizer after abrupt goal changes.
  6. Uncertainty and support trace. Plot ensemble disagreement or data distance along the selected plan and random alternatives. Selection systematically moving toward high uncertainty signals exploitation.
  7. Constraint stress tests. Perturb mass, friction, delay, obstacle position, and sensor noise. Verify both planner behavior and independent safety layers.
  8. Search-model consistency for MuZero. Compare root values before search, after search, and with realized returns; inspect visit entropy and reward predictions by unroll depth. Strong search targets cannot compensate indefinitely for drifting latent dynamics.

13 · Planning as a real-time systems problem

A controller with a 50 ms deadline has a finite inference budget. Roughly, model work scales with candidate count × horizon × stochastic particles × optimization iterations, though batching and tree reuse change constants. Longer horizon reveals distant goals but compounds error. More width covers alternatives but raises latency. More uncertainty samples estimate tail risk but reduce candidate throughput.

Production design uses the whole stack: cache the encoded current state; batch model rollouts; use lower precision where calibrated; warm-start from the shifted plan; allocate more search only when uncertainty or novelty is high; keep a small verified fallback controller; and record both mean and worst-case latency. If the model is a slow diffusion generator, it may produce beautiful video yet be unsuitable for a high-frequency servo loop. The representation must match the control clock.

Choose abstraction by decision timescale. A millisecond stabilizer may use linear local dynamics, while a one-second route planner uses latent objects and a minute-scale task planner uses symbolic subgoals. A hierarchical system can plan coarse subgoals at long horizon and let low-level MPC enforce smooth motion. One monolithic model need not answer every timescale at identical resolution.

14 · Common failure modes and concrete responses

  • Horizon myopia: the goal or delayed hazard lies beyond H. Add a terminal value, hierarchical subgoals, or longer coarse planning rather than blindly lengthening a fine rollout.
  • Model exploitation: the optimizer finds an impossible high-reward path. Penalize epistemic uncertainty, restrict support, use ensembles and hard constraints, and collect corrective data near proposed behavior.
  • CEM mode collapse: variance vanishes around a mediocre corridor. Keep a variance floor, use restarts or mixture proposals, inject broad actor-independent samples, and retain elites across rounds carefully.
  • Objective misspecification: the plan optimizes progress while creating jerk or near misses. Add operational costs and constraints, then test for new loopholes. No optimizer can infer unstated preferences.
  • MCTS branching explosion: too many actions receive shallow evaluation. Improve policy priors, use action abstraction or progressive widening, and improve leaf values.
  • Value-model non-transfer: a new goal needs latent facts absent from MuZero training. Retrain with new heads or use a richer multi-task predictive state; search cannot recover discarded information.
  • Feedback too slow: a dangerous event occurs before MPC’s next correction. Raise control rate, shorten action commitment, add a reactive shield, or move critical constraints to a faster inner controller.

Where this points next

Every planner above assumes its state preserves the entities and relations that make future consequences predictable. A single unstructured vector may forget which object moved behind which wall, confuse camera motion with object motion, or lose identity across occlusion. Lesson 10 introduces object-centric state, spatial frames, and persistent memory so prediction and planning can factor by things, geometry, and relations.

Takeaway
Planning is online optimization over action-conditioned predictions. Shooting samples sequences; CEM repeatedly fits its temporary proposal to elites; MPC is the feedback architecture that executes a prefix and replans; MCTS allocates discrete search to shared prefixes; and MuZero learns task-relative latent dynamics sufficient for reward, policy, and value search rather than observation reconstruction. Choose by action structure, risk, transfer needs, horizon, and real-time budget—and diagnose model error separately from search error.

Interview prompts

  • Why does MPC execute only the first action of an optimized sequence? The remainder is conditional on a predicted future. After one action, a new observation can correct state and model error, so replanning from evidence is more reliable than honoring an outdated open-loop commitment.
  • How exactly does CEM differ from random shooting? Random shooting samples once from a fixed proposal and selects the best sequence. CEM iterates: sample, retain elites, refit proposal mean and variance, and sample again, concentrating evaluations around promising regions.
  • Is CEM an alternative to MPC? No. CEM is a trajectory optimizer; MPC is a receding-horizon control pattern. CEM is commonly used inside MPC to solve each finite-horizon problem.
  • When would gradient planning beat CEM, and what could make it fail? It can be more evaluation-efficient in smooth, high-dimensional continuous systems with reliable derivatives. Local minima, saturated bounds, exploding long-horizon gradients, discontinuous costs, or wrong model gradients can make it fail.
  • What does PUCT balance in MCTS? It balances backed-up action value against an exploration bonus derived from policy prior and visit counts. The bonus spends simulations on plausible actions that have not yet been tested deeply.
  • What does value equivalence mean in MuZero? The learned model may differ from the physical observation dynamics yet preserve rewards, policies, and values needed by search for the specified task and policy/reward family. It is equivalence of decision consequences, not faithful reconstruction.
  • Why can a value-equivalent model fail after a reward change? Training was allowed to discard distinctions irrelevant to the old objective. If the new reward depends on one of those distinctions, no amount of search over the old latent can reconstruct it.
  • How do you separate a bad planner from a bad world model? Evaluate the optimizer against a trusted simulator or exhaustive small-case reference, and separately replay fixed real action sequences through the learned model. Search regret with an accurate model implicates optimization; incorrect rankings under fixed sequences implicate the model or objective.
