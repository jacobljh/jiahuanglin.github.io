Linear Story

Once teams share GPUs, scheduling becomes a product decision. Who gets scarce accelerators? Can a large distributed job wait until all ranks are available? Should small jobs backfill gaps? Should production preempt research? How do teams see cost? The book's scheduling chapter turns Kubernetes from a pod placer into a shared GPU platform.

Core scheduling can place pods, but AI batch work often needs bin packing, descheduling, gang scheduling, topology awareness, quotas, multitenancy, high-performance networking, storage planning, and correlated observability. Tools such as Kueue and Volcano-style schedulers exist because "first pod that fits" is not a fair or efficient policy for expensive accelerators.

Network, Storage, And HPC Bridges

Distributed training may depend on network paths as much as GPU count. Secondary network interfaces, RDMA-capable fabrics, and topology-aware placement can be the difference between useful scaling and expensive synchronization. Storage must feed data and checkpoints without turning GPUs into idle heaters. The scheduler cannot fix a data plane that cannot keep up.

The book also calls out the bridge between HPC habits and Kubernetes habits. Slurm remains common where batch scheduling and tightly coupled GPU jobs are the center of gravity. Kubernetes-native platforms may integrate with Slurm or use projects such as Slinky-style bridges so research workflows and cloud-native services can coexist. Treat this as an ownership decision: do you want Kubernetes to be the batch scheduler, or do you want Kubernetes to host services around an HPC scheduler?

Rendezvous And Scheduler Comparisons

PyTorch distributed jobs need rendezvous: workers discover each other, agree on ranks, and begin collectives together. If only half the pods are scheduled, the job is not half useful; it is stuck. Gang scheduling solves that by admitting the group only when enough resources are available. Volcano-style schedulers emphasize batch and gang semantics. Kueue emphasizes queueing, quotas, cohorts, and admission control for Kubernetes jobs. The comparison is not "which project is best?" but "where should the policy live: batch scheduler, queue controller, or platform API?"

Operational Rule

Publish the rules. GPU fairness fails socially before it fails technically. Teams should know priority classes, preemption policy, quota, expected wait time, chargeback, and incident override rules.

Mental Model

GPU fairness is a scheduling contract plus a financial contract. The platform must decide who waits, who preempts, who pays, and which topology is preserved.

Operational Checklist

  • Use queues and quotas for teams, workloads, and priority classes.
  • Use gang scheduling for distributed jobs that need all replicas before useful work begins.
  • Use topology-aware scheduling for jobs that depend on NVLink, rack locality, or secondary networks.
  • Expose chargeback or showback in dollars per GPU-hour and dollars per useful token or training run.

Failure Modes

  • Best-effort jobs consume scarce accelerators and block production incidents.
  • Bin packing improves utilization but destroys the topology needed by large jobs.
  • No one can explain GPU spend because platform metrics stop at allocation, not useful work.
