Part VIII - Autonomy and discovery

Exploration and discovery - finding unknown unknowns

The frontier pattern: agents proactively search for information, hypotheses, and possibilities.

Book source
Chapter 21 - Exploration and Discovery; PDF outline pages 228-236.
Linear position
Prerequisite: Prioritization and resource budgets, so open-ended search remains controlled.
New capability: Proactive hypothesis generation, experimentation, and discovery loops.

What the book contributes

Exploration and discovery lets agents proactively seek new information, discover possibilities, and identify unknown unknowns.

The book distinguishes this from reactive behavior and optimization in a known solution space.

Examples include scientific research automation, Google Co-Scientist, Agent Laboratory, market trend discovery, security discovery, game strategy, and creative generation.

This pattern requires safety and ethics because open-ended autonomy can enter sensitive or risky spaces.

Pattern shape

01Generate candidate hypotheses or directions.
02Prioritize by novelty, value, feasibility, risk, and cost.
03Gather evidence or run experiments.
04Record positive and negative results, then refine the search.

Implementation sketch

hypotheses = generate_hypotheses(goal, background)
ranked = prioritize(hypotheses, criteria=["novelty", "value", "feasibility", "risk", "cost"])
for h in ranked[:budget.max_experiments]:
    evidence = investigate(h)
    record_result(h, evidence)
    if promising(evidence):
        expand(h)

Book-specific deep dive

The exploration and discovery chapter uses examples such as Google Co-Scientist to show agents proposing hypotheses, searching literature, designing experiments, and iterating toward new findings. This is different from ordinary retrieval. Retrieval answers a known question; discovery helps decide which question or hypothesis is worth pursuing next.

Linearized teaching places discovery after prioritization because exploration is costly. The agent must balance novelty, plausibility, expected information gain, evidence gaps, and budget. It should keep an experiment log: what was tried, why it was promising, what evidence changed, and which paths were pruned.

  • Discovery loop: generate hypothesis, gather evidence, test or simulate, update belief, prioritize next exploration.
  • Safety rule: open-ended exploration still needs domain constraints, provenance, and human review for high-impact claims.

Running example

A performance agent explores optimization ideas: inspect profiling data, form bottleneck hypotheses, test one small change, record negative results, and propose the next experiment instead of blindly rewriting code.

Checkpoint exercise

Try it
Write three hypotheses for an open-ended task. Define what evidence would support or reject each one.

Key takeaways

  • Use exploration only with goals, budgets, and evaluation criteria.
  • Record hypotheses, evidence, and negative results.
  • Balance exploration with exploitation of known good paths.

Failure modes

  • Open-ended browsing with no stop rule.
  • Treating novelty as value without evaluation.
  • No record of rejected hypotheses, causing repeated dead ends.

Implementation checklist

  • What is the exploration goal?
  • What hypotheses are tested?
  • How is novelty or value scored?
  • What budget limits exploration?
  • What safety boundaries apply?
Linear takeaway
Discovery uses agent loops to decide what is worth learning next, while still preserving evidence and review.
