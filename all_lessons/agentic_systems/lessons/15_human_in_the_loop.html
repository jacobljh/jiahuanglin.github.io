Part V - Reliability and control

Human-in-the-loop - approval, correction, escalation

Human judgment belongs at risk, ambiguity, and accountability boundaries.

Book source
Chapter 13 - Human-in-the-Loop; PDF outline pages 147-152.
Linear position
Prerequisite: Recovery paths and goal contracts that can pause or escalate.
New capability: Approval checkpoints, human correction loops, and escalation states.

What the book contributes

HITL combines AI efficiency with human judgment, creativity, domain expertise, and accountability.

It is important in complex, ambiguous, ethical, regulated, or high-risk tasks.

Humans can review outputs, guide behavior, provide feedback, approve actions, or resolve uncertainty.

The book also notes trade-offs: scalability, latency, expert availability, training, and privacy.

Pattern shape

01Detect need for human judgment or approval.
02Create a concise decision packet.
03Pause task state while waiting.
04Resume, revise, or stop based on human response.

Implementation sketch

approval = {
  "proposed_action": "delete generated cache directory",
  "reason": "required before clean test run",
  "risk": "removes files",
  "alternatives": ["move to temp", "skip cleanup"],
  "question": "Approve deletion of ./cache?"
}
state.status = "waiting_for_human"

Book-specific deep dive

The human-in-the-loop chapter teaches that human involvement is not a single approval button. Humans may approve risky actions, correct wrong assumptions, supply missing context, adjudicate conflicts, review generated artifacts, or take over when policy requires judgment. The agent should know which kind of human help it needs.

Educationally this pattern comes after recovery because escalation is one recovery path. A useful HITL design packages the right information for the reviewer: goal, proposed action, evidence, alternatives, risk, and exact consequence of approval. The human should not have to reconstruct the trace from raw chat logs.

  • Approval packet: action, reason, evidence, risk, alternatives, rollback, and deadline.
  • Human role: decision maker for value judgments, permissions, high-impact side effects, and ambiguous evidence.

Running example

The coding assistant can read files and run tests without approval, but must ask before deleting files, installing packages, pushing commits, or changing production configuration.

Checkpoint exercise

Try it
Choose three actions in your agent that should require human approval. Write the exact approval packet fields.

Key takeaways

  • Use human review for risk, ambiguity, accountability, and irreversible side effects.
  • Give humans concise decision packets.
  • Store human decisions as state and evaluation data.

Failure modes

  • Escalating vague questions that require full trace reconstruction.
  • No timeout or owner for pending approval.
  • Using humans as a hidden crutch for weak automation.

Implementation checklist

  • Which actions require approval?
  • What does the human see?
  • What happens on timeout?
  • How is feedback stored?
  • Can corrections become eval cases?
Linear takeaway
Human review works best when the agent brings a concise decision packet, not a pile of raw transcript.
