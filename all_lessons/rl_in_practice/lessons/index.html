Reinforcement Learning in Practice, from first principles

The algorithm tracks teach mechanisms — PPO, GRPO, Q-learning, actor-critic. This one teaches the skill that sits on top of them: given a real-world problem, turn it into a reinforcement-learning system that ships. Twenty domains — games, driving, trading, recommendation, robotics, energy grids, networks, medicine, epidemics — each worked through the same loop: write the MDP, find the one thing that makes it hard, engineer exactly that, and guard it in production. Adapted into English from a 659-page Chinese RL interview bank.

Who this is for
You know what an MDP is and roughly what PPO does — but when someone says "design the RL system for autonomous-driving path planning" or "for cloud autoscaling," you don't have a repeatable way to attack it. By the end you do: a method that produces the state/action/reward formulation, names the binding difficulty (sparse reward? partial observability? safety? non-stationarity? sim2real? real-time?), and reaches for the mechanism that removes that specific difficulty — plus the production guardrails that keep it alive. This is the applied-RL / ML-engineering-interview layer.

The one method, applied to twenty domains

"Applied RL" sounds like a pile of domain tricks. It isn't. Every lesson in this series is the same five-step loop, run until the system holds. The art is only in which difficulty binds first — and that is something you can read off the MDP before writing any code.

This is what "from first principles" means here: you never reach for a mechanism — an action mask, a safety layer, an offline-RL estimator, domain randomization — until the MDP has named the difficulty it removes. Mechanisms are answers to a difficulty you diagnosed, never a checklist.

Part I · Perception-to-control agents

Agents that turn raw sensory input into low-level control. Shared difficulties: hybrid/continuous action spaces, partial observability, sparse reward, real-time budgets, and the sim-to-real gap.

01
Game-AI agent training — real-time + self-play
The domain that built deep RL. Installs the method: hybrid action heads, action masking that won't NaN, decaying reward shaping with a 3-layer hack detector, self-play staleness as a variance multiplier, and the 16 ms frame budget.
02
Autonomous-driving path planning — safety + sim2real
Continuous control under hard safety constraints. Action normalization for physical consistency, constrained RL and control-barrier safety layers, imitation+RL hybrids, high-dimensional perception, and domain randomization for sim2real.
09
Robotic grasping — sparse reward + sim2real
Sparse success signals and contact-rich physics. Goal-conditioned reward and HER, dexterous action spaces, sim2real transfer for contact dynamics, and sample-efficient learning on real hardware.
13
UAV path planning — continuous control + safety
3-D continuous navigation with energy and no-fly constraints. Reward design for smooth feasible trajectories, obstacle/collision safety, and robustness to wind and localization noise.

Part II · Markets & recommendation

Environments that are non-stationary by nature and where you mostly cannot experiment live — so off-policy evaluation and exploration discipline dominate.

03
Quant-trading order execution — non-stationary + OPE
Limit-order-book state, joint price/quantity actions at microsecond latency, market-impact reward design, offline policy evaluation under non-stationarity, and compliance constraints baked into the policy.
04
Recommender-system cold start — exploration + cold-start
Interaction-sequence state without leaking the future, exploration vs exploitation for new users/items, reward design beyond clicks, and the gradient conflicts of fusing text and ID embeddings.

Part III · Systems & resource control

Control loops inside computer and network systems: delayed actions, partial observability of load, latency SLOs, and cost as a first-class reward term.

07
Network congestion control — partial obs + delayed reward
Setting send-rate/window from noisy delayed signals. Throughput-vs-latency reward, partial observability of the bottleneck, and fairness across competing flows.
08
Edge-computing task offloading — constrained + latency
Deciding what runs on device vs edge vs cloud under energy and deadline constraints. Mixed discrete-continuous actions, queue-aware state, and latency/energy reward trade-offs.
12
Cloud-resource elastic scaling — delayed action + cost
Scaling fleets ahead of demand when provisioning has lag. Predictive state, the cost-vs-SLA reward, action delay, and safe exploration on a live bill.
16
5G network-slicing resource allocation — constrained + SLA
Partitioning radio/compute across slices with per-slice SLAs. Constrained RL with SLA-penalty rewards, fast online inference at the MEC edge, and meta-adaptation to new traffic.
19
Data-center cooling — safety + continuous control
Minimizing cooling energy without ever overheating hardware. Continuous setpoint control, hard thermal-safety constraints, and offline learning from BMS logs before any live actuation.

Part IV · Industrial & energy control

Physical plants and power systems: continuous actuation, stochastic disturbances, and safety/feasibility constraints that cannot be violated even once.

05
Industrial control & parameter tuning — continuous + safety
RL as an adaptive controller over a process plant. Continuous action spaces, safety/feasibility constraints, sample efficiency on slow real processes, and offline pretraining from historian data.
10
Energy-management microgrid — continuous + uncertainty
Dispatching storage and generation under uncertain renewables and demand. Forecast-augmented state, cost+reliability reward, and chance constraints on supply.
11
Wireless-network power allocation — constrained optimization
Allocating transmit power across users to maximize rate under interference and power budgets. RL vs convex baselines, graph-structured state, and per-constraint feasibility.
18
Satellite-constellation power control — constrained + non-stationary
Managing power/beam budgets across a moving constellation. Time-varying topology in the state, hard energy constraints, and robustness to long control-loop delays.

Part V · Logistics & scheduling

Combinatorial decision-making over time, often with many agents sharing one objective — where the action space is the central difficulty.

06
Smart-logistics AGV scheduling — multi-agent + combinatorial
Routing a fleet of warehouse robots without deadlock. Multi-agent coordination, combinatorial action spaces, conflict/collision constraints, and credit assignment across the fleet.
15
Intelligent traffic-signal control — multi-agent coordination
Coordinating signals across an arterial or grid. Per-intersection vs city-wide reward, multi-agent value decomposition, and partial observability of upstream demand.
17
Smart-manufacturing scheduling — combinatorial
Job-shop scheduling as sequential decisions. Encoding the disjunctive graph as state, large structured action spaces, makespan reward, and constraint feasibility.

Part VI · High-stakes & society

Domains where you cannot freely explore on real subjects — so offline RL, causal reasoning, and conservative safety dominate every choice.

14
Personalized medical dosing — offline RL + safety
Learning dosing policies from logged patient data you can never A/B test. Offline RL with conservative value estimation, OPE you can trust, and clinically hard safety bounds.
20
Epidemic intervention strategy — offline + causal + high-stakes
Choosing interventions over a compartmental model from sparse, confounded observational data. Causal state estimation, reward that balances health and economic cost, and deep uncertainty.

How to use this

  1. Read lesson 01 first. It installs the five-step loop on a domain you already understand (a game bot). Every later lesson assumes the loop — Formulate → Diagnose → Engineer → Guard.
  2. Predict the binding difficulty before you read. Each lesson opens with the MDP. Pause, ask "what makes this MDP hard?", then check. The gap between your guess and the answer is the lesson.
  3. Touch the widget. Each lesson has one interactive knob whose extreme setting flips the binding difficulty — sparse becomes dense, feasible becomes unsafe, cheap becomes prohibitive. Finding that flip is the design intuition.
  4. Read in any order after 01. Parts I–VI group domains by their dominant difficulty, but each lesson is self-contained. Together they're the proof the method generalizes across every domain RL touches.
