Design a payment system

Payments are not a single API call. They are a state machine around money movement, external providers, retries, and auditability.

Source: Vol. 2 Ch. 11, archive Case drill Trade-off first

0. Interview hinge

The hinge is the moment where the prompt stops being generic and the architecture becomes forced. For this case, that hinge is: Payment intent, ledger, idempotency, retry, PSP integration, reconciliation.

Invariant to protect
The design is only correct if the user-visible contract survives retries, partial failures, stale caches, and reordering. A concrete invariant to defend here: Double charge must be handled by Use idempotency keys and intent state transitions..
Tempting but wrong
A common shortcut is to choose Sync confirmation vs async pending just because it buys simple UX. That shortcut fails when its cost matters: provider latency coupling. Use it only when the workload matches the condition: fast card auth.

1. Clarify the contract

The prompt is best treated as a product contract before it is treated as a component diagram. The system needs to do the following:

  • Create and confirm payment attempts.
  • Integrate with payment service providers.
  • Avoid double charge on retries.
  • Track state transitions and webhooks.
  • Reconcile internal state with provider settlement.

2. Put numbers on the shape

The exact numbers depend on the interviewer, but these are the estimates that should steer the architecture:

  • Payment QPS is usually lower than browsing QPS but correctness bar is much higher.
  • Provider latency can be seconds, so user flows need pending states.
  • Webhook delivery is at-least-once and out of order.
  • Audit storage is cheap compared with financial ambiguity.

5. Linearized design

Walk the design in the order a request or event actually moves. This keeps the interview grounded and exposes the first bottleneck naturally.

  1. 1. Create a payment intent representing the business purpose and amount.
  2. 2. Use an idempotency key for every client confirm request.
  3. 3. Send a provider charge request from a durable payment attempt record.
  4. 4. Process provider responses and webhooks through a state machine.
  5. 5. Record financial effects as append-only ledger entries.
  6. 6. Run reconciliation against provider reports and settlement files.

6. Deep dives

Intent vs attempt

The customer wants one purchase. The system may make multiple provider attempts. Separating intent from attempt prevents retries from becoming duplicate business orders.

Webhook ordering

A success webhook can arrive before the synchronous response, and duplicate webhooks are normal. The state machine must accept idempotent transitions.

Ledger

Balances should come from immutable entries, not mutable status fields. Status is for workflow; ledger is for money truth.

9. What to say in the interview

Strong signal
Lead with the dominant constraint: Payment intent, ledger, idempotency, retry, PSP integration, reconciliation. Then defend the design by naming what you made faster, what you made more reliable, and what complexity you accepted.
