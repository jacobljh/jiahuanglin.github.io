Design metrics monitoring and alerting

Monitoring is a write-heavy database with a human contract: alert only when action is needed, and preserve enough history to debug.

Source: Vol. 2 Ch. 5, archive Case drill Trade-off first

0. Interview hinge

The hinge is the moment where the prompt stops being generic and the architecture becomes forced. For this case, that hinge is: Ingestion, time-series storage, rollups, alert evaluation, cardinality control.

Invariant to protect
The design is only correct if the user-visible contract survives retries, partial failures, stale caches, and reordering. A concrete invariant to defend here: High-cardinality metric storm must be handled by Reject or relabel at ingestion and alert owners..
Tempting but wrong
A common shortcut is to choose Pull vs push just because it buys central control. That shortcut fails when its cost matters: harder for short-lived jobs. Use it only when the workload matches the condition: stable service fleets.

1. Clarify the contract

The prompt is best treated as a product contract before it is treated as a component diagram. The system needs to do the following:

  • Ingest metrics from many services and hosts.
  • Store time-series data efficiently.
  • Query dashboards over recent and historical windows.
  • Evaluate alert rules reliably.
  • Control cardinality and cost.

2. Put numbers on the shape

The exact numbers depend on the interviewer, but these are the estimates that should steer the architecture:

  • Metric volume equals series count times scrape or push frequency.
  • Labels can explode cardinality faster than hosts or services.
  • Recent high-resolution data and old downsampled data need different storage tiers.
  • Alert evaluation must tolerate ingestion delay without paging incorrectly.

5. Linearized design

Walk the design in the order a request or event actually moves. This keeps the interview grounded and exposes the first bottleneck naturally.

  1. 1. Collect samples through agents, scrapers, or push gateways.
  2. 2. Validate labels and drop or cap high-cardinality dimensions.
  3. 3. Shard ingestion by series fingerprint.
  4. 4. Store recent samples in a write-optimized time-series store.
  5. 5. Downsample older data into rollups for cheaper long-range queries.
  6. 6. Run alert evaluators on aligned windows and persist alert state.

6. Deep dives

Cardinality

The most common monitoring failure is not CPU; it is unbounded labels like user_id or request_id. The system needs guardrails at ingestion.

Alert semantics

An alert should represent user pain or imminent capacity risk. Alerting on every internal cause creates noise and trains people to ignore the system.

Rollups

Averaging old data can hide spikes. Store min, max, count, sum, and selected percentiles depending on query needs.

9. What to say in the interview

Strong signal
Lead with the dominant constraint: Ingestion, time-series storage, rollups, alert evaluation, cardinality control. Then defend the design by naming what you made faster, what you made more reliable, and what complexity you accepted.
