Design a news feed

A feed is the canonical fanout trade-off: precompute reads for ordinary users, but avoid exploding writes for accounts with enormous audiences.

Source: Vol. 1 Ch. 11 Case drill Trade-off first
How to read this case
This page is an original educational walkthrough based on the case topic, not a transcription. Read it linearly: contract, estimates, data, flows, bottlenecks, trade-offs, then failure modes.

0. Interview hinge

The hinge is the moment where the prompt stops being generic and the architecture becomes forced. For this case, that hinge is: Fanout-on-write vs fanout-on-read, celebrity users, ranking, freshness, storage.

Invariant to protect
The design is only correct if the user-visible contract survives retries, partial failures, stale caches, and reordering. A concrete invariant to defend here: Celebrity post overload must be handled by Classify high-fanout authors and merge at read time..
Tempting but wrong
A common shortcut is to choose Fanout-on-write vs read just because it buys fast reads. That shortcut fails when its cost matters: write amplification. Use it only when the workload matches the condition: ordinary users with modest followers.

1. Clarify the contract

The prompt is best treated as a product contract before it is treated as a component diagram. The system needs to do the following:

  • Publish posts from users or pages.
  • Read a personalized feed with low latency.
  • Support follow graph changes.
  • Keep feed reasonably fresh.
  • Handle users with very large follower counts.

2. Put numbers on the shape

The exact numbers depend on the interviewer, but these are the estimates that should steer the architecture:

  • If one post fans out to 10M followers, write amplification can dominate the entire system.
  • Feed reads are usually much more frequent than posts.
  • A home timeline cache of 500 item IDs per active user can become large but manageable for daily active users.
  • Ranking models or feature fetches can easily exceed the latency budget if done synchronously for every candidate.

5. Linearized design

Walk the design in the order a request or event actually moves. This keeps the interview grounded and exposes the first bottleneck naturally.

  1. 1. Store the canonical post once in a post store.
  2. 2. For normal authors, fan out post IDs to followers' home timeline caches on write.
  3. 3. For celebrity authors, skip full fanout and merge their posts at read time.
  4. 4. On feed read, fetch timeline IDs, hydrate post bodies, apply light ranking, and return a cursor.
  5. 5. Use backfill jobs for follow changes and missed fanout.
  6. 6. Track freshness lag, fanout queue depth, and read fallback rate.

6. Deep dives

Hybrid fanout

Pure fanout-on-write makes reads fast but punishes celebrity writes. Pure fanout-on-read keeps writes cheap but makes every feed read expensive. Hybrid is the senior answer.

Timeline as cache

The home timeline should be treated as derived data. If it is wrong, rebuild from posts and graph. That makes recovery possible.

Ranking boundary

In an interview, separate retrieval from ranking. The system first collects candidates cheaply, then ranks a bounded set. Ranking the universe is not a design.

9. What to say in the interview

Strong signal
Lead with the dominant constraint: Fanout-on-write vs fanout-on-read, celebrity users, ranking, freshness, storage. Then defend the design by naming what you made faster, what you made more reliable, and what complexity you accepted.
