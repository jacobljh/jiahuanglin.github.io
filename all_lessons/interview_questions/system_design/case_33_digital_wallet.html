Design a digital wallet

A wallet is a ledger product. If you store only balances, you cannot explain how the balance happened or recover from mistakes safely.

Source: Vol. 2 Ch. 12 Case drill Trade-off first
How to read this case
This page is an original educational walkthrough based on the case topic, not a transcription. Read it linearly: contract, estimates, data, flows, bottlenecks, trade-offs, then failure modes.

0. Interview hinge

The hinge is the moment where the prompt stops being generic and the architecture becomes forced. For this case, that hinge is: Ledger model, balance reads, double-entry accounting, holds, auditability.

Invariant to protect
The design is only correct if the user-visible contract survives retries, partial failures, stale caches, and reordering. A concrete invariant to defend here: Negative available balance must be handled by Enforce conditional debit against available balance..
Tempting but wrong
A common shortcut is to choose Compute balance on read vs snapshot just because it buys simple truth. That shortcut fails when its cost matters: slow for long histories. Use it only when the workload matches the condition: low-volume accounts.

1. Clarify the contract

The prompt is best treated as a product contract before it is treated as a component diagram. The system needs to do the following:

  • Credit and debit user wallets.
  • Transfer funds between users or accounts.
  • Show current and available balances.
  • Support holds, reversals, and dispute adjustments.
  • Provide audit trails.

2. Put numbers on the shape

The exact numbers depend on the interviewer, but these are the estimates that should steer the architecture:

  • Balance reads may be high, but ledger writes carry the correctness risk.
  • Double-entry means every movement creates balanced debit and credit entries.
  • Available balance differs from posted balance when holds exist.
  • Ledger tables grow forever and need partitioning by account and time.

The data model is the first architecture. If the data ownership is vague, every later box-and-arrow decision will be vague too.

5. Linearized design

Walk the design in the order a request or event actually moves. This keeps the interview grounded and exposes the first bottleneck naturally.

  1. 1. Model every wallet as an account in a ledger.
  2. 2. Represent money movement as a ledger transaction with debit and credit entries.
  3. 3. Write entries append-only in a database transaction.
  4. 4. Maintain balance snapshots for fast reads, derived from entries.
  5. 5. Use holds to reserve funds before final capture.
  6. 6. Reconcile snapshots against the ledger and never edit history in place.

6. Deep dives

Double-entry

For every debit there is a credit. This invariant catches missing money and makes reconciliation possible.

Available vs posted

A user may have money posted but not available because of holds. Mixing the two creates overspending bugs.

Append-only correction

Financial systems correct with new entries, not by editing old entries. The audit log is the product.

9. What to say in the interview

Strong signal
Lead with the dominant constraint: Ledger model, balance reads, double-entry accounting, holds, auditability. Then defend the design by naming what you made faster, what you made more reliable, and what complexity you accepted.
