Microservices, containers, deployment, and Lambda

Infrastructure choices are boundary choices. Containers, services, and serverless functions each decide who owns runtime, scaling, and failure.

0. Interview hinge

The hinge is the moment where the prompt stops being generic and the architecture becomes forced. For this case, that hinge is: Runtime boundaries, rollout strategy, serverless execution, operational trade-offs.

Invariant to protect
The design is only correct if the user-visible contract survives retries, partial failures, stale caches, and reordering. A concrete invariant to defend here: Bad deploy must be handled by Use canary metrics and automated rollback..
Tempting but wrong
A common shortcut is to choose Monolith vs microservices just because it buys simple local transactions. That shortcut fails when its cost matters: larger deploy unit. Use it only when the workload matches the condition: early product.

1. Clarify the contract

The prompt is best treated as a product contract before it is treated as a component diagram. The system needs to do the following:

  • Explain service boundaries and communication modes.
  • Package applications consistently.
  • Deploy safely with rollback.
  • Scale runtime capacity.
  • Know when serverless helps or hurts.

2. Put numbers on the shape

The exact numbers depend on the interviewer, but these are the estimates that should steer the architecture:

  • Every service boundary adds network latency and operational ownership.
  • Container startup is usually faster and more predictable than full VM provisioning.
  • Serverless cold starts matter for latency-sensitive endpoints.
  • Deployment blast radius depends on rollout strategy and cell or region boundaries.

5. Linearized design

Walk the design in the order a request or event actually moves. This keeps the interview grounded and exposes the first bottleneck naturally.

  1. 1. Start with domain boundaries, not one microservice per table.
  2. 2. Package services into immutable images with explicit config.
  3. 3. Deploy through rolling, blue/green, or canary strategy with health checks.
  4. 4. Use service discovery and load balancing for runtime routing.
  5. 5. Choose serverless for event-driven or bursty workloads with limited runtime assumptions.
  6. 6. Observe each boundary with metrics, logs, traces, and deploy markers.

6. Deep dives

Distributed monolith

Splitting code without splitting ownership and data creates the worst of both worlds: network calls plus coupled releases.

Deployment strategy

Canary reduces blast radius by exposing a small slice first. Blue/green gives fast rollback but needs duplicate capacity.

Lambda shape

Serverless removes server management but introduces cold starts, runtime limits, and provider-specific operational behavior.

9. What to say in the interview

Strong signal
Lead with the dominant constraint: Runtime boundaries, rollout strategy, serverless execution, operational trade-offs. Then defend the design by naming what you made faster, what you made more reliable, and what complexity you accepted.
