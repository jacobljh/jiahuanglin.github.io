Design a stock exchange

An exchange is a correctness-first low-latency system. The core trick is to make the matching engine deterministic and keep everything else out of its way.

Source: Vol. 2 Ch. 13, archive Case drill Trade-off first

0. Interview hinge

The hinge is the moment where the prompt stops being generic and the architecture becomes forced. For this case, that hinge is: Order book, matching engine, sequencing, market data, persistence, risk checks.

Invariant to protect
The design is only correct if the user-visible contract survives retries, partial failures, stale caches, and reordering. A concrete invariant to defend here: Engine crash must be handled by Replay sequenced log and compare rebuilt state checksums..
Tempting but wrong
A common shortcut is to choose Single-thread book vs distributed book just because it buys determinism and low latency. That shortcut fails when its cost matters: per-symbol throughput ceiling. Use it only when the workload matches the condition: most exchange cores.

1. Clarify the contract

The prompt is best treated as a product contract before it is treated as a component diagram. The system needs to do the following:

  • Accept buy and sell orders.
  • Match orders by price-time priority.
  • Publish trades and market data.
  • Persist an audit trail.
  • Perform risk checks before orders reach the book.

2. Put numbers on the shape

The exact numbers depend on the interviewer, but these are the estimates that should steer the architecture:

  • Latency budgets can be microseconds to low milliseconds depending on market.
  • One symbol's order book is a natural serialization point.
  • Market data fanout can exceed order ingress by orders of magnitude.
  • Audit logs must allow deterministic replay.

5. Linearized design

Walk the design in the order a request or event actually moves. This keeps the interview grounded and exposes the first bottleneck naturally.

  1. 1. Authenticate sessions and perform basic gateway validation.
  2. 2. Run pre-trade risk checks before sequencing.
  3. 3. Assign every accepted order a strict sequence number.
  4. 4. Route each symbol or partition to a single matching engine owner.
  5. 5. Match deterministically against in-memory order books.
  6. 6. Append orders, cancels, and trades to durable logs and publish market data.

6. Deep dives

Single writer

For a symbol, a single-threaded matching loop can be faster and safer than distributed locking. Parallelize across symbols, not within one book.

Deterministic replay

If the engine crashes, replay the sequenced input log to rebuild the book. This requires every side effect to derive from ordered inputs.

Risk before match

Risk checks after matching are too late. The system must reject orders that violate limits before they can create trades.

9. What to say in the interview

Strong signal
Lead with the dominant constraint: Order book, matching engine, sequencing, market data, persistence, risk checks. Then defend the design by naming what you made faster, what you made more reliable, and what complexity you accepted.
