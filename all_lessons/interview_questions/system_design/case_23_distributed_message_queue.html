Design a distributed message queue

A message queue is the pressure valve between services. The design question is which guarantees you can afford: order, durability, replay, and throughput.

0. Interview hinge

The hinge is the moment where the prompt stops being generic and the architecture becomes forced. For this case, that hinge is: Topics, partitions, consumer groups, ordering, durability, backpressure.

Invariant to protect
The design is only correct if the user-visible contract survives retries, partial failures, stale caches, and reordering. A concrete invariant to defend here: Consumer lag grows must be handled by Scale consumers or reduce downstream bottleneck; alert on lag age..
Tempting but wrong
A common shortcut is to choose Queue vs log just because it buys simple work distribution. That shortcut fails when its cost matters: less replay. Use it only when the workload matches the condition: task queues.

1. Clarify the contract

The prompt is best treated as a product contract before it is treated as a component diagram. The system needs to do the following:

  • Let producers publish messages to topics or queues.
  • Let consumers read at their own pace.
  • Persist messages long enough for retry and replay.
  • Scale throughput by partitioning.
  • Expose delivery semantics clearly.

2. Put numbers on the shape

The exact numbers depend on the interviewer, but these are the estimates that should steer the architecture:

  • Throughput is bytes per second as much as messages per second.
  • Partition count controls parallelism and ordering scope.
  • Retention multiplies storage by ingress rate and time.
  • Consumer lag is a first-class capacity signal.

5. Linearized design

Walk the design in the order a request or event actually moves. This keeps the interview grounded and exposes the first bottleneck naturally.

  1. 1. Append producer messages to partition logs selected by key or round-robin.
  2. 2. Replicate partition logs to tolerate broker failure.
  3. 3. Let consumers track offsets per consumer group.
  4. 4. Assign partitions to consumers so each partition has one active consumer per group.
  5. 5. Retain messages by time or size independent of consumption.
  6. 6. Apply backpressure through producer quotas, consumer lag alerts, and retention limits.

6. Deep dives

Queue vs log

A queue removes messages after consumption. A log keeps messages by retention and lets many groups replay independently. Logs are better for event streams and recovery.

Ordering scope

Total order across a distributed queue kills throughput. Per-partition order is the practical contract. Choose keys so related events share a partition.

Exactly once

The honest answer is effectively-once: idempotent producers, transactional writes where possible, and idempotent consumers.

8. Failure modes

FailureMitigation
Consumer lag growsScale consumers or reduce downstream bottleneck; alert on lag age.
Poison messageUse retries with dead-letter queues and inspectable payloads.
Broker failureElect replica leader and resume from replicated log.
Duplicate processingMake consumers idempotent and commit offsets after side effects carefully.

9. What to say in the interview

Strong signal
Lead with the dominant constraint: Topics, partitions, consumer groups, ordering, durability, backpressure. Then defend the design by naming what you made faster, what you made more reliable, and what complexity you accepted.
