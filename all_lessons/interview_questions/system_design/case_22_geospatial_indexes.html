Geospatial indexes and quadtrees

Geo indexes are how you turn a continuous surface into discrete keys. The trick is choosing a discretization that matches density and query shape.

0. Interview hinge

The hinge is the moment where the prompt stops being generic and the architecture becomes forced. For this case, that hinge is: Grid, geohash, quadtree, S2-style cells, precision vs fanout.

Invariant to protect
The design is only correct if the user-visible contract survives retries, partial failures, stale caches, and reordering. A concrete invariant to defend here: Boundary miss must be handled by Always include intersecting neighbor cells and post-filter exactly..
Tempting but wrong
A common shortcut is to choose Fixed grid vs quadtree just because it buys simple keys. That shortcut fails when its cost matters: poor density adaptation. Use it only when the workload matches the condition: uniform workloads.

1. Clarify the contract

The prompt is best treated as a product contract before it is treated as a component diagram. The system needs to do the following:

  • Index points or regions by latitude and longitude.
  • Support radius and bounding-box queries.
  • Handle dense and sparse areas.
  • Avoid missing objects near boundaries.
  • Keep update and query costs predictable.

2. Put numbers on the shape

The exact numbers depend on the interviewer, but these are the estimates that should steer the architecture:

  • Smaller cells reduce candidates but increase the number of cells per query.
  • Dense city blocks may need finer partitioning than rural regions.
  • Boundary expansion can multiply cell reads.
  • Moving objects turn index maintenance into write load.

5. Linearized design

Walk the design in the order a request or event actually moves. This keeps the interview grounded and exposes the first bottleneck naturally.

  1. 1. Choose a world partitioning scheme: grid, geohash, quadtree, or spherical cells.
  2. 2. Map each object to one or more cells at an appropriate precision.
  3. 3. For a query, compute covering cells for the search shape.
  4. 4. Fetch candidates from those cells.
  5. 5. Post-filter by exact distance or geometry.
  6. 6. Adapt precision or split cells when density creates too many candidates.

6. Deep dives

Cell precision

Precision is a two-sided cost. Coarse cells read too many candidates. Fine cells read too many cells. The optimum depends on density and radius distribution.

Adaptive trees

Quadtrees split dense regions and leave sparse regions coarse. That improves balance but complicates updates and storage.

Spherical geometry

Latitude-longitude grids distort near the poles. Large-scale systems often use spherical cell libraries to avoid planar assumptions.

8. Failure modes

FailureMitigation
Boundary missAlways include intersecting neighbor cells and post-filter exactly.
Dense cell overloadSplit cells or add secondary ranking limits.
Coordinate normalization bugValidate ranges and normalize longitude wrapping.
Moving-object churnUpdate only on meaningful cell changes.
