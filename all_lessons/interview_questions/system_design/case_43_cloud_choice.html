IaaS, PaaS, SaaS, and cloud choice

Cloud choice is a responsibility trade. The more a provider manages, the less you operate, but the fewer low-level choices you control.

Source: Archive Case drill Trade-off first
How to read this case

0. Interview hinge

The hinge is the moment where the prompt stops being generic and the architecture becomes forced. For this case, that hinge is: Responsibility boundaries, managed services, lock-in, cost and control.

Invariant to protect
The design is only correct if the user-visible contract survives retries, partial failures, stale caches, and reordering. A concrete invariant to defend here: Unexpected bill must be handled by Tag resources and alert on cost anomalies..
Tempting but wrong
A common shortcut is to choose Managed vs self-managed just because it buys less ops burden. That shortcut fails when its cost matters: less control and possible cost. Use it only when the workload matches the condition: small teams.

1. Clarify the contract

The prompt is best treated as a product contract before it is treated as a component diagram. The system needs to do the following:

  • Explain IaaS, PaaS, and SaaS responsibility splits.
  • Choose managed vs self-managed components.
  • Reason about lock-in and portability.
  • Estimate cost drivers.
  • Match cloud services to team maturity.

2. Put numbers on the shape

The exact numbers depend on the interviewer, but these are the estimates that should steer the architecture:

  • Managed services may cost more per unit but save engineering time and incident risk.
  • Data egress and cross-region traffic can dominate compute in some systems.
  • Self-managed databases require on-call, backups, upgrades, and failover drills.
  • Portability has an ongoing tax even before a migration happens.

5. Linearized design

Walk the design in the order a request or event actually moves. This keeps the interview grounded and exposes the first bottleneck naturally.

  1. 1. Classify each layer: hardware, network, OS, runtime, database, application, business workflow.
  2. 2. Decide which layers your team truly needs to control.
  3. 3. Use managed services for undifferentiated heavy operations unless constraints say otherwise.
  4. 4. Model cost by compute, storage, IO, egress, and support.
  5. 5. Document lock-in at the API, data, and operational levels.
  6. 6. Design exit paths only where the business risk justifies the complexity.

6. Deep dives

Responsibility model

IaaS gives raw infrastructure. PaaS gives an application platform. SaaS gives a finished workflow. The decision is what you are willing to own.

Managed database

A managed DB is not magic. You still own schema, query patterns, and data correctness. The provider owns much of provisioning, backups, and failover mechanics.

Lock-in

Lock-in is not always bad. It is bad when it traps a critical system in a cost, compliance, or capability corner without an exit plan.

9. What to say in the interview

Strong signal
Lead with the dominant constraint: Responsibility boundaries, managed services, lock-in, cost and control. Then defend the design by naming what you made faster, what you made more reliable, and what complexity you accepted.
