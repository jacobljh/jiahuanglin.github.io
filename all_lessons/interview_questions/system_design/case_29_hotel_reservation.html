Design a hotel reservation system

Reservations are about scarce inventory under concurrency. The product wants conversion; correctness wants no impossible bookings.

Source: Vol. 2 Ch. 7, archive Case drill Trade-off first

0. Interview hinge

The hinge is the moment where the prompt stops being generic and the architecture becomes forced. For this case, that hinge is: Inventory, booking holds, overbooking, payment handoff, consistency boundaries.

Invariant to protect
The design is only correct if the user-visible contract survives retries, partial failures, stale caches, and reordering. A concrete invariant to defend here: Double booking must be handled by Use transactional conditional update on inventory or serialized per hotel-date key..
Tempting but wrong
A common shortcut is to choose Stale search vs strong search just because it buys fast browsing. That shortcut fails when its cost matters: some checkout failures. Use it only when the workload matches the condition: large catalogs.

1. Clarify the contract

The prompt is best treated as a product contract before it is treated as a component diagram. The system needs to do the following:

  • Search available rooms by hotel, date, and filters.
  • Hold inventory during checkout.
  • Confirm booking after payment.
  • Avoid overselling unless product policy permits it.
  • Support cancellations and inventory release.

2. Put numbers on the shape

The exact numbers depend on the interviewer, but these are the estimates that should steer the architecture:

  • Search traffic is much larger than booking traffic.
  • Inventory writes are concentrated by hotel and date.
  • A flash deal can make one hotel-date a hot row.
  • Payment authorization may take seconds, so inventory needs temporary holds.

5. Linearized design

Walk the design in the order a request or event actually moves. This keeps the interview grounded and exposes the first bottleneck naturally.

  1. 1. Serve search from denormalized availability indexes or cache.
  2. 2. When a user checks out, create a time-limited hold against room-type/date inventory.
  3. 3. Process payment against the hold rather than raw inventory.
  4. 4. Confirm reservation by converting the hold to a booking in a transaction.
  5. 5. Expire holds automatically and return inventory.
  6. 6. Reconcile reservations, payments, and inventory through audit jobs.

6. Deep dives

Hold model

A hold is a temporary claim. Without holds, users lose rooms during payment. With holds, abandoned checkouts reduce sellable inventory until expiration.

Inventory granularity

Most hotels sell room types, not specific rooms, until assignment. Inventory by room type and night reduces complexity while matching product behavior.

Search consistency

Search can be slightly stale if checkout revalidates inventory. This keeps search fast and moves strict correctness to the booking path.

9. What to say in the interview

Strong signal
Lead with the dominant constraint: Inventory, booking holds, overbooking, payment handoff, consistency boundaries. Then defend the design by naming what you made faster, what you made more reliable, and what complexity you accepted.
