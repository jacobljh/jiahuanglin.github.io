Design a distributed unique ID generator

IDs look trivial until they carry ordering, sharding, privacy, and operational recovery requirements. The design is a contract hidden inside a number.

0. Interview hinge

The hinge is the moment where the prompt stops being generic and the architecture becomes forced. For this case, that hinge is: Snowflake-style IDs, monotonicity, clock skew, region bits, collision budgets.

Invariant to protect
The design is only correct if the user-visible contract survives retries, partial failures, stale caches, and reordering. A concrete invariant to defend here: Duplicate worker IDs must be handled by Lease worker identity from a strongly consistent registry and fence expired leases..
Tempting but wrong
A common shortcut is to choose Central allocator vs local generator just because it buys simple global ordering. That shortcut fails when its cost matters: availability bottleneck. Use it only when the workload matches the condition: small systems with strict sequence needs.

1. Clarify the contract

The prompt is best treated as a product contract before it is treated as a component diagram. The system needs to do the following:

  • Generate IDs without a central bottleneck.
  • Avoid collisions across machines and regions.
  • Optionally preserve approximate time ordering.
  • Fit in common database integer or string fields.
  • Keep generation available during partial outages.

2. Put numbers on the shape

The exact numbers depend on the interviewer, but these are the estimates that should steer the architecture:

  • A 41-bit millisecond timestamp covers roughly 69 years.
  • 10 bits of worker identity gives 1024 workers before reallocation.
  • 12 sequence bits allow 4096 IDs per worker per millisecond.
  • If peak generation exceeds the per-millisecond sequence budget, the generator must wait or borrow more bits.

5. Linearized design

Walk the design in the order a request or event actually moves. This keeps the interview grounded and exposes the first bottleneck naturally.

  1. 1. Decide whether IDs need ordering, opacity, or only uniqueness.
  2. 2. If ordering matters, allocate bits to timestamp, region or worker, and sequence.
  3. 3. Assign worker IDs through a consensus-backed registry or static deployment metadata.
  4. 4. On each request, read the local monotonic clock and increment the sequence for that millisecond.
  5. 5. If the clock moves backward, stop, fence the worker, or switch to a reserved drift bucket.
  6. 6. Monitor sequence exhaustion, clock rollback, and duplicate detection downstream.

6. Deep dives

Why not UUID everywhere

UUIDs are easy and decentralized, but random UUIDs scatter B-tree inserts and are not naturally sortable. Time-ordered UUID variants reduce that cost but still need careful privacy thinking.

Clock is part of correctness

A time-ordered ID generator is a distributed system because clocks lie. The generator must define what happens when NTP steps backward or a VM resumes from pause.

Privacy leak

Decodable IDs expose creation time and sometimes region. That is useful for debugging but can leak business volume or user signup timing.

9. What to say in the interview

Strong signal
Lead with the dominant constraint: Snowflake-style IDs, monotonicity, clock skew, region bits, collision budgets. Then defend the design by naming what you made faster, what you made more reliable, and what complexity you accepted.
