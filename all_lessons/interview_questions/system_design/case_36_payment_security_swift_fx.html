Payment security, SWIFT, and foreign exchange

Payment infrastructure is constrained by adversaries, regulation, and external rails. The design must make unsafe states hard to express.

0. Interview hinge

The hinge is the moment where the prompt stops being generic and the architecture becomes forced. For this case, that hinge is: Threat model, rails, FX quote lifecycle, compliance and audit constraints.

Invariant to protect
The design is only correct if the user-visible contract survives retries, partial failures, stale caches, and reordering. A concrete invariant to defend here: Expired quote used must be handled by Check quote expiry atomically during payment confirmation..
Tempting but wrong
A common shortcut is to choose Store credentials vs tokenize just because it buys direct control. That shortcut fails when its cost matters: larger breach blast radius. Use it only when the workload matches the condition: rarely preferred.

1. Clarify the contract

The prompt is best treated as a product contract before it is treated as a component diagram. The system needs to do the following:

  • Secure sensitive payment data.
  • Support cross-border payment rails or provider integrations.
  • Handle FX quotes and expiry.
  • Keep audit logs for compliance.
  • Detect suspicious activity.

2. Put numbers on the shape

The exact numbers depend on the interviewer, but these are the estimates that should steer the architecture:

  • FX quotes have short validity windows, so stale quote use is a correctness bug.
  • Security logging volume is modest but retention requirements can be long.
  • Encryption and tokenization add latency but protect blast radius.
  • Manual review paths need queues and SLAs, not ad hoc spreadsheets.

5. Linearized design

Walk the design in the order a request or event actually moves. This keeps the interview grounded and exposes the first bottleneck naturally.

  1. 1. Tokenize or vault sensitive payment credentials.
  2. 2. Create FX quotes with amount, currency pair, rate, and expiration.
  3. 3. Screen parties and transactions before release to external rails.
  4. 4. Submit transfer instructions through a controlled provider or bank integration.
  5. 5. Record immutable audit events for every decision and external message.
  6. 6. Monitor anomalies and route risky transfers to review.

6. Deep dives

Quote lifecycle

An FX quote is not just a number; it is a contract with expiry and scope. Binding quote ID to payment intent prevents silent rate drift.

Security boundary

Application services should not casually handle raw credentials. Tokenization shrinks the set of systems in the highest-risk zone.

External rails

SWIFT-like messaging is asynchronous and institution-mediated. Design for pending states, acknowledgements, rejections, and reconciliation.

9. What to say in the interview

Strong signal
Lead with the dominant constraint: Threat model, rails, FX quote lifecycle, compliance and audit constraints. Then defend the design by naming what you made faster, what you made more reliable, and what complexity you accepted.
