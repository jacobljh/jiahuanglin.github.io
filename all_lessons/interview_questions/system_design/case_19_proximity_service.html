Design a proximity service

Nearby search is a geometry problem made distributed: turn latitude and longitude into a small set of storage keys without missing edge cases near cell boundaries.

Source: Vol. 2 Ch. 1, archive Case drill Trade-off first

0. Interview hinge

The hinge is the moment where the prompt stops being generic and the architecture becomes forced. For this case, that hinge is: Geohash, quadtrees, nearby search, cache by cell, privacy and freshness.

Invariant to protect
The design is only correct if the user-visible contract survives retries, partial failures, stale caches, and reordering. A concrete invariant to defend here: Missing results at cell boundary must be handled by Always query neighboring cells that intersect the radius..
Tempting but wrong
A common shortcut is to choose Geohash vs quadtree just because it buys simple keying. That shortcut fails when its cost matters: uneven density. Use it only when the workload matches the condition: MVP nearby search.

1. Clarify the contract

The prompt is best treated as a product contract before it is treated as a component diagram. The system needs to do the following:

  • Find businesses or places near a user location.
  • Filter by category, radius, open state, and ranking.
  • Return results quickly on mobile.
  • Support place updates, but not necessarily every second.
  • Protect sensitive user location data.

2. Put numbers on the shape

The exact numbers depend on the interviewer, but these are the estimates that should steer the architecture:

  • Place data is relatively static compared with user location streams.
  • Dense cities produce many candidates per cell; rural areas require larger radius expansion.
  • Caching by geospatial cell can have high hit rate for popular areas.
  • Ranking can be done after retrieving a bounded nearby candidate set.

5. Linearized design

Walk the design in the order a request or event actually moves. This keeps the interview grounded and exposes the first bottleneck naturally.

  1. 1. Normalize each place into one or more geospatial index cells.
  2. 2. For a query, convert the user location and radius into covering cells.
  3. 3. Fetch candidate place IDs from those cells, including neighboring cells at boundaries.
  4. 4. Hydrate place metadata and apply filters.
  5. 5. Rank by distance, relevance, quality, and business rules.
  6. 6. Cache popular cell-category queries while keeping user coordinates out of cache keys when possible.

6. Deep dives

Geohash vs quadtree

Geohash is simple and string-prefix friendly. Quadtrees adapt to density better. The right answer depends on whether density skew or implementation simplicity is the binding constraint.

Boundary problem

A user near a cell edge needs results from neighboring cells. Always explain the neighbor expansion or the design will miss nearby places.

Privacy

Do not log exact user coordinates unnecessarily. Coarsen, hash, or separate analytics from serving when possible.

9. What to say in the interview

Strong signal
Lead with the dominant constraint: Geohash, quadtrees, nearby search, cache by cell, privacy and freshness. Then defend the design by naming what you made faster, what you made more reliable, and what complexity you accepted.
