Design a web crawler

A crawler is a queueing and ethics problem disguised as a fetcher. The hard part is deciding what to fetch next without harming other sites or your own fleet.

0. Interview hinge

The hinge is the moment where the prompt stops being generic and the architecture becomes forced. For this case, that hinge is: Frontier scheduling, politeness, duplicate detection, robots rules, content storage.

Invariant to protect
The design is only correct if the user-visible contract survives retries, partial failures, stale caches, and reordering. A concrete invariant to defend here: Crawler trap must be handled by Detect infinite calendars and repeated URL patterns..
Tempting but wrong
A common shortcut is to choose Breadth-first vs priority crawl just because it buys coverage. That shortcut fails when its cost matters: less freshness for important pages. Use it only when the workload matches the condition: initial discovery.

1. Clarify the contract

The prompt is best treated as a product contract before it is treated as a component diagram. The system needs to do the following:

  • Discover pages from seed URLs and extracted links.
  • Respect robots.txt, per-host politeness, and crawl delay.
  • Avoid fetching duplicate or low-value content repeatedly.
  • Store fetched content and metadata for downstream indexing.
  • Scale fetchers while keeping host-level rate limits.

2. Put numbers on the shape

The exact numbers depend on the interviewer, but these are the estimates that should steer the architecture:

  • At 1 KB metadata and 100 KB content per page, 1B pages is roughly 100 TB content before compression.
  • A million hosts with per-host queues cannot all be hot in memory unless state is compact.
  • DNS, TLS, and fetch latency dominate CPU for simple crawlers.
  • Duplicate detection saves bandwidth, storage, and downstream indexing cost.

5. Linearized design

Walk the design in the order a request or event actually moves. This keeps the interview grounded and exposes the first bottleneck naturally.

  1. 1. Normalize URLs to collapse obvious duplicates before enqueueing.
  2. 2. Partition the frontier by host so politeness is enforceable.
  3. 3. Let schedulers lease eligible URLs to fetchers only when host delay has elapsed.
  4. 4. Fetch robots.txt and cache host policy with expiration.
  5. 5. Store content, status, headers, and extracted outgoing links.
  6. 6. Feed new links back through normalization, dedupe, and priority scoring.

6. Deep dives

Frontier design

A single global priority queue cannot enforce host-level politeness. The usual shape is per-host queues plus a global heap keyed by next eligible fetch time.

Duplicate detection

URL fingerprints catch exact URL repeats; content fingerprints catch mirrored or canonicalized duplicates. Near-duplicate detection is more expensive and belongs downstream.

Freshness policy

Important pages need recrawling; static pages do not. The crawler should learn recrawl intervals from change frequency, not hammer everything equally.

9. What to say in the interview

Strong signal
Lead with the dominant constraint: Frontier scheduling, politeness, duplicate detection, robots rules, content storage. Then defend the design by naming what you made faster, what you made more reliable, and what complexity you accepted.
