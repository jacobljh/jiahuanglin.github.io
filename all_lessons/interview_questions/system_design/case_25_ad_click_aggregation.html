Design ad click event aggregation

Ad click aggregation is a data correctness problem under money pressure. Every duplicate, missing event, and late event can become billing or trust damage.

Source: Vol. 2 Ch. 6 Case drill Trade-off first
How to read this case
This page is an original educational walkthrough based on the case topic, not a transcription. Read it linearly: contract, estimates, data, flows, bottlenecks, trade-offs, then failure modes.

0. Interview hinge

The hinge is the moment where the prompt stops being generic and the architecture becomes forced. For this case, that hinge is: Event pipelines, dedupe, late data, batch vs stream, exactly-once illusions.

Invariant to protect
The design is only correct if the user-visible contract survives retries, partial failures, stale caches, and reordering. A concrete invariant to defend here: Collector outage must be handled by Buffer locally where possible and fail over DNS or edge routing..
Tempting but wrong
A common shortcut is to choose Stream vs batch just because it buys freshness. That shortcut fails when its cost matters: late correction complexity. Use it only when the workload matches the condition: dashboards.

1. Clarify the contract

The prompt is best treated as a product contract before it is treated as a component diagram. The system needs to do the following:

  • Collect click events at high volume.
  • Deduplicate repeated clicks or retries.
  • Aggregate by campaign, ad, publisher, and time window.
  • Support near-real-time dashboards and accurate billing.
  • Handle late and out-of-order events.

2. Put numbers on the shape

The exact numbers depend on the interviewer, but these are the estimates that should steer the architecture:

  • Peak click volume can be bursty around campaigns or fraud attacks.
  • Raw event retention is needed for replay and audits.
  • Aggregation keys multiply quickly with dimensions.
  • Billing accuracy may require slower corrected results than dashboards.

5. Linearized design

Walk the design in the order a request or event actually moves. This keeps the interview grounded and exposes the first bottleneck naturally.

  1. 1. Ingest click events at edge collectors with minimal synchronous work.
  2. 2. Append raw events to a durable log.
  3. 3. Deduplicate by click ID or derived event fingerprint.
  4. 4. Stream aggregate into short windows for dashboards.
  5. 5. Run batch reconciliation over raw logs for billing-grade outputs.
  6. 6. Store corrections so downstream consumers understand revisions.

6. Deep dives

Dashboard vs billing

Realtime dashboards can be approximate and corrected. Billing should be reproducible from raw events with clear dedupe rules.

Late events

Event time and processing time differ. Watermarks let the system decide when a window is mostly complete while still accepting corrections.

Fraud and idempotency

A duplicate retry and a fraudulent repeated click are not the same. The pipeline should preserve enough raw evidence to make that distinction later.

9. What to say in the interview

Strong signal
Lead with the dominant constraint: Event pipelines, dedupe, late data, batch vs stream, exactly-once illusions. Then defend the design by naming what you made faster, what you made more reliable, and what complexity you accepted.
