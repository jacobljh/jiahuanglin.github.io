Search Ads & Recommender Systems — MLE interview prep

A linearized read on the production ML stack that powers ranking and ads. First-principles derivations, the trade-offs between every choice, and the specific things an interviewer will probe.

Why these two together

Recommender systems and search ads look like two products. Inside, they are the same machine with one extra layer:

recommender feed search-ads SERP ──────────────── ────────────── [retrieve ] ◄── billions of items ──► [retrieve ] [rank ] ◄── scoring funnel ────► [rank ] [re-rank ] ◄── business logic ───► [re-rank ] [auction ] ◄── ads only [pricing ] ◄── ads only

Everything left of the dashed line — candidate generation, embedding retrieval, CTR/CVR prediction, calibration, debiasing, A/B testing — is shared. The auction and the bidder are the two pieces ads adds on top. So an interviewer who works on either side expects you to understand both halves of the diagram, plus the ads-specific layer if you're targeting an ads team.

What an interviewer is actually testing

Four independent skills, and they're orthogonal — being strong at one tells the interviewer nothing about the others.

SkillWhat good looks likeWhat weak looks like
Decompose a system into the funnel Asked "design YouTube Home", you draw retrieve → rank → re-rank in 30 seconds, then go deep on the bottleneck the interviewer steers you toward. You know which metric each stage optimizes and why they can't be the same metric. You jump into model architectures before naming the funnel. You can't say why two-tower is at the retrieval stage and DLRM is at ranking.
Reason about objectives You translate a product goal ("more daily users") into a proxy label, then critique your own translation: position bias, survivor bias, selection bias, novelty effects. You distinguish predicting P(click | shown) from P(click | shown, position) from causal uplift. You optimize for raw CTR and don't notice you've created a clickbait feedback loop.
Quantitative trade-offs You can reason in numbers: a 10 ms p99 latency budget, a 1B-item index, a 10⁵ QPS workload, a $0.01 per-impression revenue, a 0.5% MDE on an A/B test. You know which choices buy and spend each. You discuss qualitative pros and cons without ever multiplying. You don't know how big "big" is for the systems you're discussing.
Calibration & counterfactual thinking You know why CTR predictions must be probabilities, not just rankings, the moment money enters the system. You understand IPS, propensity, doubly robust, and why "we trained on logged data" is not free. You treat softmax outputs as probabilities. You evaluate a new ranker by comparing its top-K on old logs.

Part I · The core ranking & ads stack

Each lesson builds on the previous. Lessons 01–08 cover the shared ranking machinery, 09–10 the ads-specific auction layer, and 11 is an end-to-end design walkthrough. Lessons 12–23 — added from a production RecSys engineering syllabus — go deeper on modeling, serving at scale, and the responsible-AI constraints a senior interview probes. Lessons 24–31 (Part V) close the remaining breadth against a comprehensive RecSys/ads question bank: the graph and generative frontiers, causal reasoning, and the feature/data/reliability layers beneath the model. The first eleven are still the core for any ranking-team interview; treat the rest as depth on the axis your target team works on.

01
The funnel — retrieve, rank, re-rank
Why production ranking is a multi-stage cascade and not one model. The latency / recall / precision budget at each stage. How recsys and ads systems share this structure.
02
Candidate generation — from CF to two-tower
Collaborative filtering, matrix factorization, two-tower neural retrievers. Why you train the towers separately and how the loss differs from a ranking loss. Cold start handled at the right layer.
03
Embeddings & approximate nearest neighbour
Dot product vs cosine vs L2. Maximum-Inner-Product Search, why it isn't metric. HNSW, IVF, PQ — the three knobs of an ANN index and what each costs. Recall@K vs latency vs RAM.
04
The ranking model — LR to DLRM to DCN-v2
Sparse high-cardinality features, embedding tables, feature crosses. Why GBDTs lost to deep learning in ranking and the specific architecture moves (DLRM, DCN, MaskNet) that won. What "feature engineering" still means.
05
Losses & calibration
Pointwise BCE, pairwise BPR, listwise LambdaRank. When ranking is enough and when calibrated probability matters. Platt, isotonic, temperature, expected calibration error.
06
Negative sampling
Why you can't train on the full softmax over a billion items. In-batch negatives, sampled softmax with the log-Q correction, hard-negative mining. The bias each one introduces and how to fix it.
07
Position bias, selection bias, debiasing
The training data is logs from the old ranker, not the world. Click models, the examination hypothesis, IPS, doubly robust, intervention harvesting. Why on-policy retraining stops working and what to do.
08
Evaluation — offline metrics & A/B tests
NDCG, MAP, AUC, log-loss, calibration error. What each measures and what each misses. A/B test design: power, MDE, variance reduction, network effects, novelty, long-term metrics.
09
The ad auction — GSP, VCG, quality score
Why first-price auctions broke, what GSP fixed, why VCG is truthful, and why Google still ran GSP for a decade. Quality score as expected-CTR × bid. Reserve prices, click value, externalities.
10
Bidding & pacing
Autobidding (tCPA, tROAS, max-conversions) viewed as a Lagrangian dual problem. Budget pacing as a control loop. Why the system's optimization horizon matters — and what breaks when the advertiser, the auction, and the pacer all optimize at once.
11
System design walkthrough
An end-to-end "design Instagram Reels ranking" / "design Google Sponsored Search" walk-through. Where each lesson's content shows up, with the order an interviewer expects you to reason through it.

Part II · Modeling depth

Past the core CTR model: the objectives, sequences, modalities, and learning paradigms that separate a competent ranker from a production one. These are where a senior interviewer goes once you've drawn the funnel.

12
Multi-task & multi-objective ranking
Why one score is never enough. Shared-bottom, MMoE, PLE, and ESMM's entire-space CVR trick; loss weighting, gradient conflict, and fusing click / watch-time / like / retention into one ordering.
13
User-behavior sequence modeling
Implicit vs explicit feedback and why a click is a biased label at birth. Target attention (DIN) and interest evolution (DIEN); short- vs long-term interest; sessionization.
14
Multimodal recommendation
When an ID embedding is undefined on day zero, the pixels and text aren't. Per-modality encoders, fusion strategies, and CLIP-style contrastive alignment as a cold-start lifeline.
15
Cold start
The chicken-and-egg of collaborative filtering. User / item / system cold start, content side-information, and exploration as a first-class need — multi-armed and contextual bandits, UCB, Thompson.
16
Reinforcement learning for recommendation
When optimizing the next click quietly destroys retention. The feed as an MDP, bandits as the one-step case, policy gradients, off-policy / IPS correction on logged data, and reward design.

Part III · Systems at scale

The same model, now under a latency SLA, a freshness requirement, and a billion-row embedding table. The systems half of the interview.

17
Real-time & streaming recommendation
Why freshness is a feature. The offline / nearline / online planes, the streaming substrate, exactly-once vs at-least-once, online learning and its dangers, and the latency budget.
18
Deployment & serving
The serving latency budget, export formats, the train–serve skew bug that silently kills models, shadow → canary → ramp → rollback, and the monitoring that predicts an incident.
19
Large-scale optimization
Billion-row embedding tables and how to shard them, parameter-server vs all-reduce, ANN at scale (HNSW/IVF/PQ), quantization & distillation, and the $/query bound that closes the design.

Part IV · Responsible & advanced

The constraints that increasingly bound what a recommender may do — and the topics a thoughtful interviewer uses to tell a builder from a tuner.

20
Fairness & diversity
Filter bubbles, popularity bias, and the feedback loop. Diversity as a re-rank objective (MMR, DPP), two-sided fairness in a marketplace, and the debiasing levers across the stack.
21
Explainable recommendation
Why explanations earn their latency: trust, debugging, compliance, auditing. Intrinsic vs post-hoc, attention-as-explanation and its caveats, path- and counterfactual explanations.
22
User privacy protection
Why anonymization fails, differential privacy done concretely (ε, sensitivity, the noise mechanisms), DP-SGD, on-device inference, and the regulatory frame — right to be forgotten included.
23
Federated learning for recommendation
Training on data that never leaves the device. FedAvg, the communication–computation tradeoff, non-IID convergence, the on-device item-table problem, and an honest verdict on when it pays.

Part V · Full-bank breadth

The lessons above are a focused ranking-and-ads track. These eight close the remaining gap between that track and a comprehensive RecSys/ads interview — the graph and generative frontiers, causal reasoning, the feature and data plumbing beneath the model, reliability, and the segmentation lens. (Pure ML/DL and Python-language fundamentals are deliberately still out of scope — they belong in a separate foundations track.)

24
Graph neural networks for recommendation
The interaction matrix is a bipartite graph and CF is its one-hop special case. Message passing, GraphSAGE / PinSage / LightGCN, high-order connectivity that rescues sparse nodes — and over-smoothing as the cap on depth.
25
Multi-agent reinforcement learning
The moment several learners share an environment it turns non-stationary and single-agent guarantees break. CTDE, MADDPG, QMIX; the ranking cascade and competing bidders as agents; an honest verdict on when MARL earns its complexity.
26
LLMs & generative AI in recommendation
LLM-as-judge and user simulators for evaluation, generative retrieval & semantic IDs, generative re-rankers, AIGC content — and the cost wall that keeps LLMs off the retrieval hot path plus the feedback-loop homogenization trap.
27
Causal inference & uplift modeling
Predicting P(click) is not the causal effect of intervening. Potential outcomes, S/T/X-learners and causal forests, the persuadables quadrant, and PSM / DID / IV / synthetic control for when you can't run an A/B test.
28
Feature engineering as a discipline
The craft the embedding table can't replace: one-hot / target / hierarchical encoding and the leakage it invites, numerical scaling and skew transforms, filter/wrapper/embedded selection, and train–serve parity.
29
Data engineering for recommendation
The plumbing under the model: batch (MapReduce / Spark) vs stream, Lambda vs Kappa, ETL and point-in-time-correct feature stores, data-quality monitoring, EDA, and class imbalance handled the recsys way.
30
High availability & disaster recovery
A down recommender serves nothing — so it must degrade, never fail. Nines and error budgets, load balancing, multi-region active-active failover with RTO/RPO, circuit breakers, and the graceful-degradation ladder.
31
User segmentation & profiling
Profiles and tag systems, RFM, K-means vs embedding clustering, fast/slow interest fusion, and segment-sliced evaluation — the lens that catches the Simpson's-paradox regression an aggregate metric hides.

How to use this

  1. Linearly. Each lesson uses vocabulary from the previous one. Lesson 5 (losses) is far more compelling once you've seen the model architectures in Lesson 4. Lesson 9 (auctions) presumes you understand calibrated CTR from Lesson 5.
  2. Touch every widget. Each lesson has at least one interactive component — a slider, a calculator, a toggle. They exist to let you feel a trade-off that prose alone makes abstract. The system-design lesson uses them to count latency budgets.
  3. Read the "interview prompts" boxes. Each lesson ends with 4–6 prompts that the role actually gets asked. The answers in the prose tell you what good looks like, not just the facts.
  4. Skip the auction half if you're only doing recsys. Lessons 09 and 10 are ads-specific. Lesson 11 has variants for both. The first eight are core for any ranking-team interview.

A note on intellectual honesty
Recsys and ads are domains where the published research lags production by years and the production tricks are guarded. Names like DLRM and DCN-v2 are open; the actual feature crosses, calibration tweaks, and pacing constants at every major company are not. This curriculum teaches the shape of the problem with enough precision that you can reason about a specific company's choices in an interview, not by reciting their proprietary recipe.
